James Frank
May 7, 2017

On Saturday, an Associated Press article appeared with an inflammatory headline and factually incorrect statements. This was picked up by some news sources and has become fodder for a number of folks to comment on. It is clear that many of these people have not read the bill.

CPS reform has been a top priority of the Texas Legislature during this session. One of our biggest challenges is a lack of adequate, quality foster homes. This foster care capacity issue has caused unnecessary turmoil for many of these children as they have to be moved hundreds of miles away from their homes, schools, and friends. Some have been forced to sleep in CPS offices.

A substantial part of any answer to this problem will be found in the faith-based community. Faith-based child welfare service providers have been taking care of Texas children for more than a century. They began this mission even before there was a Child Protective Services.

Today they comprise approximately a quarter of all providers. Unfortunately, the ability of many of the faith-based institutions to continue offering services is threatened by the prospect of litigation for declining to provide certain services (such as abortion) because of sincerely held religious beliefs.

At a time when we need all hands-on deck, we face the real risk of seeing a large number of these providers leave the field, as they are forced to make the choice between devoting a substantial amount of resources in fighting litigation and other adverse action, or using those resources on other services to fulfill the tenets of their faith.

HB 3859 seeks to protect faith-based providers from adverse actions for exercising their deeply held religious beliefs. At the same time, it requires the Department of Family and Protective Services to ensure alternative providers are present to offer any service denied for reasons of sincerely held religious beliefs. Not one foster parent/family who wants to provide a home for our kids will be denied from doing so. Not one. Additionally, the same standard used for placing children now (best interest of the child) clearly remains in place. If HB 3859 passes, children will have a greater network of potential homes for placement so they do not have to move halfway across the state or spend nights in CPS offices.

We need a number of fixes to CPS to address capacity issues. Along with other legislation put forth this session, HB 3859 will mean more homes for foster children.

There are many in the news media who do a terrific job attempting to remain objective, learning their subject matter, and getting a variety of viewpoints and sources before publishing a story. I have interacted with a few of them during my work on CPS reform over the last 15 months.

Unfortunately, stories like the one written by Meredith Hoffman feed a perception that the news media is biased and more interested in pushing a narrative than doing the work of real journalism. When stories with inflammatory and objectively false headlines and content are published by a supposedly reputable source like the Associated Press, it is easy to understand why the fake news moniker gains traction and why Americans no longer trust the news media. Add one more strike against the AP and notions that its reporters and editors can be trusted to disinterestedly report the news in a factually correct manner.

Link to the bill: http://www.capitol.state.tx.us//85R/bill/pdf/HB03859H.pdf
