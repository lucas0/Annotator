Scorpion Antidote Is Ineffective
 
  Rebecca Voelker
 

Researchers in Tunisia have some bad news for the nearly 40,000 people stung by scorpions in their country each year: scorpion antivenin is no more effective in relieving or preventing symptoms than placebo.

In a study of 825 patients who came to Tozeur Hospital's emergency department with scorpion stings, half received a placebo and half received intravenous antivenin. Some patients also received acetaminophen or hydrocortisone for symptom relief. In more than three fourths of the patients, pain at the site of the sting was the only symptom. However, symptoms, including cardiogenic shock, were life-threatening in 11 patients.

After 4 hours of observation, symptoms resolved in 55% of those in the antivenin group and in 66% of patients who received a placebo. Overall, 94% in the antivenin group and 96% in the placebo group had no worsening of their symptoms. But because of the small number of very ill patients in the study, the researchers cautioned against assumptions that antivenin offers no benefit to patients with severe symptoms.

Even so, they concluded that scorpion antivenin should not be given routinely. "Scorpion envenomation is a public health problem and routine treatment with scorpion antivenom has a high financial burden," they noted in the September 11 Lancet.
