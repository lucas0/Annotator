"E Pluribus Unum" becomes controversial

Religious Right seeks to banish inclusive motto

Posted Mar 13, 2011

  • SHARE
  • TWEET

  • MORE

Source:
E Pluribus Unum has never been considered controversial. The motto, which is Latin for "out of many, one," was adopted by the Founding Fathers in 1782 as part of the Great Seal of the United States, intendedto represent the federal nature of the nation - out of many states, one country. The framers sharply disputed many issues in the formation of the nation, but E Pluribus Unum was not one of them.

In fact, as the nation's population has grownthe motto has taken on a secondary meaning as well, reflecting the country's melting pot nature - out of many peoples, one American people. Certainly, if there's one issue we can all agree on, it's that E Pluribus Unum is an excellent statement of America and its values, right?

Not so fast. The Religious Right, led by Rep. Michele Bachmann (R-Minnesota)and the Congressional Prayer Caucus (yes, our government actually has a Congressional Prayer Caucus), wants you to know that E Pluribus Unum is, well, almost un-American.

The CPC, a leading voice of religious conservatism on Capitol Hill, recently wrote a letter to President Barack Obama, chastising him for referring to the motto E Pluribus Unum. The letter may very well reflect a new milestone in the culture wars, for never before has E Pluribus Unum been portrayed as controversial. But according tothe CPC, Obama's reference to E Pluribus Unum amounts toan anti-God statement.See theletter here.

As the letter shows, to the Prayer Caucus, Obama's reference to E Pluribus Unum borders on unpatriotic. They say he should instead be promoting the motto "In God We Trust," which was adopted as the national motto at the urging of religious conservatives in 1956, during the McCarthy era and at the height of the Cold War.

With all the credibility of public figures who believe the world is only six thousand years old, the caucus wants Obama to "issue a correction." Apparently, referring to the E Pluribus Unum motto is now sacrilege in the Christian right's America. They also say Obama isn't mentioning God enough in his speeches and that he shouldn't refer to "inalienable rights" without mentioning that they come from God.

Source:
Should we be concerned that religious conservatives, many of them biblical literalists, wield so much power in modern America, a country founded on Enlightenment principles by men who embraced science and reason? Obviously, we are a country that appreciates religious freedom, and individuals from any religious background are welcome to run for office and serve. But what does it say when large numbers of religious fundamentalists seem to easily glide to victory, while only one of the 535 members of Congress identifies openly as non-theistic (that would be Rep. Pete Stark of California)?

It's interesting thatBachmann and her prayer caucus colleagues often claim to stand for the "values" that America needs, often wrapping themselves in the flag and proclaiming "God Bless America!" Thus the irony that they are the ones who complain about the framers' motto, E Pluribus Unum.

For those who are concerned that an entity called the "Congressional Prayer Caucus" even exists(and is supported in any way by our tax dollars), it seems that promotion of E Pluribus Unum is a means of pushing back against the politicized theology of the Religious Right. By objecting toE Pluribus Unum, the CPC has made it a new point of contention in the culture wars.

This country was Not founded on Christian Ideals

Submitted by R.J.Allison on September 30, 2012 - 4:56am

I know this is a long time after the origin discussion but I just can across it. In addition to the overall topic I noticed a few things I want to comment on: the quote in the article "he shouldn't refer to 'inalienable rights' without mentioning that they come from God"; the discussion thread started by Denton that "they are endowed by their Creator with ..." implies God; and Madeline's "I do believe this country was founded on Christian principles." First, Madeline, let me say I agree with your position that "it is never okay to *force* those principles on someone else" and like what you said; but as I hope you will see following, your belief about the foundation of our principles is misguided.
---
People do not seem to remember, or to know, that God was not part of our government until the 1950s. She was put there in the post McCarthy era of anti-communism; stemming from the believe that since communism disallows God and religion - we, not being communist, must praise God. That was thanks to 'the greatest generation' and largely led by Eisenhower and him being influenced by christian congressional members. (CPC? Maybe, but I forget.)

The phrase "under God" was not in the Pledge of Allegiance when it was written; nor when I first learned it as a child. The words "under God" were not put into the Pledge until 1957; and for me personally, they are still not in it. When I recite the pledge, and I did daily in a classroom as a teacher, I omit and remain silent while those words are said by others. In addition the phrase "In God we trust" was not on our money until then either. The motto of our country is what was on our money - "E pluribus unum". Most of the money throughout the 60's and into the 70's retained the original phrase; its withdrawal and purge from circulation not being completed until relatively recently.

As far as history knows, God had nothing to do with the original settlement of our country. It was however a significant reason for the Western Europeans coming to settle here. But they did not come here not for a government including God; they came for religious freedom, and to get away from a government that included God.

Our government was founded on the ideal that God Did Not belong it it; and any one of our founders would be appalled at the idea of anyone suggesting that she did. If you read any of their public or personal writings you can see that although they were nearly all deeply religious, they did not want God in the government. Not only were our founders deeply religious, they were well educated. Even the farmers like Quincy who had not been to school were well read and well spoken. They struggled over every sentence and word in the Declaration of Independence and Constitution. They being very careful to say exactly what they meant. That struggle and care with the wording of ideals is one of the reasons that the documents remain so relevant even today.

The word 'religion' is not in our Declaration of Independence at all; and God is mentioned only once. in the first paragraph when God is mentioned, God is placed secondary to nature - in the phrase " the separate and equal Station to which the Laws of Nature and of Natures God entitle them, ". They could have easily said 'the Laws of God'; but they did not. Why? They could have easily indicated that nature was from God and said 'of God's nature'; but they did not. They said "Nature's God" or that God was from nature. It was the 'Laws of Nature' and of 'Nature's God' that entitled us to our station as a country. Nature was the reason.

It is in the second paragraph that we have " that they are endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable Rights, ". They could have easily said 'endowed by God'; but they did not. Why? Given that the writers of the Declaration were, in the first paragraph, so careful to position Nature and not God as the ultimate authority for our station as a country; why would we think that they now, in the very next sentence, were so clumsy as to position God as the reason for our rights? They wanted to keep God out of it. It was our creator, whatever or whoever that may be, that was responsible. It was Nature, it was the Big Bang, it was Mother Earth, it was a prehistoric alien visitor, yes maybe God, or maybe just our mothers' and fathers' that was our creator. They didn't even know about some of those possibilities I just mentioned (they may have known that a few had different ideas); but the point is they did not, for the purposes of the Declaration, care. They just did not want God in our government. So about - "he shouldn't refer to "inalienable rights" without mentioning that they come from God" - they didn't come from God, they came from our creator. While the CPC may believe we were created by God, not everyone does; and our Declaration of Independence does not say we were, or that God is where our rights come from.

The work God was in the Declaration once, but God is not mentioned in our Constitution at all; and religion is mentioned only once. It is in the sixth article on oaths saying; "no religious test shall ever be required".
So God is not part of the government; and according to the writers of the Constitution, Never was to be.
