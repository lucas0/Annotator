A Mike Wendland e-Journal Special Report...

 Caught in a Web of Terror: 
There are others being terrorized these days... just ask Muzaffar Wandawi  
 

In an October 20, 2001, Detroit Free Press column, technology writer Mike Wendland chased down a story about a series of Internet sites reputed to contain secret messages for terrorists, exposing the report as being built on less than solid evidence. It's the latest in a series of hoaxes, false reports and wildly speculative rumors that have created a climate of fear and uneasiness. For space reasons, the column that appeared in the print editions was trimmed. Here's the longer, more complete version, with links, images and further resource material. 
Click on any image for original source or more information.

 By MIKE WENDLAND

  Technology Columnist
    Detroit Free Press

The Internet and those who see conspiracies afoot everywhere have been in hyperdrive during these angst-filled days following the September 11 terror attacks.

It is a dark side of the Internet that can do real harm.

In the Netherlands, Muzaffar Wandawi , a 34-year-old Web developer found that out the hard way this week. Online samples of his artwork are being cited by a controversial Web conspiracy buff in Pennsylvania as “secret messages” to a terror network to launch biological attacks against the U.S.

Further, the allegations, now being passed along and circulated through e-mail and other conspiracy sites, imply that Wandawi is an Iraqi spy, linked to one of the 19 terrorists who hijacked airplanes in the Sept. 11 attacks in the U.S.

The drawings were done years ago in his native Baghdad during the Iraq/Iran War and are displayed on a several Web sites as portfolios of his artistic abilities. 

“How could someone come up with such an idea?" asked the stunned Wandawi when I reached him on the phone. "Those are the most untrue statements that could be made. This is going to ruin me. This must be set straight." 

The charges were (www.bringmenews.com) put online Tuesday by a self-described "experimental nuclear physicist" and sometimes UFO and extraterrestrial researcher named Robert Koontz who runs a Web site devoted to national security issues and alarmist discussions about terrorism. 

Koontz, a PhD in experimental nuclear physics who says he once worked for the U.S. National Security Agency, claims he has linked Wandawi to one of the terrorist hijackers involved in the Sept. 11 attacks. 

It is a charge a flabbergasted Wandawi vehemently denies.


He claims Wandawi  is linked to Ahmed Alghamdi,  one of the terrorists aboard United Flight 175 which crashed into the South Tower of the World Trade Center on Sept. 11.
 

 
 

 
 

 

How so? He found both of their names on a fan site based in Bahrain for a popular Lebanese singer named Fairuz (www.fairuz.net). 

That’s it. That’s the link.


 

The name of Ahmed Alghamdi, who listed his age as 32 and being from Saudi Arabia, was still on the site Friday. In a message area devoted to short comments from fans he wrote... in misspelled praise: “When Fairoz  sings, another world is created.” 

But Wandawi, who says he is a fan of Fairuz’s music, also posted a message of appreciation, as did some 30 others. 
 

 
 

 
 

 

Because Wandawi's posting was accompanied by links to his portfolio Web sites (http://people.a2000.nl/mwandawi),  Koontz went to check them out and came across a series of seven themed drawings, most involving a horse and scenes of devastation. 

The most graphic shows a horse, ridden by a man, with what appear to be images of a jumbled city landscape. Koontz copied the images and enlarged them on his computer and claims shapes similar to the burning World Trade Center and Pentagon are visible in the background. 

They looked pretty grainy and indistinct to me.

A key feature in the series of horse drawings is a yellow plant, which Koontz believes represents a biological or chemical agent. The images show the plants spreading amidst progressive scenes of devastation which Koontz is convinced signals a coded message to launch a massive biological attack.

Koontz, who lives in Huntington, PA, says he called the FBI last week when he completed his analysis. They weren’t interested.

“I called four separate offices two times each and when they put me off I decided to go public with this and warn the world.”

He posted the special page on bringmenews.com site, with the title: “Coded Images Show Plans for Massive Germ Attack On U.S., Killing Millions.” 

Wandawi was shocked and frightened when I reached him by phone in Amsterdam to tell him of the site. “I do not understand how these pictures could be so twisted,” he said. “Perhaps he did this because I am from Iraq? I did the first of these drawings in 1987 when I was 20 years old.”

That was during the Iraq/Iran war, he said. The horseman figure, he says, came from a book borrowed from the library of the British embassy in Baghdad. Koontz claims the horse is an Arabian horse. Wandawi told me it was from a book showing an American cowboy. The city scape in the background is Baghdad and the devastation, he says, was to represent the destruction from the war, which cost the life of his brother.

The yellow plant that Koontz sees as a symbol for chemical warfare, Wandawi says, is really a symbol of “violation,” the ultimate effect of war and the theme of all the drawings.

“All this was done long ago,” he says. “The images are gloomy because that is what it was like in Iraq.” 



Wandawi said he fled Iraq in 1992 and 
moved to Turkey and Jordan before 
settling in the Netherlands, where he 
is a Web designer.

He is equally baffled at Koontz’s linking him to one of the hijackers. “I do not know that man (Alghamdi),” he said. “And how does this man (Koontz) know that the name on Fairuz’s page is the same as the hijacker? Maybe they are different people with the same name. This is crazy that he says I know this so-called hijacker.”

After learning of the allegations, Wandawi contacted Koontz to protest and ask him to remove the page.

But Koontz is non-plussed. In fact, he’s mad at me for looking into his allegations.

“You -- a reporter --  told Wandawi, the Iraqi intelligence agent and artist, that I had posted his pictures on the web and had decoded them, right?,” he wrote me in reaction to Wandawi’s denial.  “Well, these are the most secret things that Saddam has, for which he could get nuked.  And now Wandawi knows that his incompetence has led to the plan being compromised.”

His site is still up, claiming “Muzaffar Wandawi was involved in the attacks on the World Trade centers, and it appears he uses his drawings and paintings to encode orders to terrorists, including al Qaeda.”

Coded Images Show Plans for Massive Germ Attack On U.S., Killing Millions
By Robert W. Koontz, Ph D.

Wandawi wants to set the record straight. “I am not a spy. I don’t know this hijacker. My drawings have nothing to do with this. There is no basis for him to say such things.” 

Since Koontz put his allegations online this week, Koontz says he finally met with the FBI and told them his theory about the coded messages behind Wandawi’s message.

A spokesman for the FBI’s terror task force in Washington had no comment on what, if anything, they were doing with Koontz’s allegations.

But Wandawi is worried. “I am afraid that American authorities would think this is true,” he said. “I don’t understand how someone can make such statements. It’s just crazy. Now, I must look out for people who may be thinking I am something bad. This is not good.”

No. It’s not. It’s not good at all.

It is another example of why the Internet’s unrestrained ability to let anyone gain a worldwide audience to say anything about anyone and anything has a dark side as disturbing as what Koontz thought he saw in Wandawi’s drawings.
