Executive Summary

The Joint Chiefs of Staff have provided military planners with an operationally based template for the evolution of the Armed Forces over the next several years (Joint Vision 2010). Based on this emerging framework, future operational concepts will require improved command, control, and intelligence. The ability to collect, process, and disseminate an uninterrupted flow of information, while exploiting or denying the enemy’s ability to do the same, is paramount to the success of these new strategies. Assured access to the radio frequency (RF) spectrum will thus be at least as critical to the military in the future as it is today.

Although many individuals and organizations use the spectrum, the military faces unique challenges in its spectrum operations. Military communications-electronics systems perform a wide variety of functions, many of which must be performed simultaneously on comparatively small platforms or by units in close proximity to one another. Military aircraft, for example, must not only perform voice communications, radionavigation, and radar surveillance operations, they also have uniquely military requirements such as sustaining multiple radar tracks of hostile forces, launching and controlling weapons, conducting data communications and/or relay with cooperating units, jamming hostile sensors, and performing counter-countermeasures when necessary. Successfully performing all of these spectrum dependent functions in the face of active attempts by hostile forces to jam and disrupt their operations, demands various control measures that in turn require sufficient spectrum to be effective. Limiting the tuning capability of military systems by decreasing their available spectrum inherently increases their vulnerability to hostile attack and self-induced interference.

Technologically superior equipment has been critical to our past combat successes. We must maintain this edge even as we face the wider range of threats our technologically advanced adversaries will employ. Many recent developments in military technology, including anti-stealth and portable mine detection radars, integrated aircraft avionics, and secure, high capacity communications, require greater spectrum access rather than less. In the case of the highly mobile, and often life threatening operations of the military, there simply are no alternatives to using the spectrum.

In light of its increasing information requirements, DoD is continuing to participate in, and often leads, efforts to develop spectrum efficient methods and technologies. Long-term goals include the development of technologies such as adaptive, software-driven equipment and advanced spectrum management techniques. Mid-term initiatives include a multi-layer national technology thrust in aerospace communications efficiency, with immediate emphasis on developing and implementing new, efficient modulation techniques. However, technology advances and their implementation in military systems, doctrine, and training cannot occur overnight. It is imperative that we devise and adhere to an orderly transition from legacy technologies and methods to those of tomorrow. An orderly transition is critical if we are to sustain the military readiness and superiority required for the broad range of deterrent, conflict prevention, and peacetime activities that we will face in the foreseeable future. Preserving access to the spectrum resources required by today’s military is a prerequisite for the transitions to follow.

As illustrated in the table below, military access to the spectrum is being increasingly constrained in the face of increasing requirements. Many of the military technology advantages employed today and planned for tomorrow rely heavily on access to specific portions of the electromagnetic spectrum. Unfortunately, the same spectrum, that below 5 to 6 GHz, is of great value to both military and commercial applications. In fact, over ninety percent of DoD’s permanent frequency assignments are registered in bands below 3 GHz.

One of the DoD’s major concerns is the increasingly rapid rate at which reallocations are mandated. The report to Congress for Title VI of the Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act (OBRA-93) was submitted after 18 months of analysis and negotiation. The Balanced Budget Act of 1997 (BBA-97) was signed into law on 8 August 1997 with NTIA’s report on Title III due back to Congress in six months (delivered 8 February 1998). NTIA made its first spectrum band selection for BBA-97 during the second week of September 1997 and requested impacts and cost analysis by 30 November 1997. Selected bands were changed several times over the following months, and the final decision on band selection was not made until early January 1998. The DoD, as primary user of these bands, will experience major cost impacts and impairments to critical missions as a result of the reallocations. Adequate time is necessary to conduct the appropriate analysis and research required if we are to minimize the impacts of spectrum reallocations.

This report identifies the following for the spectrum identified by NTIA for reallocation as a result of OBRA-93.

· Current missions and functions performed in each band

· Primary systems in each band and their estimated operational lifetimes

· Estimates of the costs associated with the reallocations

· Details regarding the cost drivers associated with each of the estimates

· The nature and severity of anticipated operational impacts

· The primary sites affected by the reallocation and an assessment of the impact for those sites

A previous report identifies similar information regarding the spectrum identified for reallocation by BBA-97. (1)

The DoD’s greatest concern is the operational impacts that will result from spectrum reallocations. The impacts discussed in this report are in the context of a National defense posture that will remain unchanged for the foreseeable future. This posture requires that our forces receive adequate training and be prepared for short notice deployment to regional hotspots throughout the world. There is also a continuing requirement for non-combat operations worldwide as the military continues to participate in a broad range of deterrent, conflict prevention, and peacekeeping activities. No medium other than the RF spectrum can support the DoD’s attendant mobility and flexibility requirements. Critical missions and operations affected by reallocations include surveillance and control of North American airspace, air launched missile control and precision strike test and training operations, and aeronautical telemetry operations that support flight testing of aircraft, spacecraft, and missiles at major military test ranges and facilities.

The NTIA intended to provide the means to continue some of DoD’s critical operations by reallocating some of the spectrum on a ‘Mixed Use’ basis and by providing selected area protection zones where military use of the spectrum will be permitted to continue for a specified time. The DoD is concerned that these means will neither provide for the military’s needs nor prevent EMI between Federal and non-Federal operations. For example, the DoD’s airborne tactical communications are not protected by the zones identified for the 139-140.5 and 141.5-143 MHz bands reallocated by BBA-97. In fact, in some cases, a single protection zone is proposed for multiple sites that are geographically separated by a distance greater than the diameter of the protection zone.

Of additional concern to the DoD is the cost of implementing OBRA-93 and BBA-97 reallocations. Although analysis is ongoing, estimates to date clearly show that the costs will be substantial. Both low and high cost estimates are presented herein, along with identification of the uncertainties and expected conditions that will strongly influence the ultimate cost. The low and high estimates of OBRA-93 reallocation costs are $247.2M and $1,240.2M, respectively. The OBRA-93 reallocation costs reported herein are based on information provided to NTIA as part of the OBRA-93 band selection process. Estimates of the BBA-97 reallocation costs range from a low of $436M to a high of $2,518M, as reported in Reference 1. The total estimated cost of both reallocations is between $520.3M and $2,701.5M. These totals, identified below, take into account the combined costs of both reallocations and include the effect of reallocating bands for BBA-97 that are adjacent to those reallocated by OBRA-93.

An estimated low cost / high cost approach was employed to account for the complexities and technical, policy, and doctrinal uncertainties associated with constraining the operation of systems supporting critical functions such as continental air defense, tactical radio relay, missile telecommand and guidance, and aeronautical flight-testing. The low cost / high cost estimate accommodates the uncertainties involved for cases where reducing the frequency availability would have an unknown impact on operational readiness. That is, if the missions can still be accomplished with the reduced-spectrum capability, the low cost estimate will be realized. If, on the other hand, the mission would be compromised by the reduced-spectrum capability, the estimated high cost would be required to develop other means to accomplish the mission. The same basic rationale applies to all bands and equipment included in this report.

More specific details describing individual system complexities and uncertainties regarding operational impacts are provided in the discussion that follows; however, low cost estimates generally consist of modifications to existing equipment to facilitate their operation in the remaining spectrum. High cost estimates generally consist of major equipment modifications or development of new, replacement systems that operate in an alternative frequency band. It should be noted, however, that numerous factors such as the physics of propagation, the density of current spectrum users, and concern regarding possible future reallocations make it extremely difficult for the DoD to gain access to alternative spectrum.

The OMB has requested that the DoD provide a description of the conditions, actions, regulations, or policies that would need to occur or be in place in order for DoD to realize the lowest cost impacts. (2) Obviously, both cost and operational impacts would be reduced if some or all of the planned spectrum reallocations were rescinded. Likewise, full reimbursement of the costs incurred as a result of OBRA-93 and BBA-97 would negate reallocation costs to the DoD, provided adequate alternative spectrum was available.

Reference 2 also requested that the DoD identify any additional legislative or regulatory actions that could reduce cost or operational impacts to DoD. The DoD believes the following are actions that should be taken in this regard:

· Spectrum used by National Security systems should be exempted from reallocation.

· A footnote should be included in the National Table of Allocations authorizing use of the 1385-1400 MHz band by CINCNORAD radar assets during a national defense situation, as determined by CINCNORAD.

· Sufficient time and funds should be provided to conduct a thorough analysis of reallocation alternatives prior to drafting future spectrum reallocation legislation.

Solutions to the challenges arising from the impacts of spectrum reallocations are being aggressively pursued, and the DoD is expending significant resources to project its spectrum needs for the future. These are formidable tasks, however, since they include not only a determination of future operational scenarios and corresponding information requirements but also an informed estimate of the types and extent of future technology development. Completing such complex tasks will require time, as will migrating to any future spectrum management techniques and advanced technologies. The Defense Department requires adequate spectrum access to support National Defense missions in the interim. Measures that would help to mitigate the impacts of spectrum reallocations and assure adequate spectrum availability include:

· Instituting a moratorium on reallocation of spectrum used by DoD

· Rescinding the reallocation of spectrum that is not highly valued by the civil sector

· Mandating the identification of and guaranteed access to alternative spectrum for displaced DoD users

· Reimbursing the full costs incurred to migrate from one frequency band to another

· Implementing and enforcing equipment (transmitter and receiver) standards for all spectrum users.

While it is certain that advancements in technology and spectrum management techniques will enable more to be done with a given amount of spectrum, the demand for increased information capacity and the spectrum to support it has historically outpaced technological developments. Thus, the only long-term way to make adequate spectrum available for Government and non-Government needs is to develop technologies and practices that address the issue of sharing spectrum. Compatible spectrum sharing will not just happen, however. Apportioning the limited spectrum resource in the best interests of the American people requires the formation of a National Spectrum Plan to establish and protect our national priorities.

1390-1400 MHz

Overview of 1390-1400 MHz

The 1390-1400 MHz band is used by the DoD for long-range air surveillance and air defense radars, drone control systems, tactical radio relay, and various data links. Simultaneous use of closely spaced radar, drone control systems, and data links is a requirement for test and training range operations.

The low cost estimate for reallocation of the 1390-1400 MHz band is $140M. This estimate is based on the assumptions that the Air Defense mission remains and that civil systems using the reallocated band are compatible with the military systems which will continue to operate in adjacent spectrum. That is, it is assumed that the addition of filters, crystals, and other appropriate hardware and software modifications will permit surveillance radar systems to operate at their full design capability. NTIA noted:(3)

"… adopting adequate regulatory and industry receiver standards for new non-Federal equipment in this band is essential to assure satisfactory performance of new non-Federal services in bands adjacent to Federal high-power radars."

The low cost estimate includes the costs of modifications to the radar systems and the minimum estimated amounts for replacing the Multi-Service Target Control System (MSTCS) and the Range Application Joint Program Office (RAJPO) data links. Although there would be some adverse impact, it was assumed that extensive modifications would not be required for Army tactical systems to operate on new frequencies. The primary cost drivers for the low estimate are the software modifications and the acquisition and installation of filters and crystals in surveillance radar systems, and the low cost estimate for replacement of the MSTCS and RAJPO data links.

The high cost estimate for reallocation of the 1390-1400 MHz band is $1,033M. This estimate is based on the assumption that the remaining available frequencies will not provide sufficient spectrum resources to support today’s air defense radar systems and that major hardware modifications will be required. The high cost estimate also includes the high estimates for replacing the MSTCS and RAJPO data links. Although there would be some adverse impact, it was assumed that extensive modifications would not be required for Army tactical systems to operate on new frequencies. The primary cost drivers for the high estimate are the major modifications to surveillance radar systems and replacement of the MSTCS and RAJPO data links.

Reallocation of this band significantly degrades the ability of the DoD’s primary surveillance radar systems to detect intrusion into our nation’s airspace in peacetime and operations other than war (OOW). The quality of performance for these missions depends, in some cases, on radar emissions at two separate frequencies. DoD is concerned that reduced spectrum assets will also degrade the radar’s operational anti-jam capabilities.

Major Functions and Missions

The 1350-1400 MHz band is used in conjunction with the 1215-1240 MHz, 1240-1300 MHz, and 1300-1350 MHz bands for long-range radars, aeronautical radio location, drone control, and tactical radio relay.

The DoD and Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) operate the Joint Surveillance System (JSS) radar network on frequencies throughout the 1215-1400 MHz band. The JSS provides coverage of the US perimeter for radionavigation, air and fleet defense, and drug interdiction. The lower 25 MHz of this band (1215-1240 MHz) is used by GPS and GLONASS navigation receivers that operate using very low level signals.

Paired band data links in the 1350-1400 MHz band are used in conjunction with those in the 1427-1435 MHz band to rebroadcast aircraft position during tests and training. The RAJPO data link system is deployed at all major military aircraft and missile test centers and is used in flight testing of aircraft for each of the Services.

Additional uses of the 1350-1400 MHz band include the Navy’s MK 23 Target Acquisition System (TAS) and AN/TPS-59 tactical radar and the Army’s tactical transportable radio relay systems.

Reallocation will, at a minimum, require system software modifications, circulator replacement, and the installation of new filters and crystals at an estimated cost of $35M. If the remaining available frequencies cannot support the dual-channel frequency-hopping requirement, major additional hardware modifications will be required at an estimated cost of $525M and will require 5 years to complete.

AN/FPS-117. The AN/FPS-117 has a surveillance (air defense warning) mission, primarily in Alaska. The radar is designed to operate on twenty frequencies (channels) up to 1400 MHz, in both peacetime and crisis situations. Loss of channels results in degraded radar performance, potentially creating gaps in radar coverage of North America and jeopardizing the NORAD Integrated Tactical Attack and Warning Assessment system’s mission. Currently channels 1,16, and 20 are not used at all AN/FPS-117 sites, in order to prevent interference with other systems. Some sites have additional channels unavailable due to site-specific interference concerns. The following actions are necessary, as a result of OBRA-93, to alleviate concerns over the implications of secondary use of the spectrum, even at remote sites:

· Transmission requirements. Software modifications for a frequency synthesizer will be required to preclude the use of the reallocated channels and to retain the radar’s operational capabilities within the remaining band.

· Reception requirements. A hardware modification will be required to replace the frequency oscillators with a frequency synthesizer and to prevent use of the 1390-1400 band segment.

· Mission requirements. Target detection will not be affected by civil sources transmitting in 1390-1400 MHz; however, commercial sources may be reported as false detections. If this occurs, a hardware modification will be required to eliminate the problem.

· Calibration and Maintenance Requirements. The loss of 1390-1400 MHz will require modification of the software used for calibration, monitoring, and fault isolation.

If replacement of major system components becomes necessary because the remainder of the band cannot accommodate mission requirements, costs are estimated at $350M. Past availability of the existing high-power, solid-state devices has lent itself to cost effective component savings and extended the system’s useful life. This is in direct correlation with preserving the mission requirement of Operational Availability with a minimal attended maintenance savings concept.

AN/FPS-124. The AN/FPS-124 is a multi-channel frequency agile radar with a surveillance (air defense warning) mission that supports the Alaskan air defense network. The loss of the 1390-1400 MHz band reduces the probability of target detection and may result in the requirement to redesign and replace the system. Loss of the 10 MHz will also compound the difficulties of interference resolution with similar systems in Canada and Iceland. Radar operations in Alaska will continue on a secondary basis, meaning they would be shut down in the event of commercial interference. As a result, the North Warning System would lose its ability to provide targeting data and accurate surveillance information. Loss of these radars will allow coverage gaps for threats capable of delivering payloads to the North American continent to go undetected.

If interference occurs, a complete replacement of the system may be necessary since the remainder of the band cannot support the mission requirement. If system replacement becomes necessary, it will cost an estimated $130M and take 5 years to complete.

AN/PPQ-2(V)1 Tactical Radar. The AN/PPQ-2 has a surveillance (air defense warning) mission. There will be a loss in the tuning range of the radar. A redesign of the radar will be required to limit operations to below 1390 MHz. Development of the redesigned radar constitutes the low and high cost estimates of $5M.

Multi-Service Target Control System. MSTCS supports range operations (military training) missions. The data link for the MSTCS requires two frequencies within the 1350-1400 MHz band. This is a multi-user band, and frequencies used for transmission are chosen to reduce interference between the two MSTCS frequencies and between those frequencies used by the RAJPO Data Link System (DLS) and ARSR-4 radar systems. With the proposed reallocation, there may not be any clear frequencies available for the MSTCS at some test ranges without shutting down other systems. Therefore, a new frequency band for the MSTCS would be required in order to achieve interference free operation. Since the MSTCS data link was optimized for operation in the current band, moving to a new band would require extensive redesign, potentially wasting all of the incurred $20 to 40M investment costs, and incurring additional costs in test program delays. As indicated in Table 3, the system replacement cost has not been determined.

RAJPO Tri-Service Air/Ground Data Link. RAJPO supports range operations (military test and training) missions by providing precise position and velocity data. The RAJPO DLS was designed with a number of 3 MHz wide channels, selectable within software control. A minimum of three channels, separated by some minimum required number of megahertz, are necessary to support a given mission. Therefore, the maximum number of missions possible at a given range or within a geographic area is dependent on the number of available channels, and fewer available channels equates to fewer test missions per unit time. The Southwestern US is one example where spectrum congestion is an issue within a RAJPO DLS operation area. Within this area, there are six test and training facilities in close proximity to each other, each requiring unique and compatible DLS frequencies for simultaneous, interference-free operations. There are also several FAA and DoD radars in the region whose frequencies must be avoided in order to prevent interference. The close proximity of the test and training ranges creates conditions that use up most of the available spectrum in the 1350-1400 MHz band. Interference prevention is compounded by the frequent need to use the RAJPO DLS and MSTCS together during the same operation. If the ranges are unable to perform simultaneous operations, it will limit their ability to effectively schedule test events. The Air Force estimated that the costs due to delays in aircraft testing may exceed $1M per occurrence. The costs to the Army and Navy for test delays are similar and are in addition to those of the Air Force. Flight test and range personnel, as well as specialized range hardware are idled during delays in testing, but still incur costs. These potential program delay costs are not included in Table 3, due to the uncertainties involved. However, in order to avoid the costs and operational impacts associated with flight test delays, it will be necessary to modify RAJPO. Modifying the RAJPO will result in an estimated nonrecurring cost of $10M and an estimated recurring cost totaling $13M, and will take 5 years to complete.

MK-23 Shipborne Radar. The MK-23 radar has a surveillance (shipborne air defense warning) mission. Although the use of frequencies registered within the government master file (GMF) is generally authorized for operation no closer than 100 nautical miles (nmi) from the coast, and are on a non-interference basis (NIB), various highly populated areas have pre-coordinated authorization for co-primary use of the affected channels within 100 nmi of the coast. Reducing the available tuning range of this system will reduce the anti-jamming and interference margin of the radar, making it more susceptible to interference from new and existing systems operating in the remaining portion of the band. The impact of losing part of the system’s tuning range is particularly severe if, as frequently happens, two or more ships are operating within 200 nmi of each other. Such interference can be severe enough to render the radar incapable of detecting targets and performing its mission. The Navy will implement operational procedures to restrict the system’s tuning range in coastal waters.

AN/TPS-59 Tactical Radar. This radar has a surveillance (air defense warning) mission. The Marine Corps operates the AN/TPS-59 long-range air surveillance radar and point-to-point digital, wide band communication systems in the 1215-1400 MHz band. Operational procedures will be implemented to restrict the tuning of these systems to below 1390 MHz.

Operational Impacts and Assessment of Impact

AN/FPS-117/AN/FPS-124/ARSR-4 Radars. There are 33 AN/FPS-117 sites across Alaska, Northern Canada, Hawaii, and Iceland; 40 AN/FPS-124 sites across Alaska and Northern Canada; and 44 ARSR-4 sites around the perimeter of the continental US, Alaska, and Hawaii. Collectively, these radars are our primary system for detection and interception of unidentified and/or hostile, atmospheric weapon delivery systems. They are used to maintain our air sovereignty, support ATC, and for drug interdiction missions. In order to avoid gaps in coverage, these radars operate within view of one another, and in fact, overlapping coverage of critical sectors is a direct mission requirement. Decreased survivability, increased susceptibility to jamming, and the potential for gaps in the radar coverage of North America will result from the loss of the full design operating frequency range. This, in turn, presents a serious deficiency in the overall Integrated Tactical Warning Attack Assessment (ITW/AA) system and its Atmospheric Early Warning Systems. The system’s current implementation of diplexing, i.e., the use of dual frequencies, provides the necessary high accuracy and clarity for target detection. Diplexing also provides the user with the ability to avoid interference with adjacent systems, and with the capability to operate in a jamming environment without jeopardizing continuity of operations. Narrowing the operating frequency range greatly degrades NORAD’s ability to avoid radar-to-radar system interference and hostile jamming.

MSTCS. MSTCS is a developmental program whose immediate goal is to provide a common control system for unmanned target vehicles, initially at three DoD ranges, and whose ultimate goal is to extend that system to all DoD ranges. The data link for the MSTCS has been designed to operate on two frequencies within the 1350-1390 MHz band. This is a multi-user band, and frequencies used for transmission are chosen to reduce interference between the two MSTCS frequencies and between those frequencies used by the RAJPO DLS and the ARSR-4 radars. With the loss of the proposed spectrum, there may be no clear frequencies available for the MSTCS at some test ranges without shutting down other systems.

RAJPO Tri-Service Air/Ground Data Link. The DoD’s investment in the RAJPO DLS may be jeopardized if continued access to the 1350-1400 MHz band is not available. The loss of the proposed bandwidth may eliminate the ability to conduct simultaneous independent operations at some test ranges.

1427-1432 MHz

Overview of 1427-1432 MHz

The 1427-1432 MHz band is used for tactical radio relay, test range aeronautical telemetry, and telecommand for various types of guided weapons systems.

The cost estimate for reallocation of this band is $23M. This estimate is based on the assumption that range telemetry can continue in the 1432-1435 MHz band and that the loss of spectrum will not severely degrade the system’s capability. Degraded operation of range telemetry equipment will cause scheduling delays, and modifications will be required to control systems to maintain safe and reliable telecommand of launched missiles, range scoring systems, and Tethered Aerostat Radar Systems (TARS). It was also assumed that airborne weapon command and guidance systems need to be retrofitted to use the 1432-1435 MHz band if they are presently wired for the 1427-1432 MHz band. Although there would be adverse impact, it was assumed that tactical communications could be accomplished in other bands.

Reallocation of this band causes negative impacts on weapons systems inventory readiness and on the training of pilots to use of those weapons. The impact is compounded because the weapons data link that operates in this band is also a critical component of major new missile system acquisitions.

OBRA-93 Summary for 1427-1432 MHz

NTIA identified the 1427-1432 MHz band for reallocation on an Exclusive, non-Federal use basis beginning in January 1999. Military airborne operations will be continued for nine years at the fourteen sites listed in Table 6.

Major Functions and Missions

This band is used by the military for tactical radio relay communications, military test range aeronautical radiolocation, telemetry, telecommand, and various types of guided weapon systems.

Paired band data links in 1350-1400 and 1427-1435 MHz rebroadcast aircraft position during tests and training. This system is deployed at all major military aircraft and missile test centers and is used in flight testing of aircraft for each of the Services.

Assumptions and Cost Drivers

RAJPO Tri-Service Air/Ground Data Link. RAJPO supports range operations (military test and training) missions. The Southwestern US is one example where spectrum congestion is an issue within a RAJPO DLS operation area. Within this area, there are six test and training facilities in close proximity to each other, each requiring unique and compatible DLS frequencies for simultaneous, interference-free operations. The close proximity of these ranges creates conditions that require the use of all presently available spectrum in both the 1350-1400 MHz and 1427-1435 MHz bands in order to prevent interference. Interference prevention is compounded by the frequent need to use the RAJPO DLS and MSTCS together on the same operation. Delays result when the ranges are unable to perform simultaneous operations. The Air Force estimated that the costs due to delays in aircraft testing may exceed $1M per occurrence. The costs to the Army and Navy for test delays are similar and are in addition to those of the Air Force. None of these test delay costs are included in the cost tables. In order to avoid the costs and operational impacts associated with flight test delays, it will be necessary to modify RAJPO at an estimated cost of $23M.

AWW-13 Data Link. The AWW-13 has a Command, Control and Communications data link mission. The loss of the 1427-1432 MHz band will impair the operations of Navy missile command and guidance telemetry systems, ultimately affecting weapon system testing and training. Navy pilots adhere to the “train as we will fight” policy when training and testing airborne weapons systems. The normal test and training missions are conducted at an altitude of 36,000 feet. At this altitude, the weapons are well within line-of-sight of adjacent, major metropolitan areas. Missiles in the inventory have factory-fixed frequencies of operation within the 1427-1432 MHz band and their expected service life runs well into the next century.

Operational Impacts and Assessment of Impact

RAJPO Tri-Service Air/Ground Data Link. The Air Force, Army, and Navy all operate the RAJPO DLS in this band. The RAJPO DLS is operated in the vicinity of twenty-two DoD test and training ranges, and in the broad areas covered by their associated airspace, to relay precise air vehicle locations to ground control locations and ground systems. The RAJPO DLS is crucial to the flight testing of many high value aircraft. To achieve the required communications reliability, this system operates in both the 1350-1400 and 1427-1435 MHz bands. NTIA has stated:(4)

"A concern in reallocating this band (1427-1432 MHz) involves the RAJPO GPS Data Link System. To achieve the designed communications reliability under low-level flight conditions, simultaneous dual frequency usage is required, with adequate frequency separation to assure reliable communications. Since adequate frequency separation is not available solely within 1350-1400 MHz, continued primary access to a minimum of 3 MHz in the 1429-1435 MHz band is essential, if this $70M Federal investment is to be preserved."

Navy Weapons Development. The 1427-1435 MHz band is used for proficiency training using various guided weapon systems. The weapon systems and supporting data links that operate in this band include the AWW-13 Advanced Data Link, used in the Walleye and SLAM. The current Navy inventory includes approximately 200 Walleye and 800 SLAM weapon systems. The loss of this band for missile command operations will render Navy systems more susceptible to jamming and will impair their terminal guidance. Compounding the problem are developmental weapons, such as the Joint Standoff Weapon Unitary (JSOW Unitary), that will use the AWW-13. The AWW-13 requires spectrum for both command and video functions.

Table 9 lists the military base locations provided with protection zones for the 1427-1432 MHz band, as a part of OBRA-93. First, the radius of operation for air vehicles generally exceeds the protection zones, as shown in the table, and second, the radio line-of-sight for an aircraft at a typical flight test altitude of 36,000 feet is over 400 km. This means that an aircraft near the edge of its operating area may already be outside the protection zone, and it has a potential radio interference range that extends up to an additional 400 km. 1710-1755 MHz

Overview of 1710-1755 MHz

This band is used by the military for fixed, point-to-point communications and weapons command and control. Additional limited use is for ATC.

The low cost estimate for reallocation of this band is $31.32M and is based on re-engineering and modifying systems to operate in adjacent parts of the spectrum.

The high cost estimate for reallocation of this band is $131.32M and is based on re-engineering and modifying systems to operate in adjacent parts of the spectrum, and retuning weapon command and control links.

Although many of the tactical communications systems in this band can tune throughout the 1350-1850 MHz band, this 10 percent loss of spectrum is significant because it further compresses the authorized segments available for use in the US, and exacerbates the already difficult task of determining interference free frequency assignments. Significant costs will be incurred to re-engineer and modify other systems that presently use this spectrum.

NTIA identified the 1710-1755 MHz band for reallocation on a Mixed, Federal/non-Federal use basis beginning in January 2004. · Reimbursement is provided to the affected Federal agencies

· Appropriate Federal Agency acquisition procedures are implemented to support moving the Federal systems to another band, and

· Suitable and sufficient radio spectrum is available for the move

In addition, Federal operations that provide safety-of-life and other critical functions, and are located outside the twenty-five largest US cities, are also exempted from reallocation and will continue operation and be protected from interference.

ACE. Tactical Radio Relay. Lightweight, transportable equipment is a fundamental requirement for the dynamic battlefield environment facing the Army. They use the 1710-1750 MHz band extensively for headquarters nodal connectivity within their area-wide integrated communications networks. This equipment is transportable to support a fast moving network and flexible to the tactical tempo and operating conditions. The transmitter power, propagation characteristics, available bandwidth, and other physical and technical factors make this the ideal band to support such operations. The option of tuning to another part of the band is an undesirable one due to existing congestion. Although these systems can tune throughout the 1350-1850 MHz band, this 10 percent loss is significant because it further compresses the authorized segments available for use and exacerbates the already difficult task of determining interference free tactical frequency assignments.

TARS. The Aerostats can be tuned to another part of the 1755-1850 MHz band.

Hill AFB ATC. The ATC system at Hill AFB can be retuned to another part of the 1755-1850 MHz frequency band at an estimated cost of $20,000.

Microwave Communication Links. Air Force gap-filler radar, command and control, and other point-to-point microwave links can be re-engineered to operate in another frequency band at an estimated cost of $8.3M.

Guided Weapon Missile Systems. Reallocation of this band could reduce the anti-jamming capability of these systems by approximately 40 percent, rendering the guidance links useless in the presence of jamming. Re-engineering and modifications of the approximately 1,000 units would be required at an estimated cost of $100M.

Army tactical communications (MSE/TRITAC) links will suffer the greatest impacts from this reallocation. Assigning frequencies for tactical operations will become more difficult, making it likely that interference will adversely impact certain operations. The larger an operation becomes, the more likely it is that interference will create problems.

The other systems listed can largely be accommodated in other bands or parts of the 1755-1850 MHz band, but will incur re-engineering and modification costs to implement the changes in frequency.

Reallocation of the 2402-2417 MHz band on an Exclusive basis was projected to impact the interrogator at estimated costs of $125K for FY94, $450K for FY95, $10M for FY96, $14M for FY97, and $11M for FY98.

MST-T1A. Modifications to electronic warfare equipment to lock-out the reallocated band segments were estimated at $1M for changes to system software.

Operational Impacts and Assessment of Impact

The systems that operate in this band generally have wide tuning ranges that can accommodate the reallocation. The operational impacts of this reallocation are difficult to quantify, but the inability to conduct special research projects does ultimately impact advances in science and technology for both the Federal and non-Federal sectors.

Overview of 3650-3700 MHz

The primary military system of concern in this band is the Navy’s aircraft carrier ATC radar. Additional uses include Air Force and Army electronic countermeasures (ECM) training and advanced development research for hypersonic air vehicles. Reallocation of the band on a Mixed use basis and protection of essential radar operations at three sites will minimize the impact on military systems. The cost impact of this reallocation for analytical, training, and documentation is estimated at over $100K.

NTIA identified the 3600-3650 MHz band for reallocation on a Mixed, Federal/non-Federal use basis beginning in January 1999. ECM Training / HySTP. Testing and electronic warfare training activities will suffer some adverse impact due to the loss of tuning range but will implement operational work arounds to accommodate the reallocation.

Assumptions and Cost Drivers

Tactical Radio Relay. The estimated cost of retuning and replacing these systems is $3.65M.

UAV Data Links. The Army operates an UAV system at the National Training Center in Ft Irwin, CA. Preliminary cost estimates of the impact to this system are in excess of $10M.

AAW & Ship Defense Links. The next generation wide band anti-air warfare and ship defense system is being developed to operate in these and adjacent bands. Reallocation options, transition plans, and specific reallocation cost estimates for these systems were not available. However, a significant amount has been spent for the research, design, and early development of this defense system.

Operational Impacts and Assessment of Impact

The tactical radio relay systems that operate in this band are used by both the Army and Air Force to conduct point-to-point and troposcatter communications for comprehensive, realistic training, humanitarian relief, natural disaster operations, and to maintain combat readiness. The reasons for not tuning these systems in other frequency bands include the existing congestion in those bands and various technical issues. In essence, the technical issues are (1) that moving to a lower frequency band limits the available information bandwidth and results communications delays and (2) the physics of propagation associated with moving to a higher band limits link distances and increases set-up and alignment times. Neither of these encumbrances is tolerable in today’s rapid-tempo, highly mobile battlefield. The Army and Air Force have accommodated the reallocations by restricting the tuning range of their tactical radio relay systems and are attempting to address the adverse impacts through advanced frequency management techniques and other operational control measures.

It is unlikely that the reallocation will have any impact on the Navy AAW/Ship Defense links during 'blue water' operations conducted well away from the coast. However, the increasing requirement to train for and conduct littoral exercises increases the concern that interference may result from this adjacent band system.

Primary Sites Affected

The Army UAV system is operated at the National Training Center at Ft Irwin, CA. Tactical communications systems are operated nation-wide. Navy shipboard systems are operated in various test/training areas along US coastlines.
