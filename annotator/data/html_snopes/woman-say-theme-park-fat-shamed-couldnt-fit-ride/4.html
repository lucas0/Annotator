Ep. 220: Kat Brown Akoochuk - Being Robbed of Our Connection to Ourselves

7/8/2019

0 Comments

 
Haudenosaunee bead artist and advocate, Kat Brown Akoochuk
     Kat Brown Akoochuk is a Haudenosaunee member of the Oneida Nation of the Thames. She currently lives on the traditional territory of the Kanaka Maoli on the island of Oahu while she reclaims her tribal identity. Kat is an advocate for radical fat acceptance and identifies as fat herself but she doesn’t talk about her body a lot in her personal life and that’s because a lot of people in her life haven’t done the same amount of work around fatness and fat acceptance. Jana wanted to interview Kat because of her online advocacy and her beadwork!  
     Kat and Jana discuss being white-coded, having family members who do not pass and how they could never blame anyone for quieting their Native identity and move through the world navigating anti-Indigenous racism. Kat is relearning her own Indigenous language as well as the languages of the places in which she lives and the language of her Native Alaskan husband. 
     Jana jumps right into discussing Kat’s beadwork. Beadwork was one of Kat’s first step into reclaiming her identity. The two discuss how beading is medicine, both for the beader and the wearer of the beadwork. They chat about pricing their beadwork and how beadwork is always underpriced for the amount of labor it requires. They also bring up the ways in which wearing beadwork as a non-Native person is not appropriation as long as the wearer is not dressing up as a Native. 
     Kat and Jana discuss the Elizabeth Warren issue and online microaggressions that Native people often incur, especially around politically heightened moments in mainstream media. Kat brings up the “America is a Nation of immigrants!” quote that folks like to throw around which silences the existence of Indigenous people on this land. When AOC said it and got called in by Katherine Cricket Crocker on Twitter, AOC actually apologized, which is more humanity than Native people usually get! Of course, the same courtesy was not provided by Warren and the two dissect that issue at length. Another issue that they discuss are Black Indigeneity and how there is a lot of anti-Black rhetoric and thought within colonized Native communities. 
    Going hand in hand with lack of representation and mainstream’s misconceptions about Native beauty is carrying a fat identity. Not only are Native women expected to look a certain way, through the colonized lens, we better not appear as the fat, lazy, alcoholic, drug addicted stereotype we’re told we are. Kat uses frybread as a metaphor for our a story of survival. Jana wonders how much Native communities have internalized our own stereotypes as well as how much medical professionals have internalized them in order to pathologize Native communities. The two discuss identity politics within Indian Country and Kat tells the audience to read the work of Kim Tallbear. You can hear Kim on Media Indigena. 
    Kat closes out the interview by discussing how fat acceptance plays out in the many different places she’s lived all over the world. She grew up in Saudi Arabia, lived in Canada and found the most body acceptance - surprisingly - in Japan. She illustrates this experience in discussing Japanese communal baths.     
Kat’s ONE COOL THING is a book called Trail of Lightening by Rebecca Roanhorse and Jana’s ONE COOL THING is the actor Michael Greyeyes on the third season of True Detective. Check em out!

Ep. 219: Laura House - Wordy, Nerdy and Pretty Sturdy

7/1/2019

0 Comments

 
Comedian, writer and meditation teacher Laura House
     Laura House is a writer, comedian, meditation instructor and former educator who doesn’t really think about her body. Laura and Jana open their discussion by talking about their education roots, their school experiences and growing up under teacher parents. Laura has a storied career in Hollywood and she discusses her first acting job on MTV. Times have really changed in the way that we discuss bodies in Hollywood, not only the way fatness is identified but also the way the fat narrative is depicted. 
    Laura launches into discussing the ways her body image was formed by the women in her family - the reality of indulgence right alongside body shame, and these themes are replayed in our families as well as institutionally. As Laura recounts they way her body was seen by her family as a young girl, she also discusses the generation that our mothers and grandmothers grew up within and how the values of that time were really the origination of modern American body image issues. Those Mad Men days really rattled our sense of self and people seeing us. 
Laura House and husband
    Jana and Laura launch into discussing body image in their comedy and the ways in which modern culture, social media culture promotes fat positivity, especially for women of a certain age who may not have been exposed to this kind of thinking throughout their adult lives. The two talk about how fatness has carved out space - albeit a small space - so that choice for fat women has become accessible. 
    Ultimately, Laura argues that being authentically herself right now has brought her more joy, more fulfilling relationships and a stronger understanding of her direction in life. After telling us about her early career in comedy, tv writing and the endless hustle of Hollywood. She chats about her recovery from alcohol addiction and falling into a practice of meditation as a relief from the anxiety of the Hollywood grind. Laura leads us through a meditation on the show and shows us just how easy it is! Take a meditation class with Laura, you won’t regret it!

Ep. 218: Wendi Muse: A Leftists History Lesson

6/24/2019

0 Comments

 
Scholar, historian and leftist researcher Wendi Muse
     Wendi Muse is a Black woman from the South and identifies as a descendent of slaves from two Black parents. She has begun to see her body as a thing that functions or has difficulty functioning after time and experience. She’s technically in straight sizes but shops in plus size and men’s sizes for a preferable fit, an experience that has made her aware of sizeism and the use of numbers to qualify bodies. She was recently diagnosed with MS and because of the plethora of physical symptoms that come with Multiple Sclerosis, Wendi has taken the perspective around her body of just identifying how it feels and how to keep symptoms at bay. Her relationship to self has changed after her diagnosis.  
    Wendi is a leftist anti-racist and argues that the fundamental texts and teachings of leftist politics do synthesize the ills of racism, classicism, sexism and homophobia. Her doctoral work at NYU is in Brazilian History and Leftists from Brazil and Anti-colonial Activism in Brazil. Her research has led her to believe that leftist movements, anti-colonial movements and anti-racist movements go hand-in-hand and absolutely inform how a people relate to themselves, relate one another and engage in work around race and racism. Wendi embarked on this field of work after studying Portuguese in Brazil in 2004, connecting with the people of Brazil and learning about the history of slavery in Brazil which is far more pronounced than slavery in the U.S. After teaching English in Brazil and getting to know many African, specifically Angolan students studying in Brazil, Wendi embarked on the study of Brazilian History, the history of enslavement and the connection and communication between Brazil and Africa.
     Jana asks Wendi how her studies have connected to leftism in the U.S. She describes seeing the poverty of Brazilian communities in her travels during the Obama presidency and the Lula presidency, who is now imprisoned. She has seen the effort to lift the poor out of poverty in Brazil to a degree that the U.S. has never really exerted. This influenced her understanding of the politics in the U.S. in the Democratic Party specifically and her realization that there is a lot that leftists could be doing to address economic inequality in the States. She sees potential within our political system but recognizes that we have a long way to go. As far our current 2019 politics go, Wendi addresses Trump, his administration and many of his supporters as fascists, and argues that we cannot dance around the issue. The value in characterizing Trump a fascist is that we have a playbook to combat fascism. Wendi compares the anti-facist movements happening in Brazil to those happening in the U.S., primarily using “Alternative Government Spaces ” through very local community cooperatives aimed at reducing harm for families living with economic precarity.
Wendi takes us on a deep dive into her own political leanings here in the U.S. being left of the Democratic party and helps us to come to terms with our own “a la carte leftism.” She takes on the issue of Indigenous blood quantum with Jana, anti-racist politics amongst white politicians, racialized rhetoric versus anti-racist action. The ladies culminate the interview with Wendi discussing current fascist President Bulsonaro and the Quilombos of Brazil, who Bolsonaro wants to steal land from to hand over to agricultural conglomerates. 
    Wendi’s One Cool Thing is a podcast called Millenials Are Killing Capitalism. She specifically shouts out episode #27 called Trans Liberation Through Decolonial Marxism. We will tune in!

Ep. 217: Caleb Luna - Race, Class and The Fat Body

6/17/2019

0 Comments

 
Writer, scholar and fat femme activist, Caleb Luna
    Queer, femme writer, educator, activist Caleb Luna uses the word fat to describe their body, along with the word Brown as a descriptor because of their relationship to Latinx and Chicanx culture. Jana asks Caleb to discuss and educate listeners about the term “femme” before they launch into a wider discussion about fatphobia. Caleb makes the case the feminine identities, specifically in queer communities - are more “under inspection” than others and under constant scrutinization. Caleb and Jana discuss how confusing the word masculinity has become, with no clear signifiers other than anger and hostility or toxicity.
         Caleb applies meaning to their existence in the intersection of fat, femme and Brown. They describe marginalization even in QPOC spaces and how much they struggle holding that complication. Caleb has been taking up as a point that fatness is “the last acceptable predjudice”  because they argue that it exists in tandem with many other oppressions. Caleb goes on to point out that fatness in itself isn’t the source of oppression, rather it signals to the viewer associations to class difference and racial implications - therefore fatness does not exist within a vacuum. Caleb makes a succinct point in connecting fatness to classism and poverty which inherently associates to race - Black and Brown bodies - which is intrinsically connected to settler colonialism and the transatlantic slave trade. So when fatphobia occurs, consciously a person hates the fat body for it’s “poverty” but subconsciously experiences white supremacy and anti-Black and Brown disgust.  And are we discussing trauma’s effect on the body? How has 500 years of enslavement and genocide made an impact on the physical body? Caleb pulls from Joy DeGruy’s Post Traumatic Slave Syndrome, a study that examines the lasting effects of the transatlantic slave trade. Still, the two discuss how studies and science are still minimal around the connection between fatness and settler colonialism. Caleb also drops the title Fearing the Black Body: The Racial Origins of Fatphobia by Sabrina Strings and cites how she is making poignant connections between fat phobia and Black Womanhood.
  Caleb discusses the difference between “fat positivity” and “fat justice” in their work. Caleb celebrates their own fatness while describing equity issues for fat people. They identify how bleak access looks for fat folx in professional, medical and academic spaces. Caleb brings into the conversation the term “fat liberation” which is deeply tied to Black liberation and decolonization because fatphobia comes from a very Western, hierarchical value system. Caleb and Jana discuss their indoctrination into settler colonial narratives and how settler colonialism is a structure not an event and that it can be redesigned and restructured. The two discuss similarly how to exist within and outside of capitalism and the shitty privilege that exists within that discourse. 
         Jana and Caleb’s discussion ends in Caleb explaining how their mental and emotional capacity has been impacted by the space they’ve created in academia to discuss fatphobia and how privileged people have taken advantage of this space to ere their fatphobia. The two discuss how leaving the house means dealing with toxic, fatphobic, inaccessible environments and the realities of being exhausted by this world. It cuts down to the ways we’re relating to each other, taking care of each other and providing space for each other to exist physically and emotionally at all in personal spaces. Eventually the two get onto tv shows, their favs and the amazingly bad cold opens in Great British Bakeoff.

Ep. 216: Ashleigh Tribble - Let's Talk About SEX, Fat Folx

6/10/2019

0 Comments

 
Ashleigh Tribble - Fat, Sex Posi Feminist, Activist and Artist
Ashley Tribble identifies as a queer fat Black woman and only uses affirming and plain language about her body in order to normalize the use of “fat.” She describes discovering fat positivity and fat positive language in college when Natalie Hodge modeled it for Ashleigh’s college community. Ashleigh’s body image changed then and she became more radicalized in her feminism and her fat acceptance advocacy.  
    Ashleigh unfolds her thoughts about thin privilege and thin privilege within the fat community. She believes that fatness is becoming somewhat trendy which pushes larger fat-bodied individuals back into the margins. She tells stories about microaggressions she’s encountered and weight stigma as a bigger body while watching smaller fat women’s bodies and experiences become commodified and centered in more mainstream conversations. Ashleigh also identifies where Blackness enters into this mess, being appropriated and decentered online and on tv. She states that we only see one kind of fat experience being proliferated where we’re not talking about race, toxic positivity and systemic fatphobia.
     Ashleigh’s mission is exposing the narrative that “sex is not for fat people,” a message that is intentionally pushed in sex positive spaces and environments. She goes in about how this new wave of sex positivity is not at all inclusive to fat bodies, therefore, those living the fat experience are often not having important discussions about how to elevate their sexual experiences. If the language isn’t being explored, than the experience isn’t advancing. 
     To illustrate her opinions about fat exclusion in sex posi spaces, Ashleigh pulls upon her experience working in a dungeon. All of the same things that she encounters as a fat Black woman in the regular world are amplified in her sex work. Fetishization, racialized fantasies and anti-fat biases showed their asses in her work. Ashleigh discusses sex posi, feminist events that also exhibit fatphobia and white supremacy in their hosting and moderation. Ashleigh firmly illuminates that discussions about fatphobia in sex, or why fat romance and fat sex are erased are extremely important conversations that we need to have. So much of the sex that cishet men are having with fat women is informed by really badly developed porn. Ashleigh shares many stories of being mistreated sexually by men and the lack of communication around sex with men that was genuinely bad! It begs the question, how much GOOD SEX do fat women have access to because of anti-fat bias? Dare we interrogate those private circumstances where we are being told that “this is the best you can get” because of our fat bodies?
