Well it appears that the people of the time looked a good deal different than we commonly depict in Western movies including the movies made only 40 to 50 years after the end of the Old West era. The photographs we have depict Western men wearing many of the same clothes we see in the photographs of the Eastern United States taken at the same time. Most men wore hats but the hats were commonly bowler and Derbys, not Stetsons. Stetsons were rare.

Moreover, very few of them were clean shaven. It was the style of the time to have, at minimum a moustache. Wyatt Earp, Bat Mastermasterson, Vermillion Jack, John Wesley Harden and almost every other male adult had a moustache or moustache and beard in the Old West, just as they had in the Eastern sections of the United States at the same time.

Formal QuickDraw gunfights were rare and almost non-existent. Although there were probably several, the only historically recorded one was Bill Hickock and Dave Tutt. However he also had more formal duels that contained elements of a fast draw duel and rapid firing. In 1867, two years after killing Tutt, Hickock was involved in a count down duel with 4 drunken Cowboys, killing 3 and wounding the 4th.

Gunfights were so rare that the Shootout at the OK Correl has become famous as the epitome of the Old West gunfight by a town or city Marshall force and a gang of outlaws, and mostly because it was recorded through trial transcripts.

Another common misconception concerns Doc Holiday, Wyatt Earps close friend. Holiday who was a genuinely tragic figure, did indeed become famous as a gunfighter during his lifetime, especially after the OK Correl shootout. However, in reality he was a terrible shot, mostly because of his constant alcohol consumption. Frequently but not always, the safest place to be in a gunfight with Doc Holiday was standing straight in front of him.

Judge Roy Bean, who advertised himself as the law west of the Pecos, is commonly depicted as a more or less self-appointed hanging judge. He was a Justice of the Peace. He was neither completely self-appointed nor is there any record that he ever hanged anyone, although there is evidence that he commonly threatened to do so.

Lastly there is a very common misconception about the Indians. They are depicted today as noble savages whos land and cultures were stolen by White American Settlers. In reality, the Comanches and Sioux and certain other groups and tribe were hated, feared and forced out because they were indeed vicious and brutal. They commonly murdered raped and tortured the people they captured and at one time had the entire Southwest in fear. They were the real reason for the formation of the Texas Rangers.

This is a huge question. How do we even define the Wild West? At its geographical narrowest, it's unbelievably huge: from Texas to Montana, from the Mississippi River to the Pacific Ocean. And you could say that it was even bigger than that. The Western story is often traced as far back as Cooper's often-filmed Leatherstocking Tales, which are set in upstate New York. Was the Klondike Gold Rush part of the Wild West? Emperor Maximilian of Mexico and the battle of Buena Vista? You could argue it.

In time, where does "the Wild West" begin? With the end of the Civil War? Then we exclude The Good, the Bad, and the Ugly, whose theme music is almost the definition of the Western movie. With the beginning of the Civil War? Then we exclude Pale Rider and most of How the West Was Won. With the Louisiana Purchase? Then we still exclude all the versions of The Deerslayer and The Last of the Mohicans. And when does the era end? The dawn of the 20th century? Then we exclude Destry Rides Again. With World War I? Then we exclude The Treasure of the Sierra Madre. With the Great Depression? Then we exclude Brokeback Mountain.

The Western movie is a subject almost as sprawling as the actual historical West; to say anything that would be true of all of the westerns ever made is almost as hard as saying something true of all of the real-life West. The Great Train Robbery was made in 1903, five years before the bloody death in Bolivia of two outlaws named Robert Leroy Parker and Harry Longabaugh, better known as Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid. 112 years after The Great Train Robbery, westerns are still being made; Django Unchained won gold at the Oscars in 2012 and grossed $425 million, and the westerns released in 2015 alone featured the likes of Samuel L. Jackson, Michael Fassbender, Leonardo DiCaprio, and Kurt Russell. In those 112 years, westerns have evolved dramatically, from the simplistic black hats and white hats of the silents, to the more literate westerns John Ford introduced in Stagecoach, to the more violently amoral antiheroes of Duel at Diablo and The Wild Bunch, to the more humanized, realistic, and morally ambiguous characters of Unforgiven. And that's just in America; the western is America's most recognizable cinematic export. Not only are John Wayne and Clint Eastwood some of the best known American faces on TV sets worldwide, but foreign film industries have adopted the western and made it their own with gusto. Italy, of course, made the "spaghetti western" the most popular form even in America, but also Cneyt Arkin introduced the Western to a whole generation in Turkey, and India made westerns enthusiastically in the 1970s; there are few sights more surreal to an American moviegoer than K.S.R. Das's Pilla Piduga, with Hindi starlets in bright saris dancing Bollywood-style for crowds of Stetson-sporting, Colt .45-toting cowboys in mockup saloons. No, the Wild West wasn't much like that, but certainly some western movies have come a lot closer.

A question of emphasis

Many of the people and events shown in Westerns are real, which is not to say that they're accurately depicted. Jesse James, Billy the Kid, George Armstrong Custer, Geronimo, Wyatt Earp, Isaac "The Hanging Judge" Parker (True Grit), Wild Bill Hickok (Little Big Man), Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid are all real; so are the gunfight at the O.K. Corral, the battle of Little Bighorn, cattle drives, the California Gold Rush, massacres of whites by natives, massacres of natives by whites, bounties on outlaws, Comanche kidnappings of children (The Searchers), train and bank robberies, and so on. But it is one thing to say that these people and events were real, and another to say that they were as important or prominent as the movies make them.

Who do we not see in westerns, although they were there in the real West? Until about 1960, blacks were almost completely absent from westerns, although in history they were about one seventh of all cowboys. In 1960, John Ford made Sergeant Rutledge, about a falsely accused member of the black Buffalo Soldiers, but not until Duel at Diablo in 1966 was an A-list black actor (Sidney Poitier) able to appear in a western role, as a horsebreaker, where his race was not the mainly important thing about him. Hispanics another one seventh of the cowboys, were almost as invisible; the 1978 miniseries Centennial in 1978 was one of the first to give them a role as cowpunchers. We often hear complaints about how native Americans are shown in westerns, but at least they are there, and not always as villains; sympathetic portrayals appeared as far back as They Died With Their Boots On in 1941.

But the racial blind spots have largely been corrected today. What we still do not see are events and issues of the utmost importance to the actual people of the West. Labor unions in the gold mines of Virginia City, Nevada and Coeur d'Alene, Idaho; the fight over free silver, the Grange and the Populists. Republican and Democratic party politics and presidential elections are never shown; sometimes an election on statehood is shown, but with the party politics left out. Farm co-ops and their struggle with the railroads are hardly ever seen.

An example: in the early 1900s, Glacier National Park, home of the Blackfeet people, was advertised to Eastern tourists as one of the last places you could see the American Indians, who most whites thought would all be gone soon. Thousands of tourists came and saw scenes like this:

Blackfeet family and tipi, 1909.

Blackfeet promoting Glacier National Park, 1924.

White people who had never seen a Native American before saw these folks, and made movies with "Indians" who looked like this. And so every Native American in every western, from the Yaqui to the Apache to the Kiowa to the Oglala, ended up looking like the Blackfeet of Glacier National Park. You rarely saw natives who looked or dressed like this:

Quanah Parker, Comanche chief.

Goyaale (dubbed "Geronimo" by the Mexicans), Apache war leader.

The movie image is based on reality, yes, but only a small part of the reality.

The clean-shaven hero

Curse Woodrow Wilson. In 1913, the clean-shaven Princetonian replaced our last mustachioed president, William Taft, and for decades afterward, most western heroes had to be baby-faced, no matter that the people they were playing invariably had mustaches.

Movie gunfighters don't go to jail

There were certainly many occasions in the real Old West where a man gunned another down; on a few of those occasions, it was face-to-face on a street or in a tavern as in many classic western movie scenes. What the movies almost never include, though, is that, much like today, the killer could expect to end up in jail. When Wild Bill Hickok shot Davis Tutt in Springfield, Missouri in 1866, he was promptly charged with murder and put on trial, though he was acquitted. Luke Short was arrested and charged for the killing of Charlie Storms in Tombstone in 1881 and again for killing Jim Courtright in 1887, although both times the charges were dismissed before trial. "Deacon" Jim Miller, a psychotic killer-for-hire, stood trial for his murders and was acquitted so many times that a frustrated mob in Ada, Oklahoma finally lynched him in 1909.

But not all gunfighters escaped justice. John Wesley Hardin was finally convicted in 1878 for the killing of deputy Charles Webb and served 17 years in prison. Bill Longley, who claimed to have killed as many as 32 men, was hanged in 1878 in Giddings, Texas for the murder of his former pal Wilson Anderson.

Much like today, there was an exception to the rule: a lawman who killed in the line of duty was not likely to stand trial. Hickok had been tried for Tutt's murder, but after he pinned on the city marshal's bade of Abilene, Kansas, he was not charged for killing Phil Coe, nor even for the accidental shooting of his own deputy Mike Williams.

In the movies, gunfighters who leave bodies in the street in front of witnesses are rarely even arrested. It is assumed that the "Code of the West" excuses anyone who shoots an armed opponent in the front in a fair fight. While such an idea may have been one reason so many real gunfighters were acquitted (like Hickok with Tutt), it generally didn't help you avoid contact with the law altogether.

Unlimited ammo and other gun sins

OK, this is a flaw of lots of action movies, not just westerns. It stands out in westerns, though, because so much of the shooting is with revolvers that held only six rounds - in some models, only five. This could be reduced to five shots and four respectively if the carrier left the hammer down on an empty chamber, which was safest with the single-action revolvers of the time. In the final scene of Butch Cassidy and the Sundance Kid, Robert Redford fires at least 23 shots from two 1873 Colt six-shooters without reloading. In Pale Rider, seven men fire more than 60 shots at Spider - nine rounds per gun - without reloading or drawing another revolver. The actual gunfight at the O.K. Corral was over in a matter of thirty seconds, with almost everyone being out of ammo by the end; the film version from Tombstone takes 89 seconds, that from Wyatt Earp takes 45 seconds, and the one in 1957's Gunfight at the O.K. Corral takes up over four minutes.

Many real Western towns had laws against carrying guns. This is rarely mentioned in western movies, although it figures prominently in Unforgiven. But Unforgiven shows that enforcing such laws was a major difficulty, and the violence-checkered history of real boomtowns like Tombstone, Arizona, or cowtowns like Newton, Dodge City and Abilene in Kansas, shows that enforcement was questionable in real life, too.

Conclusion

This barely scratches the surface of the similarities and differences between the historical Wild West and the kaleidoscopic range of movie versions of it. Hundreds of books have been written about the Wests of film and reality. Overall, the trend has been toward a more realistic depiction of the West in movies, with the reality of racial diversity better portrayed, historical sources better consulted, and less tendency to draw superhuman characters, but someone who watched The Great Train Robbery in 1903 would have little trouble, exactly 100 years later, recognizing Open Range as a western.

Lets just take a look at one aspect of Westerns: shootouts

Pistols back then were not terribly accurate and the shooters werent either.

The Westerns where the good guy shoots twenty rounds from each pistol, hitting an enemy with half of them, are anything but realistic.

Gun shots knock people off rooftops and sometimes through doorssomething debunked pretty thoroughly on the TV show Myth Busters.

Westerns are more of a reflection of how TV and movie makers want to view the 19th century USA, west of the Mississippi River.

Westerns from the mid 20th century reflected the view of the hero who stood up against corrupt sheriffs, rich ranchers and savage Native Tribes.

More recently, the hero in the Western is usually a tortured soul who is manipulated by the Patriarchy to massacre the helpless, noble savage.

Both approaches have an element of truth but neither is very accurate.

Hollywood is not about accuracy. Hollywood is about entertainment and pushing its political agenda.

As that political agenda changes so do the emphases of Hollywood movies.
