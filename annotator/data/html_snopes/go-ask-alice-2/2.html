Be aware of the stages of cocaine addiction

By ROBERT WALLACE`
Syndicated Columnist

Dr. Wallace: I'm 19 and dating a guy who is 24. I care for him very, very much. He has a good job (he's a college graduate) and comes from a very nice and respectable family. His father is a veterinarian and his mother is a teacher. We have been dating for over three months, but there is one very major flaw in our relationship. He uses cocaine. He says he isn't addicted, but the cocaine helps him think more clearly when he has a problem to solve. This bothers me immensely. I keep telling him to stop using cocaine, but he tells me he has his usage under control.

How can I tell if he indeed does have it under control or cocaine has control of him? He says he buys the cocaine from a fellow employee, so don't you believe that all cocaine sellers are gang members or the grubby-looking characters we see pushing drugs in the movies. -- Nameless, Orange, Calif.

Nameless: Your boyfriend's regular use of cocaine even when you ask him to stop is an indication the drug is very important to him. It's extremely likely that he is addicted, but there's one sure way to find out -- give him a test. Tell him he has a choice, you or cocaine. In all likelihood, the cocaine, not you, will be the winner.

Since cocaine is a powerfully addictive substance, it can hook someone after the first or second use. Ella Green, R.N., a certified chemical-dependency nurse, says that cocaine addiction can has four major stages:


* Stage One: A compulsion to use. Frequent and persistent cravings. The desire to use becomes stronger than the desire to stop. An intense fear of being without cocaine develops.


* Stage Two: Loss of control. Unable to stop using for a significant length of time, unable to say no to the drug when it is offered, excessive use or "bingeing."


* Stage Three: Denial. Minimizing consequence of cocaine use, saying that everyone is using it and that the user is in control of the drug and can quit at any time.


* Stage Four: Ignoring negative consequences. Continued use despite negative consequences, decreasing school or work performance, social or legal problems, medical problems including: 1) constriction of blood vessels causing increased heart rate and blood pressure; 2) dilation of pupils; 3) increased sweating; 4) blurred vision; 5) headache; 6) nausea and diarrhea; 7) dizziness; 8) tremors; 9) coughing; 10) runny nose; 11) nosebleeds; 12) weight loss; 13) sleep disturbances; 14) chronic sore throat; 15) nervous twitching; 16) death! Numerous deaths have been documented among those who overdosed on the first use.

Do not stay with this guy as long as he continues to use cocaine. Encourage him to get professional help and don't be involved with him until he does.

Dr. Wallace: Four other mothers and I have formed a Help-A-Teen Club and we are looking for useful hints in parenting. What are five things parents should NOT say to their children? I mean everyday statements that are not helpful and therefore unnecessary. We are asking several other columnists (Dear Abby, Ann Landers, Dr. Joyce Brothers) for their no-no lists as well. -- Mother, Elizabethtown, Ky.

Mother: It's nice to be included in such a distinguished group of advice givers. My five no-nos are:


* Shut up!


* Because I said so!


* Yes, but you must take your little sister with you.


* Open up this door immediately!


* Just wait till your father gets home ...
