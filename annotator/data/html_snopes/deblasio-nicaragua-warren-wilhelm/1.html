From His Father’s Decline, de Blasio ‘Learned What Not to Do’

By Javier C. Hernández
  • Oct. 13, 2013
For a few fleeting years, Bill de Blasio had, in his words, a “classic American family life” with his father. He went with him to Little League and Boston Red Sox games, watched him grill steaks on the barbecue, joined him on evening rowboat rides.
But by the time Mr. de Blasio turned 7, family life had started to go bad. His father, Warren Wilhelm, was drunk and angry much of the day. At one family gathering, he fell to the floor and had trouble getting back up.
After he moved out, Mr. de Blasio’s mother was increasingly nervous about his visits with their children, worried that Mr. Wilhelm was driving while intoxicated.
In Mr. de Blasio’s eyes, his father was a tragic hero, a man who fought courageously in World War II, only to lose part of his left leg to the grenade of an enemy soldier he had just killed.
He returned with a Purple Heart but found his loyalty to the United States questioned in McCarthy-era investigations.
When Mr. de Blasio was 18, his father killed himself, firing a rifle into his heart while sitting in a car outside a motel in Connecticut.
Politicians often speak about the formative influences of parents: mothers who taught the importance of persistence, fathers who instilled a love of learning. But Mr. de Blasio, the Democratic nominee for mayor of New York City who has a big lead in the polls, has defined himself largely in opposition to his father.
At times, he felt so scarred that he wondered whether he could raise a family of his own.
“My father was a picture of courage in terms of his war service and strength, and yet in his decline, I learned primarily negative lessons,” he said in an interview. “I learned what not to do.”
He became so alienated from his father that he shed the name he was born with, Warren Wilhelm Jr., in favor of his childhood nickname, Bill, and his mother’s maiden name.
Mr. de Blasio said he was still struggling to understand how his father affected his life.
“I have a real respect, and a real anger and sadness at the same time,” Mr. de Blasio said. “I don’t think I’ve ever been able to do the math on exactly what it all means.”
Yale and Okinawa
Maria Angela de Blasio was unlike anyone Warren Wilhelm had ever met.
She took flying lessons. She could recite passages of T. S. Eliot. She made elaborate salami sandwiches in the style of her parents, immigrants from the hills near Naples, Italy.
Mr. Wilhelm and Ms. de Blasio had many things in common: They were both born in New York City in 1917 and had attended elite colleges. Mr. Wilhelm, who was born on Staten Island, went to boarding school in Connecticut at the Loomis School — now Loomis Chaffee — and studied economics at Yale. Ms. de Blasio, who was born in Manhattan, had received two degrees in English from Smith.
They met at Time magazine in the fall of 1939. Mr. Wilhelm was a business reporter — he later described himself as “one part journalist and two parts economist; place in glass with chipped ice and stir” — and Ms. de Blasio worked as a researcher, one of the few female employees.
But Europe was at war, and Mr. Wilhelm decided to enlist. “Hitler was running an aggressive show that threatened to endanger Western civilization, and I thought it ought to be stopped,” he later said. The Navy turned away Mr. Wilhelm as too tall — he was 6 feet 5 inches — but he was able to join the Army in 1942, four months after the attack on Pearl Harbor.
He was sent to the Pacific, where he hopped between islands on short combat missions and earned a Bronze Star. He corresponded regularly with Ms. de Blasio, who had started working as an editor at the Office of War Information in New York, preparing American propaganda for broadcast in Italy.
As the war neared its conclusion, Mr. Wilhelm expressed relief that he had managed to avoid harm. “No Purple Heart, thank God,” he wrote in a 1945 Yale alumni report. But in the spring of that year, he joined the Battle of Okinawa, an 82-day conflict that proved to be one of the war’s most horrific. More than 200,000 people were killed in a deluge of kamikaze attacks, gunfire and napalm bombs.
On May 30, 1945, a few weeks before the battle would end, Mr. Wilhelm rushed ahead of his fellow soldiers to establish an observation post. A Japanese soldier emerged from a foxhole with a grenade. Mr. Wilhelm killed the soldier, but the grenade exploded below his left foot; his leg was amputated below the knee.
Four months later, as he recuperated in a hospital in Atlantic City, he asked for temporary permission to leave. He wanted to marry Maria de Blasio. They set the wedding for Sept. 27.
The Loyalty Board
At 32, Mr. Wilhelm was a model of American achievement: a decorated veteran who had obtained a master’s degree from Harvard after the war and landed a job as a budget analyst for the federal government.
But in the spring of 1950, a yellow envelope stamped “confidential” arrived at his home in Georgetown. The government, concerned about spying by Soviet sympathizers, had established a program in 1947 to examine civilian government employees. Its Fourth Regional Loyalty Board was investigating whether Mr. Wilhelm, who had studied the Soviet economy at Harvard, and his wife, who had been an active union member, were Communists.
The Federal Bureau of Investigation asked co-workers, professors, neighbors and family friends whether they had seen evidence that he was a traitor. On July 14, 1950, at 10:15 a.m., the couple was questioned by the Loyalty Board at a federal office building. Had he enlisted in the military with hopes of turning his gun on his superiors? Had she conspired with Communists at Time, as a prominent anti-Communist writer at the magazine, Whittaker Chambers, had alleged? Was it true that they kept recordings of Red Army songs in their home on Q Street?
Mr. Wilhelm, indignant that his patriotism could be questioned after he lost a limb in the war, was emphatic. “I consider my loyalty to the United States complete,” he said, according to a transcript of the hearing.
The panel even asked about Mrs. Wilhelm’s editing. A blunt-spoken liberal, she had been investigated before, by the F.B.I. in 1942, because of her participation in a union of federal workers that was rived by controversy over Communism; now, some of her subordinates at the Office of War Information had accused her of rewriting American propaganda to be favorable to the Communist cause.
Mrs. Wilhelm blamed the trouble on sexism, saying her male Italian-American colleagues were simply upset by her “stern” editing.
The Loyalty Board dismissed the case a month after the three-hour hearing, saying there was no compelling evidence that Mr. Wilhelm was disloyal. Still, it declared that Mr. Wilhelm and his wife had had a “sympathetic interest in Communism,” and he was denied access to classified documents.
Over the next several years, the case resurfaced as Mr. Wilhelm was considered for promotions. In 1953, an informer who knew Mr. Wilhelm at Yale told the F.B.I. that he was a security risk because of his “ultraliberal thinking.”
Not long after, the family left Washington. Mr. Wilhelm did not often speak about the investigation and never told his children about it.
Warren Wilhelm Jr.
A baby was on the way, and Maria and Warren Wilhelm were nervous.
They already had two boys — Steven and Donald — but now Mrs. Wilhelm was 43, heightening the risk of complications during pregnancy. So when she went into labor, in May 1961, they hurried from their home in Norwalk, Conn., to a hospital on the Upper East Side of Manhattan. Around 3 p.m., at Doctors Hospital, across the street from the mayor’s residence, Gracie Mansion, the baby was born. They named him for his father: Warren Wilhelm Jr. For reasons that are now unclear, they called him Bill.
Mr. Wilhelm was relieved to be back in New England, where he had grown up and gone to school. In Washington, he had tired of urban life: having a garden the size of a pool table, and waking up each morning to the screech of braking buses. In Connecticut, he had a yard full of trees, a double-ended rowboat he had built from red cedar, and the nearby Long Island Sound.
Mr. Wilhelm had become Texaco’s chief international economist, commuting by train to the Chrysler Building in Manhattan; for a time he also worked with Senator Jacob K. Javits on an effort to increase private investment in Latin America. Mrs. Wilhelm, who was working in public relations at the Italian consulate, joined him on the train, one of the few female professionals who made the morning trek.
Mr. Wilhelm often seemed to be in his own world. After work, he drank two martinis and did not say much. He buried himself in books, listened to Beethoven or Vienna waltzes, and sometimes touched up his wooden leg, adding leather veneer and honing the socket.
Though he had a slight limp and used crutches at home, he stayed fit by rowing. He relished teaching his children how to sail and challenging them to take apart and rebuild the basement furnace. He was charming, especially outside the home, but he could also be distant and he was not always faithful, his son Steven Wilhelm recalled recently.
“He was sort of there, but not there,” Steven Wilhelm, now 65 and a journalist in Seattle, said. “It never really occurred to me to confide in him.”
The Family Breaks
Mr. Wilhelm had long dreamed of returning to Cambridge and teaching at Harvard Business School. So in the mid-1960s, when he was offered a job at Arthur D. Little Inc., a consulting firm in Cambridge, he did not hesitate: The family would again be uprooted.
The Wilhelms moved into a spacious home on Brewster Street, a short walk from Harvard Square. Mrs. Wilhelm eventually found work in public relations at Polaroid, serving as an aide to the company’s founder, Edwin H. Land. Their youngest son, the future Mr. de Blasio, began kindergarten.
But before long, family life deteriorated. Mr. Wilhelm was drinking, heavily. When Mrs. Wilhelm asked her husband to seek help, he bristled, saying he could not risk blemishing his professional record, Steven Wilhelm recalled.
Bill, the youngest brother, was in some ways the least aware of the change, because he did not have much memory of his father’s healthier days. His father regaled him with stories about the camaraderie of soldiers, or played catch with him.
“I could talk to him in a way a lot of other family members couldn’t,” Mr. de Blasio recalled. “I didn’t know the previous reality.”
But those moments were rare, and in 1969, three days before their 24th anniversary, Mrs. Wilhelm filed for divorce, choosing “cruel and abusive treatment” from among a list of reasons on court documents. Mr. de Blasio, in a recent interview, said that his father had not been abusive or violent, but that Mr. Wilhelm’s behavior had taken a psychological toll on the family.
“The pain he caused people, even if he didn’t mean to, just so many people were badly affected,” Mr. de Blasio said. “I think I really was angered by that.”
Mrs. Wilhelm retained custody of Bill and Donald — Steven was grown — and kept a Chrysler. Mr. Wilhelm took the Dodge, the rowboat he had built and the family’s Wianno sailboat.
The couple agreed to a schedule for Mr. Wilhelm to spend time with the children, but it quickly broke down. Mrs. Wilhelm became “profoundly uncomfortable” when Mr. Wilhelm was around, Mr. de Blasio recalled, explaining: “She felt an obligation to bring him into the family from time to time. But she hated it. She really hated it.”
By the time Mr. de Blasio reached adolescence, he was spending less and less time with his father; when he did visit, he would try to go early in the day, when his father tended to be less drunk.
Bill physically resembled his father — he grew to be the same height, and both had broad foreheads — but he identified increasingly with his mother, her sisters and their Italian heritage, rather than his father’s German roots. In high school, classmates called him Senator Provolone, reflecting his twin penchants for politics and Italian sandwiches. When he graduated from high school, he asked that his mother’s last name be added to his diploma. He wanted to be known as Bill de Blasio-Wilhelm.
“My mother’s parents, the de Blasios, came from the boot of Italy and started a dress shop in New York,” Mr. de Blasio told The Boston Globe when he was 17. “I felt very close to them. They made me realize how it is to start in a new country with all the odds against them.”
Mr. de Blasio left Massachusetts to study at New York University and never moved back.  In 1983, he changed his name to Warren de Blasio-Wilhelm, telling a court that his mother’s Italian family had been the “single most important influence” in his life. In 2001, after winning a Brooklyn seat on the New York City Council, he formally dropped his father’s names. He was now legally Bill de Blasio, the name he had used socially and professionally for some time. He remained close to his mother, moving her to a home near his in Park Slope, where she stayed until her death in 2007.
A Solitary End
Mr. Wilhelm had returned to Connecticut by the early 1970s, unemployed, isolated and morose. His leg was bothering him. A longtime cigarette smoker, he was developing lung cancer and emphysema. He talked about the guilt he felt in abandoning the family, particularly his youngest son, Bill.
Near the end of his life, Mr. Wilhelm’s sister, Jean, paid a visit. They had once been close, but over dinner, Mr. Wilhelm sat silent, too drunk and delusional to speak, she recalled.
“He was a defeated guy who should not have been defeated,” Jean Wilhelm, who is 86 and lives in Maine, said. “He let everybody down, and he was not a person to let people down. But he knew he had.”
From time to time, his sons visited. Mr. de Blasio saw him mostly on holidays and occasionally spoke to him by phone. Sometimes, Mr. de Blasio told his father how frustrated he was by his behavior, but the conversations went nowhere.
Mr. Wilhelm’s cancer spread and his drinking intensified. He refused to seek treatment for alcoholism, and he twice attempted suicide, Steven Wilhelm said.
On July 28, 1979, he drove to the Rocky River Inn in New Milford, Conn., where he shot himself in the chest with a rifle and died.
“On the one hand, it was a shock; on the other hand, it wasn’t a shock at all,” Mr. de Blasio told WNYC-FM recently. “We knew his life was going to come to an end. We didn’t expect it to be this way.”
Mr. Wilhelm had sent suicide letters to Mr. de Blasio’s mother, his oldest brother and the local police chief. He spoke of not wanting to die in a hospital, and he reflected on the happiest times of his life, which he said had included serving in the war. He did not write to Mr. de Blasio.
Soon after his death, family members took a rowboat out on Long Island Sound. They held roses, shared memories and read poetry. Then they scattered Mr. Wilhelm’s ashes over the water, and they paddled back to shore.
Susan C. Beachy contributed research.
