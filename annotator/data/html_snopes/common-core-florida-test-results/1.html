From One Parent to Another

The role of principal has been one of the most gratifying experiences of my life. I could not have wished for a better job or more satisfying situation than the one in which I currently find myself. Every day is a new adventure; actually, a more apropos comparison would be to a roller coaster. You see, there is no level track; there are only gigantic peaks and valleys being traveled at breakneck speed. It is quite an intense ride! Where I find the level, relaxing track is at home, being a dad. This week fatherhood has opened my eyes to yet another unique perspective on what we are doing at Mason Classical Academy, and it came from my eight-year-old son.

The story goes something like this:

I arrived home at about 7:00 in the evening from the last school board meeting. My five children greeted me as usual when I entered the house. Remembering the day prior when my son, Ethan, told me about some interesting things he learned at school, I asked him if he would like to go on a run. Of course he wanted this, so we changed into some running clothes, stretched, and took off down the street. It was dark, but the temperature was perfect. Luckily, the moon was out instead of panthers and bears. On this occasion, rather than listening to music through headphones, I decided to converse with my middle child. It was most fortunate that I only had to say a few words, since I am currently too out of shape to do much talking while running. It looks like this New Year's resolution is going to be the same as the last.

"What did you learn at school today?" I asked.

That question was all it took. Not only were our legs literally off and running, but so was Ethan's mouth. He talked nonstop for two miles, and I did not have to say another word!

He told me about the Punic Wars (only the first two, because his class would learn about the third the following day); how Carthage and Rome both wanted ownership of Sicily (that is the "island on the tip of the boot if you look on a map of Europe"); about how the Romans had the superior infantry while Carthage had the stronger Navy; and he went on and on with great detail. This led to the sequel and another run two evenings later; unfortunately, I had to wait a day because of my aching legs. Come on New Year's Eve!

I began our second run with the same question and great anticipation: "What did you learn at school today?" This time Ethan took me on a journey deep into the eyeball. Some of the vocabulary words he used while describing the human eye included, and I quote, "vitreous humor, melanin, retina, rods, cones, sensitive receptor, pupil, sclera, pigment, cornea, optic nerve, and dilator muscle." Understand that we were running, and he did not have access to his notes.

After about the first mile, we passed a large mailbox. My eight-year-old then used the opportunity to explain that, "You don't actually see everything. It's really just the light bouncing off the object and going into your pupil. Pretend there is another mailbox right there, and you see it. The image goes into the optic nerve and into your brain. So, a couple days later you see another mailbox. This time it doesn't go through your optic nerve and through your brain because that mailbox image is already stored in your brain because of the cerebral cortex, or for short, you can just say the cerebrum." There may be a few flaws in his thinking, but he said this as a third-grader!

If your child is with my son in Mr. DeMasi's class, I highly encourage you to take a look at his or her composition notebook - namely the History one; Ethan's notebook is already halfway full of notes in outline form that any middle school student could be proud of.

This second run wrapped up with my son musing on how Frankenstein's monster came to life. Coincidentally, he just finished an abridged version of the book. Sadly I did not even know he read it; I thought he was still stuck reading Ready Freddy books for fun.  He also finalized the previous run's lesson - Rome won the war.  

I do not tell you this to gloat or to pretend as though my kid is more special than any other. I am merely writing from one parent to another. My motive is to convey to you that this is the kind of education all students at Mason Classical Academy are being offered. If you are a parent of an MCA student, please take a look at your child's work; discuss with them what they are being taught in school; hear their stories; continue to encourage them to do their best, to study; to care about their work; and to take advantage of this educational opportunity. I can guarantee that MCA teachers are doing their part; and if your child is doing his part, I can also guarantee that he is learning many important things.

There is, however, a serious conundrum we face as a classical, public charter school: Not only are we to use our own curriculum and offer a different choice than the regular public schools, but we are also mandated to pass the state test. This begs some questions. Which is more critical, a solid education or passing state tests? Can we accomplish delivering both? Is it ethical to focus more on state standards than a rigorous curriculum influenced by one of the most prestigious colleges in the world, Hillsdale College? Are we able to accomplish our mission with state mandates and Common Core breathing down our necks? Do we have our students practice for state tests on computers because tests are now computer-based? Are our students at an automatic disadvantage because we choose paper and pencil over keyboards and mice? These are difficult questions to answer, and ones that will be revealed only by time.

Instead of attempting to answer such questions or to be all things to all people, I am confident in sharing with you some basic truths about our school - our own "MCA Standards", if you will. Standard one, we have a strict discipline policy to which all students must adhere. Although it has taken some time, it has helped to create an atmosphere of learning and respect throughout the school. Students are not allowed to disrupt the learning of others and will be held accountable if they do. Standard two, our teachers work incredibly hard to impart knowledge onto students in the firm belief that a rigorous curriculum delivered by a respected professional will surpass any state mandate or Common Core standard that could ultimately be the make-or-break point of our school. Teachers spend most of their time in front of the classroom, while students are seated, facing the front, and learning from their teacher. Standard three, we model, encourage, and teach pillars of virtue. Character traits such as respect, integrity, citizenship, and responsibility are not only explicitly taught during assemblies, but are embedded throughout classical literature, history, and fine arts instruction. Where are those standards mandated by the state? Do those standards not "level the playing field" for all students? Are those core concepts mentioned once in the Common Core standards?

For now I trust that what has been promised to parents and students of Mason Classical Academy will be the best, most solid and ethical path to take; one that holds true to delivering a classical education in the spirit of creating exceptional American citizens who are virtuous and knowledgeable. Our curriculum surpasses that required to pass the state tests. Our realistic math ability grouping allows students to learn at their own instructional level. We do not put headphones on students and send them to a computer lab for test practice. We do not use the advanced kids to teach struggling ones. One will not see in our classrooms projects that take weeks to complete, but have little value in imparting or retaining knowledge. I have faith that a liberal arts education, traditional in nature, is the best option for children and will prepare them to tackle any challenge life will throw at them.

One day, perhaps, my son will be an eye surgeon. He will, of course, need to write Mr. DeMasi a letter and thank him for the inspiration. And perhaps even better than the idea of Ethan earning a surgeon's salary that will also take care of my wife and me in our old age, will be his letter written neatly in cursive - yet another benefit of a classical education.

David Hull, Principal
