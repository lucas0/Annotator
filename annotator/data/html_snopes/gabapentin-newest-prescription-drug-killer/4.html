Abuse of Gabapentin is Associated with Opioid Addiction

Abstract

The objective of this study was to evaluate the extent of gabapentin misuse in a dually diagnosed correctional population, and to evaluate if this abuse is specific to the presence of an opioid use disorder (OUD). Two-hundred and fifty former inmates, living in a correctional community center, who were referred for a psychiatric evaluation, were asked, through a brief written questionnaire, whether or not they used the following drugs for non-medical use in the past: opiates, gabapentin, buproprion, quetiapine, and fluoxetine. The average age of this population was 37.2 ± 12.1 years (n = 250). Sixty-four percent were male, 72 % were white, 27 % were black, and 1 % was Hispanic. All patients had substance use disorders, the large majority (72 %) to more than one substance. Fifty-eight percent had an opioid use disorder, again mostly in combination with other drugs and/or alcohol. Depressive disorders and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder were the most common psychiatric conditions. Sixty-two percent of patients reported prescription drug misuse of any kind. As expected, a high percent (55 %) reported opiate misuse. No patient reported fluoxetine misuse. Sixteen percent reported having misused gabapentin in the past. Of patients with an opioid use disorder (OUD: n = 145), 26 % endorsed gabapentin abuse while only 4 % of patients without an OUD (n = 105) endorsed the non-medical use of gabapentin. This difference was highly statistically significant (Chi square χ2 = 21.6, p < 0.0001). A growing concern about gabapentin misuse was supported in this study: 26 percent of opiate addicted patients reported illegally obtaining, overusing, or malingering problems to obtain gabapentin. This study highlights the fact that gabapentin abuse appears specific to an opioid addicted population.

Introduction

“Prescription drug abuse is the use of a medication without a prescription, in a way other than as prescribed, or for the experience or feelings elicited”: in the United States, the abuse of prescription medications has been declared a public health problem [1]. Most commonly, opioids have been implicated, although other DEA scheduled medications are misused as well, such as benzodiazepines and stimulants [2, 3, 4].

Many factors may be involved in the perpetuation of this phenomenon. All of the abused medications are mind altering, especially when used in higher doses or delivered in different ways from medically indicated administrations. They are clinically indicated for conditions for which no objective measures exist, such as pain, anxiety, or attention problems. The absence of objective parameters for the severity of these conditions make it easy to obtain higher doses of prescribed medications, simply by reporting poor effectiveness, or by reporting symptoms that do not exist. Health care factors, such as overreliance on procedures and pills, societal factors with an emphasis on quick and easy ways of doing things, and physician factors with indiscriminate prescribing of medications with an addiction potential are among other reasons for this problem.

A growing concern is the misuse and abuse of non-DEA scheduled medications, medications that are deemed not to have an addictive potential. Several papers have described abuse of gabapentin, quetiapine, and buproprion in recent years [5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10]. While these medications have specific FDA indications, they are often prescribed off label, for example, gabapentin for anxiety, buproprion for ADHD, and quetiapine for sleep. This off label prescribing tremendously increases the patient population exposed to these drugs. Also, patients with anxiety, insomnia or ADHD may have addiction co-morbidities, increasing the potential for abuse.

The true extent of abuse of non-scheduled drugs is unknown. Certain populations are clearly at higher risk: chronic pain patients, psychiatric patients and patients with addiction problems. Dual diagnosis, psychiatric and addiction, patients with a criminal background may be at highest risk because of the combination of recurrent recklessness, access to different substances in a drug-infested milieu, addiction to illicit drugs with cycles of intoxication and withdrawal, and mental health problems of depression and anxiety.

This study estimated the extent of gabapentin misuse in a correctional community center’s (CCC) population of dually diagnosed patients. Because of clinical experience and case reports, it was hypothesized that gabapentin abuse would be substantial, estimated at ten or more percent of the population. It was further hypothesized that this abuse would be specifically associated with opioid addiction. These hypotheses were based on recent studies [11, 12]. However, these studies did not investigate whether or not the association with an opioid use disorder is specific. The misuse of gabapentin in cocaine addiction has been reported as well [7].

As a matter of exploration, quetiapine and buproprion were added to the study design because data from correctional institutions indicate abuse of these substances as well [8].

Method

The community correctional center’s treatment program provides integrated treatment for dually diagnosed, mental health and addiction, residents. A comprehensive intake process includes review of prison records, a drug and alcohol evaluation, and a psychiatric assessment. As part of this intake process, patients filled out a questionnaire about prescription drug abuse. The questionnaire first explained what was meant by prescription drug abuse (not having a prescription and obtaining the drug elsewhere; having a prescription but using too much; obtaining a prescription to use the drug for other than medical reasons). The questionnaire then asked which drugs had been used for non-medical reasons in the past: opiates, gabapentin (Neurontin), fluoxetine (Prozac), buproprion (Wellbutrin), and quetiapine (Seroquel). Opiates and fluoxetine were added to obtain some measure of validity, expecting opiates to be strongly endorsed and fluoxetine not to be endorsed. All patients signed informed consent for the assessments.

Results

Two hundred and fifty patients completed the questionnaire. Three patients did not. The average age was 37.2 ± 12.1 years (n = 250). Sixty-four percent were male, 72 % were white, 27 % were black, and 1 % was Hispanic.

All patients had substance use disorders, the large majority (72 %) to more than one substance. Fifty-eight percent had an opioid use disorder, again mostly in combination with other drugs and/or alcohol.

Depressive disorders (25 %) and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (25 %) were the most common psychiatric conditions necessitating treatment, followed by adjustment disorders (22 %), anxiety disorders (18 %), post traumatic stress disorder (11 %), bipolar disorder (8 %), and schizophrenia (3 %). Many patients were identified in the record as having antisocial personality disorder, but personality disorders were not systematically evaluated in this study.

Several indices of severity of psychiatric and addiction problems were present: 66 % had past inpatient addiction treatment, 48 % had past psychiatric hospitalizations, and 25 % had hepatitis C infection.

One hundred and fifty six patients (62 %) endorsed the non-medical use of any of the five listed medications: opiates (n = 138; 55 %), gabapentin (n = 41; 16 %), quetiapine (n = 21; 8 %), buproprion (n = 15; 6 %), fluoxetine (n = 0; 0 %).

Of patients with an opioid use disorder (OUD: n = 145), 37 endorsed gabapentin abuse (26 %) while only 4 patients without an OUD (n = 105) endorsed the non-medical use of gabapentin (4 %). This difference was highly statistically significant (Chi square X2 = 21.6, p < 0.0001). Only one of 30 patients (3 %) with a cocaine use disorder not co-morbid with an opioid use disorder reported abusing gabapentin.

Only 14 patients (6 %) abused quetiapine or buproprion in isolation from gabapentin.

Discussion

The results of this study need to be put in the context of its limitations. The main limitation is the fact that the results are entirely dependent on the patients’ self report. Patients with an antisocial and addiction background cannot be assumed to be truthful, as denial and manipulation are many times part of their life style. Because of this, two medications were used as validators: opiates, because they are well-known to be abused, and fluoxetine, which is not recognized as a misused medication. Fifty-five percent of the patient population indicated opiate abuse and zero percent indicated fluoxetine abuse, giving face validity to the results of the self report assessment. Nevertheless, an underreporting of abuse of gabapentin, quetiapine and buproprion is possible.

Another limitation is the patient population: correctional dually diagnosed individuals. This population may be at highest risk for abuse of any substance, so results cannot be generalized to other populations. The results were obtained cross-sectionally and no corroborating information was available.

The self-reported prescription drug abuse was extensive in this population. Almost two thirds of the patients reported using any of the five listed medications in a manner not prescribed to them.

The focus of this study was gabapentin, a non-scheduled medication by the DEA. This medication was approved in 1993 for seizures and post-herpetic neuralgia, without an evaluation of the dependence and abuse potential in human subjects. Additional studies examined the effectiveness of this medication in withdrawal syndromes [13], bipolar disorder [14], anxiety disorders [15, 16], and insomnia [17], among others. The findings were mixed and even though no other indications were added by the FDA, the benign tolerability profile may have contributed to off label prescribing, which has been extensive in the past 20 years.

Sixteen percent in this dual diagnosis population reported misusing gabapentin. This abuse was highly related to having an opioid use disorder: about one in four patients with an OUD endorsed using gabapentin in a non-medical manner. This number is very similar to the studies by Wilens [11] and Baird [12]: in both studies, 22 % of patients reported abuse of gabapentin. The new finding in this study is the fact that gabapentin abuse almost exclusively happened in an opioid addicted population.

The reasons for this association are unclear. The study by Baird highlighted the potentiation of the opiate high with gabapentin [12]. Another possibility is the relief of anxiety symptoms associated with opioid withdrawal. A third possibility is the substitution of opioids with gabapentin. Animal data are consistent with this hypothesis [18]: gabapentin appears to increase endogenous opioid peptide levels in the amygdala, a nucleus within the addiction circuitry in the brain [19].

In summary, this study confirmed previous findings of a substantial percentage of addiction patients misusing gabapentin, but specified that this misuse is related to the existence of an opioid addiction. It behooves physicians to incorporate these findings when considering off label prescribing of gabapentin.
