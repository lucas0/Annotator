The application of power-generating fuel cell electrode materials and monitoring methods to breath alcohol sensors

Highlights

•

Power generating fuel cell electrocatalysts were tested in breath alcohol sensors (BrAS).

•

BrAS were fabricated with 20% Pt/C catalysts and their sensitivity compared to a commercial sensor.

•

20% Pt/C-based sensors used 97% less Pt compared to the commercial sensor.

•

A custom cell was developed that enabled in situ electroanalytical diagnostic measurements.

•

Diagnostics showed that sensitivity decay is due to poisoning and catalyst layer dehydration.

Abstract

A state-of-the-art fuel cell-based breath alcohol sensor (BrAS) testing station was developed and used to test electrocatalysts typically employed in power generating fuel cells for ethanol sensing. Sensor membrane-electrode assemblies (MEAs) were prepared with 20% Pt/C electrocatalysts and their sensitivity were compared to that of a commercial BrAS MEA that employs high loadings of Pt black. Using these Pt/C sensor MEAs, we were able to achieve raw sensitivity factors that approach that of the commercial sensor despite using 97% less Pt. The design of our test cell and station enabled in situ monitoring of MEA health through cyclic voltammetry (CV) and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements in an operating sensor. MEAs prepared from Pt/C display a faster rate of sensor decay compared to the commercial sensor. CV and EIS measurements confirm that this loss is due to a combination of adsorbed intermediates on the anode surface as well dehydration of the catalyst layer. Sensitivity was completely restored by simple rehydration and potential cycling.

1. Introduction

In recent years, there has been increased interest in the determination of breath compounds in medicine and clinical toxicology [1], since the analysis of volatile compounds in human breath makes it possible to observe several chemical processes in the body through a minimally invasive process. Measurements of breath alcohol concentration (BrAC) are prevalent for enforcing drinking and driving laws. While the analysis of blood alcohol concentration (BAC) is required for conviction in a court of law, routine analysis requires the use of portable and easy-to-operate devices that can be used by police agencies. The measurement of BrAC is a valid method because the alcohol concentration in alveolar air is proportional to the alcohol concentration in blood, with this relationship being 2100:1 (BAC/BrAC) [2]. This blood/breath factor has been used to calibrate breathalyzer devices, and it has become generally accepted for legal purposes to produce readings directly in BAC units [3]. It is illegal to operate a motor vehicle in Canada with a blood alcohol concentration (BAC) greater than 0.08 g/dL, which is equivalent to 80 mg of ethanol per 100 mL of blood. Similar laws are in place in most jurisdictions around the globe. Law enforcement agencies perform mandatory roadside breath alcohol tests on suspected offenders using approved devices. In recent years, devices such as these have been built into vehicles in the form of an alcohol ignition interlock, which will only allow a vehicle to be operated if the driver tests negative for alcohol in their system. A growing number of jurisdictions require alcohol interlock devices to be installed in a convicted offender’s vehicle, especially repeat offenders.

Breath alcohol measurements have been accomplished using different approaches, such as solid-state sensing [4], infrared spectrometry [4], [5], electrochemical oxidation [2], [4], biosensors [4], [6], gas chromatography [4], [7], multi-sensor array (semiconductors and infrared devices) [8] and colorimetry [9]. Of all those types of devices, electrochemical fuel-cell style sensors have received the most attention and wide spread deployment due to their portable nature allowing easy deployment for real-time measurements in the field. These devices allow in situ data analysis and avoid time-consuming sampling, transportation, and storage steps.

Electrochemical fuel-cell type breath-alcohol sensors (BrAS) get their name due to the similarity to direct alcohol fuel cells commonly studied for power generation. A schematic diagram of an electrochemical BrAS is shown in Fig. 1. The electrochemical sensor consists of two platinum electrodes sandwiched across a polymer electrolyte membrane (PEM) to form what is called the membrane-electrode assembly (MEA). A fixed volume of ethanol vapour is aspirated into the anode compartment where it is oxidized to several possible products; mainly acetaldehyde, acetic acid and possibly CO2 [10]. Protons migrate through the PEM and electrons are transported through the external circuit and into the cathode compartment where they combine with oxygen (from air) to form water. The current (or charge) transferred is measured for each injection and is proportional to the concentration/amount of ethanol introduced into anode compartment. Thus, after calibration, the sensor can determine ethanol concentrations in unknown samples, including BrAC levels in humans.

Despite the commercial success of these sensors over the last three decades, there is ample need and opportunity for improvement in the performance, durability and cost of the MEAs. While there are many examples of ethanol sensor devices in the recent literature [2], [11], very few correspond to cell configurations that are similar to those used in the commercial device. In many cases, these reports involve sensing ethanol using an alkaline electrolyte or directly sampling ethanol containing liquids (as opposed to vapor). Typical commercial sensor MEAs employ a porous polyvinyl chloride (PVC) membrane that is filled with H2SO4 (aq) [12], [13]. Each electrode contains extremely large amounts of Pt black catalyst (7–14 mg/cm2) and employ a Teflon binder, as shown in Fig. 2A. Our analysis of these MEAs demonstrated that there is a high degree of Pt agglomeration, meaning there is an extremely low electrochemically active surface area (ECSA) and very poor utilization of Pt [14]. Significant advances in MEA materials for power generation fuel cells have occurred over the last 25 years that have not been investigated or validated for applicability to these sensor formulations. For example, advances in Pt nanoparticle catalyst preparations and electrode structure optimization has resulted in increased fuel cell performance with drastically lower Pt loadings, typically 0.5 mg cm−2 or less [15]. Moreover, several reports have shown that Pt or Pd-based alloy catalysts can improve ethanol oxidation in alkaline media [16], [17], [18]. Furthermore, there have been significant advances related to in situ electrochemical diagnostic measurements that can assess MEA health in operating fuel cells. To the best of our knowledge, such materials and diagnostic methods have not been investigated nor validated for applicability to these sensor formulations.

In this paper, we have compared the sensing behavior obtained using typical power-generating MEA materials with that obtained using current commercial devices. Electrodes were prepared using two different commercially available carbon support Pt fuel cell catalyst. In order to facilitate these measurements, a sensor cell and testing station was constructed that could accommodate cell testing of different MEA materials as well as diagnostic cyclic voltammetry and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy measurements. Through these measurements, the influence of catalyst selection on sensitivity and stability was assessed.

2. Methods and materials

2.1. Materials

E-TEK 20% Pt on Vulcan XC72 carbon black was purchased from BASF and is hereafter referred to as E20. Johnson Matthey HiSpec 3000 20% Pt on carbon black was purchased from Alfa Aesar and is hereafter referred to as JM20. Both of these catalysts are composed of Pt nanoparticles dispersed onto a carbon support. Ethanol (95%) was obtained from Commercial Alcohols Inc. Isopropyl alcohol and sulfuric acid (98%) were obtained from Sigma–Aldrich. Commercial alcohol sensor cells, pumps and MEAs were donated by Alcohol Countermeasure Systems Corp.

2.2. Commercial MEA and commercial sensor cell

2.2.1. Characterization of the commercial MEA

The commercially sourced sensor unit employed MEAs manufactured by Dräger. The MEA employs porous polyvinyl chloride (PVC) membrane filled with sulfuric acid [12]. Each side of the PVC membrane is coated with a Pt-based catalyst layers. Optical microscope images were obtained using a National Digital Microscope model DM-0754 equipped with a digital camera. SEM micrographs of sensor MEAs were acquired using a JEOL JSM 6400 scanning electron microscope at a working distance of 8 mm and an accelerating voltage of 20 k eV.

The Pt loading on the Dräger MEA was determined using inductively coupled plasma-optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES). A pristine MEA was digested overnight in approximately 15 mL of aqua regia. The metals completely dissolved in aqua regia resulting in orange-coloured metal solutions and leaving the insoluble membranes and white flakes of catalyst binder. The metal solutions were filtered through Wattman 42 filter paper and then diluted in 250.0 mL volumetric flasks to obtain appropriate concentrations. The samples were further diluted in the ratio 1:50 to reduce the Pt concentration into the 0–20 mg L−1 range. A blank aqua regia solution was also filtered and diluted to the same concentration.

A series of mixed platinum and gold standard solutions in the concentration range 1–20 mg L−1 were prepared by diluting a platinum ICP standard (1000 mg L−1 Pt in 10% HCl, Assurance) or a gold ICP standard (1000 mg L−1 Au in 3% HCl, Spectropure). Calibration curves were prepared by analyzing the series of prepared standard solutions. All measurements were performed using a Varian Vista-MPX (CCD Simultaneous ICP-OES). The characteristic wavelengths investigated were 214.424 nm for Pt and 242.794 nm for Au. All measurements were performed in triplicate and the average concentration was calculated for each metal.

2.2.2. Measurements in the commercial sensor cell

The commercial test cells have a 1.9 cm2 active area. The Dräger MEAs were enclosed into the cell at the factory by the manufacturer. A photograph of the cell can be found in Fig. 2A. The alcohol source was a custom-made breath simulator vessel that contained aqueous alcohol solution maintained at 34 °C to simulate typical human expired breath [19]. The concentration of ethanol was varied between 0 and 0.18 g/dL by standard addition of 95% ethanol solution to the simulator vessel. The alcohol injection port of the cell was interfaced to the breath simulator through a DC rotary pump (Parker CTS Diaphragm Pump, Model Number E155-11-050) operating with a flow rate value of 145 mL/min. The pump was connected to a 3.2 V DC power supply and injections were controlled with electronic timers (Omega TRFP240AC). A 200 Ω load resistor was placed across the cell after which it was connected to the potentiostat to monitor current flow. To simulate a breath injection, the pump was triggered to run for 5 s via the electronic timer and the current transient was recorded. A minimum of three breath injections were made at each concentration.

2.3. Three-electrode cell measurements

The ethanol oxidation activity of E20 and JM20 was first pre-screened using 3-electrode cell measurements. This involves depositing a thin film of catalyst ink onto a glassy carbon (GC). Catalyst inks were prepared by dispersing ca. 10 mg of the catalyst powder with a 50:50 mixture of isopropyl alcohol and water (400 μL) followed by 100 μL Nafion solution to give a total volume of 500 μL. The mixture was ultrasonicated for approximately 60 min to achieve a uniform suspension. A 2 μL droplet of the well-dispersed catalyst ink was deposited onto the clean, polished, glassy carbon electrode (diameter = 3 mm, CH Instruments) using a micro syringe such that it covers the entire surface of glassy carbon. The ink droplet was allowed to dry for 30 min in air at room temperature prior to durability testing. The Pt loading of 0.11 ± 0.01 mg cm−2 was used for both E20 and JM20.

A single compartment glass cell was used for the electrochemical measurements. The catalyst-coated GC electrode acted as the working electrode (WE). A high surface area Pt wire was used as the counter electrode (CE), and an Ag/AgCl electrode was used as the reference electrode. Sulphuric acid (H2SO4, 0.5 M) was employed as the electrolyte. All electrochemical measurements were performed using a Solatron 1470E Multichannel Potentiostat and a Solartron 1260 frequency response analyzer controlled using Multistat software (Scribner Associates).

2.4. Fuel cell MEA fabrication

Fuel cell electrodes were also prepared from catalyst inks, which were comprised of a mixture of catalyst powder, Nafion solution, water and isopropyl alcohol. The mixture was thoroughly mixed for several hours and then spray deposited onto a gas diffusion layer (Toray TPGH-090, 10% wet-proofing) using an airbrush [20]. The electrodes were dried for 30 min at room temperature and then at 130 °C for an additional 30 min. The electrode had a Nafion loading of 30 wt% in all cases. Fuel cell MEAs were prepared by hot-pressing (150 kg cm−2 for 90 s at 130 °C) two 4 cm2 electrodes across a Nafion 115 membrane (Ion Power).

2.5. Measurements in UOIT sensor cell

Both in-house produced MEAs were tested in a 4-cm2 fuel cell test fixture custom made by DPM Solutions (Hebbville, NS, Canada), hereafter referred to as the UOIT cell. A photograph of this cell is shown in Fig. 2B–D. Unlike the commercial cell described above, this cell more closely resembles a power generating fuel cell design in that it has controllable gas ports on both the anode and cathode. This allows the control of the gases introduced into the cell and enables both sensor measurements as well as reconfiguration of the cell so that in situ electrochemical diagnostic measurements like CV and EIS can be performed when desired. In addition, the cell has been designed with a removable cathode plug which enables an “air breathing mode” where the cathode gas passively samples the surrounding atmosphere (as opposed to having gas flowed through the cathode). This feature enables one to subject each sensor to various temperature and humidity environments. All measurements were made here with the cathode cell plug closed. Measurements made with the cathode plug open will be described in a future publication.

2.5.1. Sensor mode measurements

For sensing measurements, the “left side” of the UOIT test cell was interfaced to the breath simulator system described above. The “right side” of the cell was exposed to ambient air. A 200 Ω load resistor was placed across the cell after which it was connected to the potentiostat to monitor current flow. To simulate a breath injection, the pump was triggered to run for 10 s via the electronic timer and the current transient was recorded. A longer pump time was used for this cell due to the larger active area. A minimum of three breath injections were made at each concentration.

2.5.2. Diagnostic mode measurements

For diagnostic mode measurements, the inlet of the “left side” of the cell was connected to humidified N2 gas feed and served as the working electrode for subsequent measurements. The “right side” of the cell was exposed to a flow of humidified H2 gas and served as both the reference and counter electrodes. CV measurements were made in a potential range of 0.0 and 1.4 V vs RHE. A minimum of 20 cycles were performed before collecting a stable CV for ECSA determination. EIS spectra were acquired for each catalyst layer over a frequency range of 100 kHz–0.1 Hz using DC bias potentials of 0.38 V (double layer region) [21], [22]. The uncompensated resistance was subtracted from all impedance spectra.

Control measurements were also made in the UOIT cell using a 4 cm2 Dräger MEA and its sensitivity was compared to that in the commercial cell.

2.6. Device development

Fig. 4shows the schematic diagram of the breath alcohol sensing station. Temperature of the breath alcohol (BrA) simulator was maintained at 34 °C by using a constant flow of warm water around the ethanol solution. The timer allows the pump to flow the ethanol vapour towards the fuel cell for a predetermined time. Finally, ethanol present in the sample was oxidized on the catalyst layer at the anode and the induced current was measured. The number of electrons (charge) generated at the anode is proportional to the ethanol concentration in the sample. The signal response is calibrated against BAC calibration standards so that the output of the sensor device is a BAC based on their total charge. Determination of ethanol in breath was performed by comparing signals obtained with the introduction of breath samples in the supporting electrolyte solution with those corresponding to the introduction of standard aqueous ethanol solutions.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Testing of commercial BrAS devices and MEAs

3.1.1. Characterization of the commercial sensor MEA

While the morphology of power generating fuel cell catalysts MEAs has been well studied in the literature [23], [24], the morphology and composition of commercial BrAS MEAs are not. As such, we have examined the composition and morphology of the commercial MEA used in this study.

Optical microscopy and SEM images obtained for the Dräger MEA are shown in Fig. 3. The entire MEA is approximately 1300 μm thick. The catalyst layers on both sides appear to be identical in thickness and morphology. Each catalyst layer is densely packed and approximately 15–20 μm thick. The PVC membrane is about 1200 μm thick. Optical microscopy of a cross section of the MEA revealed a very thin yellowish layer between the membrane and the catalyst layer, which was believed to be a gold layer.

In order to quantitatively determine the Pt and Au loadings in the sensor electrodes, ICP-OES was performed. This analysis confirmed the presence of Au in the MEA. The average Pt and Au loadings were determined to be 13.7 ± 1.1 mg cm​−2 and 0.37 ± 0.05 mg cm−2, respectively. The Pt loading is much higher than the typical catalyst loading of 2 mg cm−2 for DAFCs. The Au in the catalyst layer is not active towards either the cathodic or anodic reaction and likely originates from the deposition process used by the manufacturer. A detailed study of commercial BrAS MEAs can be found here [14].

3.1.2. Sensing behavior of the commercial MEA

The Dräger MEA (Figs. 2 A and 3) was tested in both the commercial test cell (Fig. 2A) as well as UOIT cell (Fig. 2B–D) using our breath alcohol simulator (Fig. 4). Fig. 5 shows the responses for sensor calibration tests. Based on Fig. 5A–C injections of 0.00 BAC sample did not result in any noticeable current response for all MEAs. However, by increasing the BAC in the injections, a sharp peak in current was observed corresponding to the injection of an ethanol vapour sample. The peak height and charge measured for each injection increased with increasing BAC. Fig. 5C displays plots of charge density vs. BAC obtained for the Dräger MEA in both the commercial test cell and the UOIT cell. Both cells gave highly linear calibration curves, and the slope of those lines correspond the sensitivity factor for each MEA. These sensitivity factors are compiled in Table 1. The sensitivity factors obtained for the same Dräger MEAs material in both cells are almost identical, differing by less than 2.5%. This result shows that the UOIT fuel cell breathalyzer and configuration is representative of the real-world sensing performance of commercial devices.

3.2. Power-generating MEA materials

3.2.1. Pre-screening of the E20 and JM20 electrocatalysts

In order to study alternative catalysts to replace and reduce Pt catalysts usage which is currently being used in alcohol sensors, two commercially available Pt/C catalysts have been tested in this paper. Fuel cell catalysts for power generating catalysts are typically pre-screened for their activity and electrochemical properties prior to building a cell. This is normally done by depositing a small quantity of the catalyst onto the surface of a GC electrode and studying its electrochemical properties in acidic media in the presence and absence of the fuel or oxidant [25], [26]. CVs (not shown) were obtained for each catalyst in deaerated 0.5 M H2SO4, and were used to determine an ECSA value of 70.0 and 41.5 m2/gPt for E20 and JM20, respectively. The measured values agree well with our previously published reports [21], as well as values reported by others in the literature [26], [27], [28].

The EOR activity of E20 and JM20 were investigated in the N2-saturated 1.0 M ethanol + 0.5 M H2SO4 solution. Fig. 6 shows the corresponding LSV curves. The onset potential of EOR for the JM20 was very close to E20. The peak currents for JM20 and E20 were 375 A/gPt and 300 A/gPt, respectively. It is clear that the catalytic activity of JM20 towards the EOR is higher than that of E20, although the ECSA was higher for E20. This indicated that despite their similarity, the activity of the Pt metal surfaces in JM20 was significantly higher than that of the E20 catalyst. This is important because the mass activity of Pt electrocatalysts for ethanol oxidation is a critical parameter regarding the practical application of Pt electrocatalysts. Based on these measurements, one would predict that a BrAS prepared with JM20 would be more sensitive than the one prepared with E20.

3.2.2. Ethanol sensing performance of E20 and JM20

To confirm this, MEAs were prepared using the catalysts E20 (E20BrAS) and JM20 (JM20BrAS). The Pt loadings used in each electrode were ca. 0.4 mg cm−2, which is more characteristic of loadings used in power generating fuel cells. Nafion was used as a membrane in all cases. The ethanol sensing performance of these two MEAs were compared with the existing Dräger MEA and commercial test sensor. Fig. 7 compares the sensing response and calibration curves obtained for the E20BrAS and JM20BrAS MEAs. Each MEA yielded the expected response, though the current response peaks obtained for E20BrAS were sharper than those obtained for JM20BrAS. However, integration of these peaks revealed a greater amount of charge was transferred for the JM20BrAS. The calibration curves, which are based on charge density, show that the sensitivity factor obtained for the JM20-based MEA was ca. 55% larger than that obtained for the E20-based MEA (see Table 1). This agrees with the predictions based on 3-electrode cell pre-screening.

The sensitivity and the mass normalized sensitivity of each MEA configuration are compared in Fig. 8. The highest raw sensitivity was measured with Dräger MEA. This was expected because of the substantially higher Pt loadings in the Dräger MEAs. However, the sensitivity of the in-house made MEAs compare quite well considering the lower Pt loading. In fact, while the JM20-based MEA employed a 97% lower Pt loading than the Dräger MEA, its sensitivity was only 18% lower than that obtained for the Dräger MEA. Fig. 7B displays the mass-normalized sensitivity of each MEA. Both the E20 and JM20-based MEAs have substantially larger mass-normalized sensitivity than the Dräger MEA. Thus, by using Pt/C catalysts, one could drastically decrease the amount of Pt catalyst used in these sensor, which would lead to substantial cost savings.

3.3. Long-term stability of MEAs

3.3.1. Testing sensing stability and recovery protocol

One potential reason for larger than required catalyst loadings would be to extend the durability and operating lifetime of the MEA. Degradation and/or poisoning could lower the number of available catalytic sites during cell operation. Thus, an abundance of catalyst could mitigate sensitivity loss over time.

To examine this, we have continuously operated each MEA for 7 days. Sensor responses were acquired every 30 min using a constant ethanol/water vapour concentration of 0.08 BAC. Before sensitivity measurement were performed, the cell was run in diagnostic mode and the CV and EIS response for each in-house MEA was recorded. At the end of day 7, the cell was tested again in diagnostic mode so that any changes in the CV and EIS response could be monitored.

The electrical charge for each alcohol injection was calculated and the mean value for all injections in the course of each day is reported in Fig. 9. As expected, the charge measured by the commercial MEA was higher than the MEA produced in our lab because of its much higher Pt loading. A gradual decrease in the measured charge was observed over the course of the 7 day run for all MEAs. Among the different samples that were studied, the activity of the MEA produced by E20BrAS reduced the most (30%) and the commercial sample reduced the least (4%).

At day 8, recovery cycles were applied to the JM20BrAS and E20BrAS MEAs. This recovery process involved performing CV under the flow of humidified gas, which both cleaned absorbed intermediates on the anode surface and also re-hydrated the MEA. After recovery, sensing performance was re-evaluated for both samples. The samples showed very good recovery toward sensing ethanol and the recorded charge intensity was very close to initial values (Fig. 9). This shows that it is possible to recover the activity of the sensors by cycling in a specific range of voltages.

3.3.2. Diagnostic mode CV and EIS measurements

To gain a better understanding of the changes occurring over the course of sensing measurements, CV and EIS measurements were acquired in diagnostic mode at different points during the long term test. Fig. 10 displays the CVs acquired for both the JM20BrAS and E20BrAS in diagnostic mode throughout the course of the measurement. The high resistance of the PVC membrane restricted the CV measurement on the Dräger MEA, and hence was excluded. In all cases, the typical Pt CV profile was observed. Fig. 10A shows the CVs obtained for the E20BrAS before and after ethanol sensing. Dramatic changes were observed in the CVs before and after sensing. The first cycle showed that the total charge under the Hydrogen desorption region was reduced after ethanol sensing by ca. 30% (Table 1). The decrease in ECSA may be related to the adsorption of oxidation by-products. Fig. 10B compares the CVs obtained for JM20BrAS, which showed a much smaller drop (ca. 3.4%) in ECSA over the course of 7 days. This trend correlated well with the sensing data. That is, the sample with the smallest change in ECSA remained more stable over the course of sensor testing. Furthermore, the catalysts that showed the highest activity towards ethanol oxidation was also the most stable. Further cycling of both MEAs led to complete recovery of the ECSA to their initial values. In addition to removing any adsorbed species, this recovery technique also rehydrates the MEA, which could also lead to higher ECSA measurements.

EIS has been regarded as an effective and one of the most powerful non-destructive methods for characterizing the interfacial electrical properties of electrode surfaces [22], [29], [30], [31], [32]. This method can provide information such as resistance and capacitance of an electrode. The impedance responses of E20BrAS and JM20BrAS were studied before and after sensing and are shown in Fig. 11, Fig. 12, respectively. The EIS data collected are displayed as Nyquist, capacitance, and normalized capacitance plots. A 45° Warburg-like region was observed in the high frequency region of all Nyquist plots. The projection of the Warburg length with the real impedance axis is proportional to total catalyst layer resistance (RƩ/3), where RƩ corresponds to the sum of ionic (Rionic) and electronic resistance (Relect) in the catalyst layer. For fuel cell electrodes, the electronic contributions to the impedance are negligible and consequently RƩ=Rionic [33]. The variations in ionic resistivity within the catalyst layer can be better visualized through capacitance plots [34], [35], where the series capacitance (−1/ωZ″) is plotted against the real impedance. A sharper slope in the high frequency region of the capacitance plot is an indication of higher proton conductivity within the catalyst layer and the limiting capacitance is proportional to active surface area (Platinum + carbon) [35], [36]. Comparing the ionic resistance and capacitance of the two samples before and after sensing showed that the ionic resistance was increased (Table 1) and capacitance was reduced. This kind of behaviour is characteristic of reduced ionic conductivity within the catalyst layer [37]. It is worth noting that JM20BrAS displayed a much smaller change in RΣ and capacitance than E20BrAS. Upon further exposure to the humidified H2/N2 gas, the RΣ and limiting capacitance recovered to their original values.

Finally, it can be concluded from the EIS data that the loss in performance of the two in-house MEAs over the 7 days can be attributed to a combination of both poisoning and dehydration of the membrane/ionomer [14], [36]. The Dräger MEA has a PVC based membrane, which holds liquid sulfuric acid electrolyte within its pores. This acts as a reservoir of sorts to ensure the MEA is hydrated at moderate temperatures. Therefore, dehydration of this material does not occur as readily as the in-house synthesized MEAs. Furthermore, the excessively large Pt loading in the Dräger MEA makes it more tolerant to poisoning. However, the choice of Pt/C catalyst used in the in-house prepared MEAs did influence their susceptibility to dehydration and poisoning. Modification of the hydrophilicity of the catalysts layer and/or membrane could potentially mitigate losses associated with dehydration. Likewise, the use of Pt-alloy catalysts, like those targeted for power generating direct alcohol fuel cells (e.g. Pt-Sn, Pt-Mn, Pt-Cu), could further reduce the amount of Pt used in the sensor and also improve their tolerance to poisoning.

4. Conclusions

The purpose of this study was to not only create a state-of-the-art ethanol sensing station, but also to be able to test various other factors affecting an MEA’s performance toward ethanol sensing. The designed station allows for quick and reproducible testing of simulated ethanol breath samples that are comparable to the commercial fuel cell breathalyzers. As well, we can perform in situ testing of the MEA to investigate how a material fails and whether or not these failures can be recovered.

Sensor MEAs prepared with 20% Pt/C catalysts targeted for power generating fuel cells have sensitivity that approaches that of commercial Dräger sensor MEAs, while using 97% less Pt. Sensing activity and stability correlates with EOR activity measurements in 3-electrode cell configurations. The stability of low Pt loading sensor MEAs was slightly lower than that of the Dräger MEA. CV and EIS measurements have shown that the decay in sensitivity is due to losses in ECSA and proton conductivity within the catalyst layer that occur during longer term operation. Sensitivity can be completely recovered by simple potential cycling and exposure to humidified gases. Potential improvements in sensitivity and stability in sensor MEA should arise from the use of different electrocatalysts with enhanced EOR activity and also through the use of materials that enhance water retention within the MEA.
