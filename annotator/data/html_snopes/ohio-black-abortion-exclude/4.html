Ohio Democrat Drafted Measure to Exclude Black Babies from Being Saved Under Pro-Life Law

Share
Tweet
Email
04-16-2019
-
Emily Jones
Share
Tweet
Email
 +32
Last week, Ohio Gov. Mike DeWine signed into law a bill that bans abortions as soon as the baby's heartbeat is detected, which can be as early as six weeks. 
But before it passed, a Democrat state representative from Ohio tried to make sure African-American babies were excluded from the pro-life bill and could be aborted for any reason up to 20-weeks. 
Rep. Janine Boyd proposed Amendment 0291 which would have provided an exception for black mothers to abort their babies. 
Boyd suggested protecting black children from abortion is akin to slavery.
"Black slaves were once treated like cattle and put out to stud in order to create generations of more slaves," she said during her remarks, which were broadcast on The Ohio Channel. "Our country is not far enough beyond our history to legislate as if it is."
State Rep. Derek Merrin, a Republican and chair of the committee, challenged Boyd's claims and said the law should be applied to all unborn children, regardless of their skin color. 
Ultimately, the amendment did not pass and virtually every baby with a beating heart in Ohio will be protected from abortion. The only exception is in cases when the mother's life is threatened. 
Still, pro-life activists were outraged by Boyd's amendment. 
"To reference owning humans as a defense of dismembering them is moral myopia. If it is wrong to own humans, it is also wrong to intentionally kill them," said pro-life group Created Equal president Mark Harrington, according to LifeNews.
Former Planned Parenthood executive turned pro-life advocate Abby Johnson also criticized Boyd's amendment, saying, "apparently a 38% national abortion rate in the African-American community isn't enough."
Meanwhile, pro-abortion groups continue to threaten legal action against the newly-signed pro-life bill. 
"Similar versions of this unconstitutional abortion ban stand 0-4 in federal court. Soon to be 0-5," the American Civil Liberties Union Ohio chapter tweeted.

    Thomas
    You can disagree with someone without misrepresenting what they actually say. Boyd's proposed amendment would exempt black *women* from the law. Reading her words and assuming she's speaking in good faith, the idea is not that black babies are any more appropriate to be aborted than non-black babies, but that black women, who have historically been subjected to rape, forced birth, and forced sterilization at especially high rates, warrant a special exception from this kind of state control over their bodies.

    Note for the record that I am not pro-choice and am not arguing in support of Boyd's position. I'm not pro-life either. I'm genuinely unsure what is right here. What I'm arguing for is accurately representing what Boyd, and the pro-choice movement generally, is actually valuing and thinking and saying. You don't have to agree with them, but it's good to understand what they're really thinking. It's necessary, in fact, if you ever want to have an actual discussion and maybe even change some minds, to understand where people really are. I find it very unlikely that a lot of pro-choice people are just thrilled to abort as many children as possible. It's much more likely that, as they say, they are concerned about a person's (in this case, a woman's) right to decide what happens to her own body. That's why they identify as pro-*choice*. The value they are focusing on is a person's right to choose what happens to their body. Much like the pro-*life* side is focused on the value of preserving life. Both of these are good things. But for the pro-choice side to insist on calling others anti-choice is inaccurate; it doesn't reflect the actual thoughts or values of many pro-life people. In the same way, the pro-life side insisting on calling other anti-life is similarly inaccurate; it simply misses the value that these people are actually motivated by.

    If you believe that the fetus/baby/child is a person with a right to life from the moment of conception, fantastic, keep on believing that. But understand that "Does this fetus/child have a right to life or not" is not the whole conversation. Pro-choice people believe that it is also important to ask "Does this woman have the right to decide what happens to her own body?". I know that the pro-life response is "It's not just her body, she's deciding for someone else's body too," and I feel the weight of that point. I'm just saying - if you want to engage with pro-choice people, you don't have to drop your focus on the child's right to life, but you need also to address their very real concerns about a person's right to decide what happens to their own body.  Because even if the child has a right to life from conception, it is still true that continuing a pregnancy or terminating it does affect the woman's own body, and we do generally believe people should be able to decide what happens to their own body. The fact that with abortion this choice affects both the person's own body and, some reasonably believe, someone else's body too is part of why it's such a uniquely difficult conversation. But enter the actual conversation. You can still be as pro-life as ever; it can be as simple as acknowledging that both life and a person's right to make their own healthcare decisions are important values, but that life outweighs choice - done, simple pro-life case that still takes into account what the other side is actually saying. Entering the conversation this way, with an interest in hearing and responding to what the other side is actually saying, is more accurate, more honest, more respectful, more helpful in clarifying our own thinking, and ultimately more effective in standing a chance at changing anyone's mind.

    It is possible to be neither pro-life nor pro-choice. There is such a thing as being unsure. Here's how I have gotten to my place of uncertainty. I believe that people in general have a right to life, but I'm unsure whether and how that applies to blastocysts, embryos, fetuses, humans in the earliest stages of development. A blastocyst/embryo/fetus is like you and me from day 1 in sharing human DNA and, as it develops, sharing more things like a brain and consciousness and the ability to experience. It's unlike you and me in plenty of ways too: it's unclear when and how consciousness develops, but a two-week-old blastocyst with no nervous system clearly lacks it. I have a hard time viewing a bundle of cells (with or without human DNA) with no ability to experience as a moral person in the same way I believe you and I are moral persons with rights, someone you can be said to have wronged. I'm simply not sure when and how in its development (of consciousness, mainly) a blastocyst/embryo/fetus/child starts to have what kinds of rights. I don't know. I am not a Christian, so my belief in a right to life is not based on humans' being created in the image of God or having souls.

    I also believe that people have a general right to be able to do what they want with their own body. This right is limited: I don't have an absolute right to use my body to harm others. If I hold my hand over someone else's mouth to smother them, I don't have the right to do that simply because it's my hand. It's *also* someone else being affected, and that changes things. So in the case of abortion, if the embryo lacks a right to life and/or is not a moral person, then the woman's right to control her own body entails the permissibility of abortion: it's her body and affects only her. If the embryo does have a right to life, then it's more complicated. Maybe that right trumps the woman's right to control her own body. Maybe it doesn't, maybe her right to bodily autonomy trumps the embryo's right to life in some or all cases. This happens, where we see a general right that can nevertheless be outweighed by another right when they conflict. I don't know.

    You can disagree with me and maintain that the only correct way to understand this is within a Christian framework that sees the right to life as absolute from day 1 in virtue of being created in God's image with a soul and so on. But I'm not working in that framework. So please at least understand how it is possible---*possible*--to be unsure on this issue. I have dug into this for years and I remain genuinely unsure. I myself am not in charge of either performing abortions or stopping anyone from having them, so I have the luxury of being able to remain unsure. When it comes to law and policy and practice, we as a society have to have some response, and you're right that allowing it is effectively being pro-choice. But at the individual personal level it is possible to be unsure on this issue.
