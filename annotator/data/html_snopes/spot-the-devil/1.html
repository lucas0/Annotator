Halls of Justice: A Weekly Look
Inside the Justice Department

By Beverley Lumpkin


W A S H I N G T O N, Feb. 2 — We've got mail!

STORY HIGHLIGHTS
Oh, Grow Up!  People Power  Thank You, Press  Clear Parallel  Ironic Draping   The 'Art Police' 



In last week's column, "Minnie Lou" was the just one of the topics, but it set off a bit of a ruckus — here's a little background for any gentle readers who missed it.

The Great Hall — a two-story room used for department events and ceremonies — is home to two enormous and stylized but largely naked aluminum statues. On the left, the female figure represents the Spirit of Justice; the male on the right is the Majesty of Law (early editions of last week's column gave him the wrong name because of an error on the Justice Department Web site). The male is clad in only a cloth draped over his essential parts; the female wears a sort of toga-style garment, but one breast is entirely exposed. She's been fondly referred to for years, by at least some, as "Minnie Lou."

These statues no longer exist in all their glory. Minnie Lou and her mate now can only be imagined. Draperies, ordered by someone in Attorney General John Ashcroft's office, were installed two weeks ago. Well, Minnie certainly had readers reacting — here are some of their e-mails.

Oh, Grow Up!

Will the government ever grow up? Covering the breast of a statue is inane. I demand a refund of taxpayer money — if the people working there don't like the statues, they can look in another direction. Also if the attorney general doesn't want to be photographed in front of them, he shouldn't stand in front of them! Duh! Thanks so much for your column. I love it.
— Jean Allsworth

People Power

Where does John Ashcroft have the right to cover statues that belong to the people of the United States? Will he now have them destroyed because they offend his perverted sense of morality? Michelangelo was not ashamed to adorn the Sistine Chapel with God's greatest creation, the human body. Unfortunately Ashcroft demeans the Justice Department with his petty mind set of what he considers should or should not be seen by the public. He does not own the statues at the Justice Department, the people do!
— Florence Haverland

Thank You, Press

In regards to your story on the draperies ordered for the Spirit of Justice statue, I think that perhaps instead of Ashcroft becoming angry and covering the statues, he should perhaps set up the meetings in such a way that it would be impossible for the press to get him in the picture with the statue.

I think the press should be ashamed of themselves for using such an inspiring and beautiful statue in such an insulting and demoralizing way. When one wonders why the nation is having trouble with our youth being disrespectful of the sexes, we can in most cases thank the press for it.
— Rebecca J. Roderick

Clear Parallel

Justice may not be blind under the watchful eye of current Attorney General John Ashcroft. This article highlights clearly the religious prudity of Ashcroft. As a retired educator with art training, I am well acquainted with the shrouds and prohibitions that religion has imposed on "thinking" — including "art" — throughout history.

There is a clear parallel between Ashcroft's zealous religious draping of the sculptures and the Taliban's zealous religious demolition of major sculptures of Buddha in Afghanistan. It is very embarrassing that the bureaucrat in charge of enforcing our Nation's laws should be religiously intolerant in the same manner as the Taliban. Thank you for the information.
— Roger Gaither

Ironic Draping

I believe the Taliban felt the same way about the human body, specifically that the female body be totally covered up in a burqa. I am surprised that Mr. Ashcroft didn't just purchase a burqa for "Minnie Lou".

My second comment, is that I find it quite symbolic that Mr. Ashcroft is draping two statues that are symbols of Justice.

I am finding this administration more and more like the McCarthy era — "You are either with us or you are one of them" scares the hell out of me.

Also, I am reminded of the Nazi's and how they destroyed thousands of great works of art because it was considered decadent. Censorship is taking place in the Great Halls of Justice and yet, no one objects or even complains. And the tax-payers are paying for the cost of these "garments."

Thank you for the article.
— Asheigh

The 'Art Police'

Thank you for keeping abreast of the situation with the statues at justice department. It is so reassuring to know Ashcroft is looking out for my well-being. Does this mean I can lie with impunity but that the "Art police" will be watching my art viewing habits? Maybe that's really why they are hiring all those new FBI agents.
— john l. power

New Agent Bonanza

Where's Reno and 'Minnie?'

I don't ever remember press photos of Janet Reno with Minnie Lou ... was it considered distasteful and demeaning to her and her office to publish such a thing and is it now fair game because of what you want to create as 'news' for your leftist agenda ... If you have a pic of Janet and Minnie Lou, please send it... I'd be interested.
— Dave Smith

Beverley Lumpkin has covered the Justice Department for 16 years for ABCNEWS. Halls of Justice appears every Saturday.
