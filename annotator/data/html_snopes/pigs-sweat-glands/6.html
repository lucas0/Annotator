Review of wallowing in pigs: Description of the behaviour and its motivational basis

Abstract

Wallowing, i.e. coating the body surface with mud, is a natural behaviour of pigs, commonly observed in feral pigs and wild boar, but rarely provided for in current housing systems for domestic pigs. Furthermore, in welfare science the subject has not been receiving much attention.

This paper reviews wallowing in pigs and related species. The behaviour is described and its motivational basis is examined. Underlying the review was a literature search for scientific citations. In total 48 papers were identified containing citations about wallowing behaviour in pigs and wild boar, and 12 papers contained citations about wallowing in related species.

Wallowing is observed in many related species including rhino's, elephants, bovids (e.g. American bison) and deer. Pigs also share several taxonomic characteristics with water-loving mammals such as water buffalo's, hippo's and whales.

The common perception is that pigs wallow mainly for cooling, sunburn protection and the removal of ecto-parasites. Little scientific evidence exists for other functions than thermoregulation. Pigs lack functional sweat glands and wallowing in mud is an effective behavioural control mechanism in pigs to prevent hyperthermia. Wallowing, however, may also serve other functions, e.g. in scent-marking and sexual behaviour. In addition, wallowing in pigs, like dustbathing in poultry, may be indicative of positive welfare and, perhaps, the performance of the behaviour is ‘hardwired’ and rewarding in itself. If so, wallowing could be an important element of a good life in pigs.

1. Introduction

Wallowing, i.e. coating the body surface with mud or a mud-like substance, seems to be a natural behaviour for pigs that is relatively species-specific, at least when compared to other domesticated species. It is possible that this behaviour is intrinsically motivated (‘hardwired’) and that it is important for pig welfare. Nevertheless, it has drawn relatively little attention in animal welfare research.

In a report from the Dutch Animal Welfare Council on the natural behaviour of pigs wallowing was mentioned only briefly (RDA, 2006), and three general papers on natural behaviour in farm animals did not mention wallowing at all (Jensen and Toates, 1993, Spinka, 2006, Bracke and Hopster, 2006).

For practical welfare evaluation various formulations of the Five Freedoms have been proposed (e.g. Brambell, 1965, FAWC, 1992, FAWC, 2009, LNV, 2007). One Freedom generally seems to refer to positive welfare as the animals’ ability to express normal or natural behaviour. Prime examples of such behaviours include various foraging behaviours (such as rooting in pigs and scratching in poultry), nestbuilding in sows and laying hens, and dustbathing in poultry (Jensen and Toates, 1993, Anonymous, 2001, Spinka, 2006). It is not clear, however, whether wallowing would classify as a behaviour that pigs should have the freedom to perform, and recent designs of future housing systems, allegedly based on animal welfare needs, did not seem to include wallowing facilities for pigs (De Greef et al., 2003, Van Eijk et al., 2010a, Van Eijk et al., 2010b, Van der Peet et al., 2010).

Animal welfare is a hot topic. To date, the main concern has been animal suffering in farming practices, where animals seem to be used as ‘machines’ (Harrison, 1964). There is, however, a gradual shift to address positive aspects of welfare. This is partly related to the debate being increasingly dominated by market considerations, generating the need to communicate positively about animal welfare. In research methods are being developed to measure positive welfare (Boissy et al., 2007, Bracke, 2007), and in the future, positive welfare, i.e. ‘the good life’ is expected to receive increased attention (FAWC, 2009). Perhaps, wallowing constitutes an element of ‘the good life’ of pigs, as seems to be the case for dust bathing in poultry (cf Widowski and Duncan, 2000, Olsson and Keeling, 2005).

Addressing this issue is relevant from different perspectives. Firstly, from the perspective of applied ethology wallowing may provide an interesting case because it contains different aspects of welfare, related to natural behaviour on the one hand and basic physiological needs (such as thermal comfort and parasitism) on the other hand. These aspects have received variable emphasis in welfare science (Bracke et al., 2008). In particular, wallowing in pigs may be interesting in view of the traditional study of natural behaviour (Lorenz, 1958). While early applied ethologists recognised the importance of studying animals under (semi-)natural conditions (e.g. Stolba and Wood-Gush, 1989), at present most welfare research is conducted under more controlled conditions, rarely taking a wider evolutionary perspective. To accommodate this, the present review was supplemented with information about feral pigs and wild boars, and about related (mammalian) species. Such an extended review may relate well to new trends concerning the scientific study of positive aspects of welfare, and perhaps also to the development of new ways of studying positive welfare more objectively. Secondly, animal scientists may take an interest, as their views have changed over time. Whereas previously (before the onset of intensive pig farming) wallowing was recognised as a normal part of pig housing, authors of more recent textbooks almost seem to have ‘forgotten’ that wallowing may be an important element in the pig's behavioural repertoire (Sambraus, 1981). Finally, wallowing is an important element in the public perception of pig welfare (Lassen et al., 2006) and progressive farmers may need to consider providing pools or showers for pigs to accommodate such a ‘demand’.

This paper focuses on a main issue in applied ethology: to identify the motivational basis for wallowing in pigs. The paper is based on a literature search (conducted in May 2010), primarily using Google Scholar and Scopus as a first entry with the keywords ‘wallow’, ‘mud’, ‘pigs’, ‘hogs’ and ‘swine’. From obtained references citations about wallowing in pigs and related species were extracted (see Bracke, 2010 for original formulations). Citations were retrieved from 66 references in total. Of these 48 papers contained citations about wallowing behaviour in pigs and wild boar, and 12 references concerned wallowing in related species. The citations were subsequently used for a more formalised discussion on welfare in another paper (Bracke, in press). That paper formulates criteria for an ideal mud pool, systematically assesses the importance of wallowing for pig welfare based on semantic-modelling principles (Bracke et al., 2002, Bracke et al., 2008) and provides an ethical paragraph on the subject of ‘ethical room for manoeuvre’ with respect to wallowing in pigs. The objectives of the present paper are to present a systematic inventory of descriptions of wallowing in pigs and related species, and to identify its motivational bases, including in particular the possibility that the performance of wallowing behaviour itself may be pleasurable for pigs.

2. Description of wallowing behaviour

2.1. Wallowing in domestic pigs

Pigs will root to make wallows (Horrell et al., 2001) containing a sticky and muddy substance (Van Putten, 2000). When pigs enter a wallow, they normally dig and root in the mud before entering with the forebody first. They then wriggle the body back and forth so all of the body surface may be covered with mud (Jensen, 2002). They do not always, however, immerse themselves completely in mud. McGlone (1999) described how this may depend on the environmental temperature. He stated that when the temperature is slightly warm (above freezing), pigs will stand in the cool water. When the temperature rises, they will lie with their udders in the cool water and mud. When it gets warmer still, they will coat their skin with mud and roll from time to time from one side to the other allowing the mud to evaporate. Sometimes on hot days, if wallows are deep enough, only the sow's snout and head will be visible above the water. When they leave the wallow on very hot days, if it is designed well, sows can be observed with a heavy coat of mud on their belly and flanks (McGlone, 1999). Typically 50–75% of the body will be covered with mud on hot days (McGlone, 1999).

Dellmeier and Friend (1991) distinguished between active and passive wallowing. During passive wallowing the pigs stand or lie in water or mud; during active wallowing they root below the surface, shake their heads in the water, or lie down and roll, stretch and kick in the water.

Sambraus (1981) observed sows wallowing in large mud pools with a diameter of 6 m during summer time in Illinois (USA). Under these conditions the sows wallowed on average twice a day, staying in the wallow for an average of 3 h in the morning and 0.5 h in the afternoon. Under more confined conditions, however, pigs may wallow more frequently and for shorter periods of time (e.g. 1–15 times a day for 1–9 min each, Huynh et al., 2006). In Sambraus’ study (1981) the sows mainly wallowed near the border of the pool where the mud and water was not reaching higher than the elbow joint. The middle of the pool was used only when the mud was no longer soft or when the border was occupied by other sows. The snout was used to secure solid ground. Lying in water was not adequate. Sows carefully wallowed in mud. The head was immersed in mud over the eyes, but Sambraus (1981) reported that the ear-openings were always kept dry. Some sows also avoided getting with their eyes under the surface. The sows’ lying positions in the wallow depended on depth. At low depth they lay on their sides. In deeper mud they adopted a half sideway or sternal lying position. As a result, the back was generally kept free of mud.

Wallowing has a social dimension. McGlone (1999) stated that most sows wallow in groups. Pigs tend to spread out in the wallow, usually without facing one another. Some individual pigs, however, prefer to wallow alone or in a distant part of the wallow (McGlone, 1999). Sambraus (1981) reported that sometimes a dominant sow in the wallow would get up to chase away a low-ranked animal when it arrived at the wallow. He never observed, however, that a dominant sow arriving at the pool would drive off a weaker animal to take its place.

In addition to its social dimension, wallowing has been related to various other behavioural/motivational systems, including feeding, drinking, exploration, elimination, resting, sexual behaviour, sickness and body care.

Feeding behaviour has been identified both as a pre-wallow (e.g. Rose and Williams, 1983) and as a post-wallow behaviour (e.g. Fraser, 1970a). Fraser (1970b) observed that free ranging pigs, covered in mud, frequently practiced wallowing intermittently with grazing during lengthy spells of elevated temperatures. Pigs in Papua New Guinea increased wallowing in the afternoon when earthworm availability (i.e. food items) had decreased. Before wallowing they showed increased exploratory “nuzzling”, where the pig grazes its snout over the surface of the soil giving anything that arouses its curiosity superficial attention, e.g. sticks and stones (Rose and Williams, 1983). Stolba and Wood-Gush (1989) stated that rooting in boggy areas was deeper than in other areas, indicating a connection between soil moisture and rooting behaviour (see also Van der Mheen and Spoolder, 2005).

Pigs may drink from the wallow water (Dellmeier and Friend, 1991, McGlone, 1999, Van Putten, 2000).

Elimination (urination and defecation) often occurs in draughty, damp and open areas (Fritschen, 1975), away from the feeding and lying areas (Olsen et al., 2001), and in association with drinking behaviour (Hacker et al., 1994). Olsen et al. (2001) observed that pigs deposited approximately 50% of their dung in the wallow, but this was not in accordance with Sambraus (1981). The latter reported that sows often eliminated in conjunction to wallowing, but only incidentally did a sow drop faeces or urine in the wallow. Sows generally urinated immediately before entering the wallow, and sows also frequently urinated and defecated after wallowing.

The most frequently reported post-wallow behaviours relate to body care. Sambraus (1981) noted that sows frequently shook themselves, often when still standing or sitting in the wallow. Sows only actively shake their heads and necks, because pigs are not able to actively shake the rump. This results in mud being removed only from the front part of the animal. Sows also moved to a scratching post after wallowing, to scratch the body sides from neck till hind legs. Generally, they only scratched themselves when the mud had dried and hence was removed more easily (Sambraus, 1981). For scratching, pigs have been reported to use trees, bushes and posts (Stegeman, 1938, Rose and Williams, 1983, Van Putten, 2000, Gegner, 2001), and to rub their hindquarters by sitting down and moving forward and backward using stones or rocks (Van Putten, 1978, Van Putten, 2000, Jensen, 2002).

Pigs wallow and root-wallow year-round, even when it is below freezing, but they show increased wallowing behaviour when it gets warmer (John McGlone, personal communication). Sambraus (1981) observed sows going through the wallow to take the shortest route to the drinker, feeding place or somewhere else, at temperatures near 0 °C and when the pool was covered with thin ice, without hesitation. This author, however, never observed wallowing below 12 °C and only once at 12 °C, and most wallowing below 20 °C was done after feeding. In general Sambraus (1981) noted that sows wallowed especially after feeding, when in heat and when suffering from general illness. Sows in heat generally did so only for short periods of time.

With regards to ambient temperature, adult pigs will start to show wallowing behaviour as of about 17–21 °C (Jensen, 2002, Sambraus, 1981, Heitman et al., 1962, Stolba and Wood-Gush, 1989). Comparing sows in various reproductive stages Buckner et al. (1998) found that in summer prefarrowing sows spent a large proportion of their resting time oudoors in the wallow and that these sows were also occasionally observed wallowing in winter.

In growing pigs, rooting in the drinker/wallowing area increased at about 20 °C, indicating a shift from ‘foraging rooting’ to ‘comfort rooting’, digging out the wallowing area (Andresen and Redbo, 1999). As ambient temperatures rise pigs reduce rooting and increase wallowing behaviour (Rachuonyo, 2001). Behaviour towards wallow water correlated positively with temperature (Olsen, 2000). This involves rooting, chewing, making bubbles and having the snout passively in the wallow water (Olsen et al., 2001). In particular, cooling the snout by making bubbles in wallow water correlated positively with both ambient temperature and sunshine levels, and it correlated negatively with humidity (Olsen et al., 2001). These authors observed that growing/fattening pigs showed wallowing behaviour over the whole range of temperatures in their study (from −4 to 24 °C, with temperatures exceeding 18 °C in only 5% of observations). Wallowing duration, however, increased when temperatures exceeded 15 °C (Olsen et al., 2001). Lying in the wallow correlated positively with temperature, and temperature also affected the number of lying-posture changes in the wallows (Olsen et al., 2001). McGlone (1999) stated that during very hot weather pigs may stay in the wallow for very long periods of time, leaving only for necessary activities such as feeding and nursing.

2.2. Wallowing in feral pigs and wild boar

Across the world pigs live in a wide range of habitats, but these habitats always have water sources allowing pigs to wallow (Frädrich, 1969). Movement of feral pigs may involve long travels between food and water, and water may be a driving force especially for thermoregulation through wallowing (Diong, 1982). When wallows dry out pigs will migrate to other areas (Hörning et al., 1999).

Natural wallows are depressions in mud, often filled with (muddy) water. They are created by the loafing, rolling and rooting of several animals. Initiating rooting movements include moving solid earth from the edges into to wallow and loosening the bottom of the wallow (Hörning et al., 1999). Simply lying in water does not seem to satisfy the pigs (Hörning et al., 1999). The wallowing may be brief, just long enough to plaster the skin with mud as protection against flies, to get rid of ectoparasites, or much longer for thermoregulation during the hot dry season (Graves, 1984). Wallows are often visited more than twice/day during the warm months (Campbell and Long, 2009). If a wallow has been used considerably, the surrounding vegetation usually is heavily trampled and smeared with mud. Wallows were found chiefly near the upper ends of the higher coves, in shaded, cool and wet places. Some were quite large and evidently used by several animals at a time, while others were just large enough for one individual (Stegeman, 1938). Wallows have also been found at the edges of flooded areas, muddy beds of canals and marshes, muddy depressions on trails after rain, irrigated fields and everywhere along the animals’ paths during the wet seasons. Dardaillon (1986) noted that wild boar in the Camargue area used marshes the most frequently throughout the year since all behaviours such as bedding, farrowing, rooting, wallowing and rubbing, could be performed there. Wallowing was mostly performed in marshes and on the edges of flooded areas (Dardaillon, 1986). Near flooded areas wallows were temporary, with only few instances where the same wallows had been used repeatedly within a period of a few weeks. This contrasts with the behaviour of pigs living in forests, where the animals often use the same wallows almost daily (Dardaillon, 1986). In the Great Smoky Mountains wallows had an average size of 1.3 m × 1.0 m and 25 cm deep, which involved the removal of ∼0.325 m3 of sediment (Belden and Pelton, 1976, cited from Butler, 1995).

Wild pigs have several anatomical adaptations related to wallowing. These include a relatively thick skin that has relatively few subaqueous and apocrine (sweat) glands, a propensity to store fat subcutaneously and a hair cover with bristle hairs without underwool (Frädrich, 1969). In addition, pigs have a hoof structure (a second and fifth digit, i.e. hind claws) that provide additional carrying capacity on soft, moist surfaces (Frädrich, 1969). This allows pigs to walk more easily on muddy soils in or near wallows.

Wallowing in feral pigs has also been associated with body care, e.g. rubbing to remove excess or dried mud, hair and ectoparasites (Campbell and Long, 2009). For rubbing wild boars usually choose pitch pines less than 15 cm in diameter along trails and near wallowing places. Such trees are usually muddied from near the ground (10–20 cm) to a height about equalling that of the animals (Stegeman, 1938). Also fallen logs, utility posts and rocks may be used as rubbing substrates. Campbell and Long (2009) reported a preference for rubbing on creosote-treated posts. In the Camargue wild boars use elm and tamarisk as rubbing trees for which they sometimes have to travel several hundred meters from the wallowing area (Dardaillon, 1986). Rubbing related to wallowing may also involve chemical marking. For this a pig may rub its perineal region against the ground in an anogenital rub and apply markings with the glands of the antorbital region of the head (Graves, 1984). During brief wallows, traces of body secretions are often left by means of a “side rub” in which the pig lies down and extends and flexes itself on alternate sides.

Wild pigs use wallows in all seasons, even when it is cold(er). During the dry season, feral pigs were found at waterholes at midday, where they wallowed before returning to the shade (Graves, 1984). Stolba and Wood-Gush (1989) reported that pigs in a semi-natural environment often used ten rather exposed sites near a stream or boggy area for wallowing. During hot weather conditions wild pigs prefer using mud pools which are shaded (Graves, 1984). They reduce activity during the hot parts of the day and heavily depend on wallowing for thermoregulation (Baber and Coblentz, 1986). Due to hunting pressure European wild boar may be nocturnal, feeding at night and resting during the day in beds or wallows, often on the sides of ridges (Graves, 1984). Warty pigs prefer to wallow especially on days with strong direct sunshine, but European wild boar can also be found wallowing in mud during rain and cold weather (Frädrich, 1967). Feral pigs in Australia wallow in all habitats where water is available and both during hot and cold weather (Dexter, 1998). During the breeding season male wild boar will wallow in autumn and winter in Europe, presumably for cooling purposes (Hörning et al., 1999). Wild boar will also wallow after fleeing (Dathe, 1971, cited from Hörning et al., 1999). Hunters in the Cherokee National Forest reported that wild pigs would often break the ice to wallow and that, when pursued during the winter, they frequently wallow in each stream that they cross (Stegeman, 1938).

2.3. Evolutionary perspective on wallowing in pigs

When the pigs’ motivation for wallowing is traced back in its evolutionary history one may look for pool-like existences in the past, relationships between pigs and other water-loving animals, and compare wallowing(-like) behaviours across species.

Life seems to have started in a ‘mud pool’, or ‘earth-soup’ (Wills and Bada, 2000). The ‘higher’ vertebrates evolved from fish by moving out of the water. Amphibians still move back to pools for breeding. Reptiles and birds have solved this problem by laying eggs protected by a shell. Some reptiles like crocodiles and giant tortoises are fond of wallowing in mud (Hornaday, 1875, Hatt, 2008). With some imagination the uterine environment in which mammal foetuses, including those of pigs, develop before they are born may be considered to be pool-like.

Pigs are relatively primitive Artiodactyla (even-hoofed ungulates), anatomically characterized by their simple stomachs, many low-crowned, bunodont teeth and a less-advanced unguligrade limb structure. They share these properties with peccaries and hippopotamuses (Powell, 2003). Although phylogenetic relations are disputed, pigs have been classified with hippo's in the suborder of Suiformes (pig-like animals). Hippo's are well known for their attraction to water. Even whales, perhaps the most ‘water-loving’ mammals, apparently evolved from primitive ungulate stock (Eltringham, 2003).

Pigs are medium-sized ungulates (Fernández-Llario, 2005), which appear to have evolved from a larger ancestor, perhaps the Entelodont or Giant Pig, which was about 1.5 m high and 2.4 m long (Bennett, 1970, however see also Powell, 2003). According to Eltringham (2003) ungulates evolved from anthracotherids which resembled large pigs and were probably semi-aquatic, living about 25 million years ago. Modern pigs may have inherited a motivation to wallow from their relatively large ancestor, because a large body has more difficulty losing heat due to its relatively low surface to body-mass ratio.

Reeves (2003) traced cetacean ancestry back more than 50 million years, where they arose from the Mesonychidae, an extinct family of primitive terrestrial mammals that were carrion feeders (like pigs) with large heads, powerful jaws, and five-toed feet with hoof-like claws. The transition from a wholly terrestrial to an amphibious existence is believed to have taken place initially in the Tethys Sea, a large, shallow, near-tropical seaway (Reeves, 2003). An ‘innate’ preference for wallowing-like behaviour may thus have been instrumental in the evolution of whales. In fact a wallowing-type of behaviour has been reported from oceanic environments, where beluga whales use gravel beds in shallow lagoons to rub off loose epidermis (Frost et al., 1993, cited from Butler, 1995), mixing and stirring the bottom gravels and creating muddy plumes of water.

Pigs share several characteristics related to wallowing with hippo's, whales and water buffalos. Unlike most other mammals, pigs, whales and hippo's have a limited hair cover and a limited ability to sweat. Water buffalos can sweat, but poorly, and their skin has one-sixth the density of sweat glands that cattle skin has (Marai and Haeeb, 2010). Whales lack sweat glands (Ridgway, 1972) and so do hippo's, but hippo's have large skin glands that secrete a viscous liquid that turns pink on exposure to air, is thought to have antiseptic properties and may act as a sunscreen (Eltringham, 2003). For this the pig uses mud.

Wallowing in either mud or dust is a common activity among mammals (Butler, 1995), birds and reptiles, but wallowing in mud is especially notable among pigs and piglike animals (Sowls, 1984), as well as among large megaherbivores with relatively hairless skin, such as elephants, rhino's, hippo's, water buffalos and warthogs (Owen-Smith, 1988, cited from Butler, 1995). Rhino's and tapirs are particularly fond of wallowing in mud (Hubback, 1939, Prothero and Schoch, 2002). Hubback (1939), for example, observed rhino's walking and swimming in rivers but never saw any lying down there. Instead the rhino's invariably lay down in their wallows where, like pigs, they actively modify the consistency of the mud to accommodate their requirements (presumably for thermoregulation and protection from biting insects).

Other animals such as elephant seals may also spend a considerable effort in wallowing behaviour (Hall and Williams, 1981). North American ungulates such as the moose and the American elk, use their forehooves and antlers to excavate large wallow pits during the autumn rutting season. Male moose produce new wallows each season with a typical size of about 1.2 m long, 60–90 cm wide and 5–7 cm deep (Butler, 1995). The American bison created extensive wallows across the North American prairie, wallowing in both mud and dust (Butler, 1995). American bison and moose urinate on the ground and then briefly roll in it. The hog deer urinates on a spot before bedding (Coblentz, 1976). Mixing urine with soil or mud is common in cervids in temperate regions because mud is suitable to strengthen and preserve the odour for long periods (Miura, 1985).

Animals show wallowing-type behaviours for many different reasons. McMillan et al. (2000) stated that wallowing by the domestic pig and Great Indian Rhinoceros is a thermoregulatory behaviour (Pal and Bhattacharyya, 1986, Vestergaard and Bjerg, 1996); wallowing by wapiti and male European bison during the rut is a male–male conflict social behaviour (Struhsaker, 1967, Geist, 1982, Cabon-Raczynska et al., 1987); wallowing by male European bison at other times of the year and by females is a grooming behaviour (Cabon-Raczynska et al., 1987) and wallowing by the tamaraw may be for relief from biting insects (Momongan and Walde, 1993). McMillan et al. (2000) investigated possible reasons for wallowing in American bison including grooming associated with moulting, male-male interaction (rutting), group cohesion, play, relief from biting insects, reduction of ectoparasite load (ticks and lice) and thermoregulation. They found that wallowing in American bison was associated most clearly with relief from biting insects, but other explanations could not be fully excluded. Relief from biting insects was also reported for water buffalo (Tulloch, 1988, Nalin, 1996).

Wallowing, defined in this paper as covering the body in mud or a mud-like substance, may also be related to scent-rubbing. This is a related and widespread behaviour in mammals rubbing their bodies vigorously in substances, many strongsmelling and some artificial, such as rotting meat, intestinal contents and engine oil. Gosling and McKay (1990) suggested that scent-rubbing is involved in status advertisement. The behaviour is well known in carnivores, where individuals typically collapse their forelegs then push forward with their hind legs, rubbing their face, neck and back onto the odour source. As a result, the animal's coat becomes impregnated with the odour of these substances. Essentially the same process happens in wallowing by ungulates where the odour of wet soil and decaying organic matter is transferred to the animal's body. In these cases scent-rubbing (adopting odour) is usually combined with scent-marking (depositing odour) using the secretions of specialised glands or the animal's own faeces and urine, and also with putting these substances onto their own bodies (self-anointing). Possible explanations for scent-rubbing include comfort behaviour, scent camouflage, familiarising the subject with novel odours, social attractiveness and intrasexual competition (Gosling and McKay, 1990). Animals may assess the competitors by comparing their odour with that of scent marks in the surrounding area to determine territory ownership. While the odour of the mud may not be restricted to the area of the resource holder, ungulates may add marking substances to the mud, and pigs, with their keen sense of smell, may easily be able to use such cues in status advertisement.

Among farmed species, therefore, wallowing is not only seen in pigs, but also for instance in farmed red deer and water buffalo. In New Zealand farmed red deer frequently create wallows in wet areas connected to waterways causing substantial contamination via excretal returns and erosion. Simply fencing off wallows is not a satisfactory intervention as deer will make new wallows nearby (McDowell, 2009). Water buffalos are raised on Dutch farms (and elsewhere) to produce milk for mozzarella cheese (Poelarends and Leenstra, 2009). These animals are adapted to hot and humid climates and muddy terrain. They may wallow for up to 5 h a day, immersed in water or mud and chewing with half closed eyes, and they prefer to cool off in a wallow rather than to seek shade (Marai and Haeeb, 2010). On Dutch farms drinking cups are covered with bars to prevent the buffalos from ‘playing’ with water, and the animals are only allowed to go outdoors when it has been dry of a while to prevent pasture damage from rolling in mud (Berkeljon, 2008).

Taken together this section shows that wallowing is a fairly common (rather than species-specific), functional and motivated behaviour in various species related to pigs. This indicates that the need to wallow may be anchored more deeply in the pig's evolutionary history than has commonly been realised.

3. Why do pigs wallow?

When biologists ask why-question about behaviour they refer to Tinbergen's four questions about ontogeny (How does the behaviour develop in the animal's life history?), phylogeny (What is the evolutionary history of the behaviour?), function (What is its survival value?) and causation (What triggers the behaviour?) (Tinbergen, 1963). This section focuses on the last question within a conceptual framework for welfare assessment that explicitly recognises that animals experience positive and negative emotions as products of proximate causes (impinging through the ‘immediate’ environmental conditions), ontogeny (life history) and ultimate functions (evolutionary history) (Anonymous, 2001, Bracke and Hopster, 2006, Bracke, 2008). The question then is: why are pigs motivated to wallow, i.e. which cognitive-emotional control systems (welfare needs) are involved in wallowing behaviour?

Prevalent hypotheses for wallowing in pigs include thermoregulation, ectoparasite removal and protection from sunburn in domesticated pigs (e.g. Gegner, 2001, Mayda, 2004), and disinfection of wounds (rather than sunburn protection) in wild boar (Fernández-Llario, 2005). However, as suggested in the previous section, a longer list may be formulated containing the following partly overlapping items:

•

Thermoregulatory behaviour (cooling, e.g. Vestergaard and Bjerg, 1996);

•

Skin care/grooming.

∘

Sunburn protection (Gegner, 2001);

∘

Shedding of hair, removing skin flakes (McMillan et al., 2000);

∘

Ectoparasite reduction (lice, mange, ticks; Fernández-Llario, 2005);

∘

Biting insects are prevented from biting (Tulloch, 1988, Nalin, 1996).

•

Health

∘

Behavioural element of sickness behaviour in case of general illness (Sambraus, 1981);

∘

Disinfection of skin wounds (Fernández-Llario, 2005).

•

Sexual behaviour

∘

Display/intermale competition (Fernández-Llario, 2005);

∘

Scent-rubbing/scent-marking/status advertisement (Gosling and McKay, 1990, Miura, 1985);

The options ‘anti-predator behaviour’, ‘social behaviour’, ‘resting’ and ‘play’ will not be discussed in more detail in this paper. The last hypothesis (pleasure, wallowing as an expression of positive welfare), will be discussed in more detail in the discussion of this paper because it is both speculative (i.e. disputable) and potentially important for (the assessment of) animal welfare.

3.1. Thermoregulation

Like other homeothermic animals pigs need to maintain their body temperature within certain limits. The thermoneutral zone is defined as that limited range of ambient temperatures over which the physiological functions are maintained at a minimum metabolic rate, and hence with a minimum of energy utilisation (Mount, 1968). As the pig increases in size, its critical temperature above which overheating may occur decreases. Thermoneutral temperatures are likely to be around 34 °C for newborns (which have little subcutaneous fat), 25–30 °C for 4–6 kg piglets, 25 °C for piglets aged 8–14 weeks, and 20 °C for growers (SVC, 1997).

There is general consensus that the prime function of wallowing is to prevent hyperthermia in pigs. For several reasons pigs are prone to overheating. Their sweat glands are hardly responsive to elevated temperatures (Ingram, 1965, Ingram, 1967); subcutaneous fat may result in a relatively high insulation value (Zervanos and Hadley, 1973); and their barrel-shaped body reduces body surface to body mass ratio and this reduces heat exchange. In addition, compared to wild boar, domesticated pigs have shorter snouts and this reduces their abilities to pant (SVC, 1997, Van Putten, 2000). Furthermore, while domesticated pigs have sparce hair cover and larger ears, their ears are not very mobile and vascularised as in the case of elephants, and their circulatory system has a limited capacity (Hörning et al., 1999). Finally, pigs may be producing considerable amounts of heat, e.g. due to muscular activity (feeding, fighting, play) and very high production levels (growing up to about 1 kg per pig per day; producing up to 30 piglets per sow per year).

In response to overheating pigs may show increased respiration (panting), elevated body temperature, reduced activity, reduced feed consumption, delayed return to oestrus, modified lying behaviour (reduced body contact with other pigs, lateral lying and shade seeking, Fraser, 1970b), increased drinking (EFSA, 2007a) and skin wetting or wallowing (SVC, 1997). Pigs have a marked inclination to wallow in mud or water on exposure to heat (Ingram, 1965) and without access to a proper mud pool, they will seek other wet surfaces and (try to) lie in their own faeces or urine (EFSA, 2007a). Wallowing increases at elevated ambient temperatures, but also at higher air humidity levels. Pigs are not very well adapted to a dry atmosphere as this may be a cause of irritability (SVC, 1997). With increasing relative humidity, pigs depend more on heat loss from the skin (SVC, 1997) and will show wallowing behaviour at lower ambient temperatures (Huynh et al., 2006). Wetting of the skin through wallowing indicates the pig's adaptation to a humid environment (SVC, 1997).

Documented cases of increased wallowing potentially related to heat production include body weight/production levels (significantly more wallowing in older fattening pigs (Vestergaard and Bjerg, 1996) and in prefarrowing sows (Buckner et al., 1998)), sexual activity in males (Fernández-Llario, 2005), sows being in heat (Sambraus, 1981), forced movement (Vestergaard and Bjerg, 1996), mixed groups of pigs (Vestergaard and Bjerg, 1996), predator avoidance (e.g. when being hunted, Graves, 1984), following play/exploration (Horrell et al., 2001), around foraging activity and feed intake (Sambraus, 1981, Dellmeier and Friend, 1991) and when suffering from illness (Sambraus, 1981). Feed intake, for example, has been shown to elevate the body temperature in pigs (Ingram and Mount, 1973) and this may account for increased wallowing after feed intake (Sambraus, 1981).

Pigs may lose heat from the skin through radiation, convection and conduction (Bruce and Clark, 1979) and by evaporation of water from wallowing. Conductive heat loss can be increased by direct contact with cool surfaces, e.g. moist soil (Van Putten, 2000). A recent model of heat balance in pigs indicated that skin wetting is the most effective way of alleviating heat stress in pigs (Fialho et al., 2004). Johnson et al. (2008) showed that shaded and unshaded wallows may have inner temperatures that are substantially (as much as 18 °C) lower than the ambient temperature. Cooling by conduction may be particularly effective through contact of the wallow with the metabolically-active udder surface of the pig (McGlone, 1999). Wallowing may decrease the pig's body temperature by 2 °C (Hörning et al., 1999).

Wallowing in mud leaves a coat of mud on the pig's lateral and ventral surfaces and limbs. This superficial layer of caked mud assists in relieving hyperthermia through evaporation, acting as a kind of ‘wet suit’ (helping to keep cool in a warm environment). Water in mud on the skin of a pig took 2 h to evaporate (compared to 15 min when water was used), and the evaporation rate was 700–800 g/h/m2 (Ingram, 1965). Hence, mud is more effective than clean water in temperature control because mud allows the evaporation process to continue for a longer time (Gegner, 2001, Dellmeier and Friend, 1991). Also compared with an evaporation rate of 200 g/h/m2 which cattle evaporate by sweating, pigs are more effective at losing heat during warm weather by wallowing than they would be by sweating and, on top of that, they conserve body fluids and salts (McGlone, 1999). These factors permit pigs such as breeding sows to graze and forage actively in an environment which otherwise could not be utilised by the pigs (Fraser, 1970b).

In short, wallowing is a clearly effective means of thermoregulation in pigs and this function may partly overlap with several ‘other’ possible explanations, because all behavioural and metabolic activity may affect heat production. However, it is unlikely that all wallowing (especially wallowing at lower temperatures) can be explained by thermoregulation alone.

3.2. Skin care

Pigs need to keep the body surface free of harmful substances and organisms. However, since pigs cannot reach large parts of their body surface themselves (Anonymous, 2001), they rub against scratch posts like trees or pen walls for skin care, and they may dislodge unwanted particles by wallowing in water or mud (EFSA, 2007a, EFSA, 2007b). This may happen both during wallowing itself and after wallowing when (dried) mud is removed from the body by scratching and rubbing. A layer of mud may trap debris and parasites that can be removed together with the mud. This mechanism was reported to effectively protect water buffaloes from ticks (Camoens, 1976). A layer of mud may also protect the skin after wallowing, e.g. from biting insects such as flies and mosquitoes (Tulloch, 1988, Nalin, 1996) and from sunburn (Gegner, 2001).

Domesticated pigs have less hair and a light-coloured skin compared to wild boar and this increases the risk of sunburn (Hörning et al., 1999), which usually first affects the skin behind ears and udders (Gegner, 2001). Wallowing results in a coating of mud which remains adherent and becomes dried out to form, over much of the pig's body, a protective insulation against the sun's rays (Fraser, 1970b).

Generally, wild boars maintain a high rate of parasitisation by ticks, mainly in warm climate areas, and some tick species have a strong affinity to wild boars. Ticks are found mainly behind the ears and in the genital and inguinal zones, which areas have a relatively thin skin and are difficult for the animals to access. In summer a peak is reached in the numbers of parasites on pigs. Mud wallowing can help them to diminish the number of ectoparasites (Fernández-Llario, 2005). However, observations on wild boar in the autumn–winter period in the Camargue showed that at that time parasite numbers were low and not correlated with signs of wallowing (Fernández-Llario, 2005). Though very limited in scope, this study, therefore, did not confirm the hypothesis that wallowing reduces ectoparasite numbers.

Van Putten (1978) suggested that wallowing must play a role in skin and hair care because they also wallow on cooler days. To confirm this suggestion, however, specific thermoregulatory reasons for wallowing must be ruled out, such as incidentally elevated heat production due to muscular or metabolic activity, as well as the possibility that pigs wallow simply because they like it. While the behavioural elements of wallowing per se and comfort behaviours (shaking to remove wet mud; scratching and rubbing various body parts after wallowing) are clearly closely related, supporting evidence for the suggestion that pigs wallow for skin maintenance is limited.

3.3. Health

Sambraus (1981) observed more wallowing in sows with general illness. This author also noted that wallowing had been generally regarded as beneficial for pig health in older textbooks on pig husbandry. Diseased pigs may wallow for thermoregulation (cooling in case of fever) and perhaps for body-care, resting and social comfort related reasons (alleviation of discomfort and pain).

Wallowing in wild boar has been ascribed a function in disinfection of wounds in the skin of males resulting from fights for access to females (Fernández-Llario, 2005). Male skin has sebaceous glands, which produce a bactericidal substance that acts on the frequent skin infections, mainly Staphylococcus sp. The bactericidal properties of mud could also help male wild boar to combat infection (Fernández-Llario, 2005). Observations on wild boar in the Camargue, however, did not confirm a relationship between skin wounds and wallowing (but the incidence of wounds was low; Fernández-Llario, 2005). Hence, the hypothesis of wallowing for health reasons remains circumstantial and may be related to thermoregulation.

3.4. Sexual behaviour

Fernández-Llario (2005) found more frequent signs of wallowing in wild boar males compared to females. The males that had wallowed were bigger and older than the non-wallowed males. Over the study period (October to February) most wallowing was observed during autumn (91% of all cases). While fighting wounds were not apparent, the females had active reproductive organs indicating that the rut period had coincided with increased wallowing in the adult males. Even though this may ‘just’ be a thermoregulatory response (cooling down from fighting), it may also tentatively be related to sexual behaviour in other ways, e.g. through scent marking.

The scent of male ungulates such as rams and pigs may indicate dominance, it may be attractive to females in oestrous and it may synchronize their oestrous period (Polikarpova, 1945). Synchronisation of oestrus increases the likelihood of females being bred by the best males, and this may be the original function of scent-urination (Coblentz, 1976). Wallowing in pigs may also play a (more general) role in status advertisement (Gosling and McKay, 1990), signalling group membership and identity with (smell of the soil in) the home range. Scent-marking is well known in carnivores and it may be another behaviour in which pigs resemble carnivores (e.g. contrary to most other artiodactyls pigs have large litters, perform nest-building and bed-making, have relatively undeveloped young and pigs tend to fight over food, much like dogs do, Clutton-Brock, 1999). Pigs also have a keen sense of smell and spend considerable amounts of time exploring their home range while rooting in soil (SVC, 1997). In addition, Kikuta and Stone (1986) reported that feral pigs displayed wallowing and rubbing responses to supplemented food items such as molasses and liver, tentatively suggesting that scent rubbing (whether as scent marking or as scent masking/camouflage) may be part of the pig's motivation to wallow.

4. Discussion

4.1. Description of wallowing in pigs and related species

The inventory of descriptions of wallowing in pigs indicated that it is an elaborate behaviour involving various behavioural elements both while in the wallow and in conjunction to wallowing such as rooting, feeding, elimination (urination and defecation) and various comfort behaviours. Most descriptions found in the literature, however, were qualitative, implying a general need for more quantitative studies. The review also indicated that pigs may wallow for considerable periods of time (Sambraus, 1981, McGlone, 1999) and that it has a profound basis in the pig's evolutionary history. This suggests that wallowing may be important for pigs. In another paper (Bracke, in press) the importance of wallowing for pig welfare is assessed more systematically using a semantic modelling approach.

The tentative description of wallowing in an evolutionary context indicated that the pig may be motivated to wallow both in a general sense (as mammals have evolved from fish with a foetal development in a pool-like environment, the uterus) and in a more specific sense (e.g. being genetically related to particularly water-loving animals such as hippo's and whales). Wallowing, defined widely as covering the body surface with a mud-like substance, is common in mammals such as cervids, carnivores and primates (Gosling and McKay, 1990). Pigs, however, prefer to wallow more specifically in mud, mainly for thermoregulatory reasons (cooling), and in this respect it resembles mud wallowing seen in other large animals (Giant tortoises, crocodiles, elephant seals) and in particular in the large, hairless megaherbivores such as elephants, rhino's, bison and water buffalos. Pigs probably also descended from a large ancestor, by contrast to, for example, the horse (an odd-toed ungulate) whose ancestor, the eohippus, was far smaller than his descendant (Bennett, 1970). Although no firm conclusions can be drawn from this first and sketchy outline of wallowing in an evolutionary context, the approach proves fruitful for enhancing our understanding of its potential welfare relevance, even in a modern domesticated species as the pig. Perhaps this approach may also be useful to examine other natural behaviours.

4.2. Wallowing motivations

An extended list was drawn up with possible motivational bases for wallowing in pigs. Besides thermoregulation, sun-burn protection and ecto-parasite removal, pigs may wallow for health ‘reasons’, other skin care reasons, to modify the body smell, resting comfort, play and simply for pleasure. Based on the available information it was not possible, unfortunately, to conclusively identify other reasons for wallowing besides thermoregulation. However, since relatively good arguments can be given for ‘secondary’ motivations (e.g. body care, sexual behaviour, scent-marking), these other possible explanations should not be ignored or dismissed without further study. In particular, we should not too easily think that we sufficiently understand this complex behaviour to (conveniently) conclude that we can ‘remedy’ the need for wallowing in pigs with simple cooling installations and anti-parasite treatments. This could involve a repetition of the mistake made by animal scientists and farmers who previously suggested that farm animals do not need to forage (search for food) when they are provided with nutritious food. That view has lead to serious welfare problems such as feather pecking and tail biting. We need, therefore, a better understanding of the pig's motivations for wallowing and its substitutability by other means and behaviours (e.g. other routes of thermoregulation, dry rubbing, dry deformable bedding and other ‘wallowing’ substrates). A related, perhaps even more important question to address is whether pigs may wallow because they like it, i.e. that wallowing contributes positively to welfare (whether or not this may be called an ‘ethological need’).

4.3. Wallowing in relation to positive animal welfare

There is increasing attention to positive welfare, both in research (e.g. Boissy et al., 2007) and society (FAWC, 2009). It is not clear, however, whether wallowing in pigs would contribute positively to welfare. De Greef et al. (2003), for example, suggested that wallowing indicates that something is wrong in the environment.

In the common conceptual framework for welfare assessment (Anonymous, 2001, Bracke and Hopster, 2006, Bracke, 2008), animals have cognitive-emotional control systems (‘welfare needs’) in which emotions result from (reductions of) discrepancies between the way the animal perceives its (immediate) environment and the way it wants its environment to be as ‘shaped’ by its evolutionary history and its life history (ontogeny; learning). In this framework, most welfare needs can give rise to both negative and positive emotions, e.g. when animals are deprived of food presumably negative emotions arise (hunger) and when they receive food presumably positive emotions (satiation) arise. Some needs, however, are more likely to be associated with negative welfare, e.g. pain, illness and respiration (since the provision of oxygen for breathing is not generally perceived as pleasurable, except perhaps in contrast situations). Other needs, often referred to as ‘natural behaviours’ (Spinka, 2006, Bracke and Hopster, 2006), are associated with positive affect, e.g. play, foraging and dustbathing in poultry. Some of these are typically associated with activity (‘ethological needs’), but others may concern a more passive state (as would partly be the case for dustbathing and wallowing).

Welfare needs are especially important when their deprivation leads to (symptoms of) negative welfare, when their motivation builds up over time (i.e. when animals are not able to habituate to a state of deprivation) and when they are (mainly) internally motivated (such that there are no alternative routes in which humans can take control) (see Jensen and Toates, 1993). None of these requirements, however, are necessary in order to contribute positively to welfare (see Jensen and Toates, 1993, Fraser and Duncan, 1998, Lawrence, 1987). For positive welfare only a rewarding or (positively) reinforcing effect is required. This concerns situations and behaviours that are attractive to animals (as indicated by their behaviour under ‘ad libitum’ conditions) and that promote biological functioning (Bracke and Hopster, 2006).

According to views prevalent in society and among some welfare scientists wallowing may contribute positively to welfare. Lassen et al. (2006) published a paper called ‘Happy pigs are dirty’, based on interviews with members of the public about pig production. They reported that mud bathing and rummaging about in the dirt were seen as basic and necessary activities for pigs. In the lay perspective, living a natural life is important for animal welfare. In line with this view, Dellmeier and Friend (1991) noted that observers particularly enjoy watching and photographing wallowing behaviour in pigs. According to Van Putten (2000) wallowing has an obviously relaxing effect on the animals. These references, however, do not make clear why and how wallowing would be positive for welfare.

Firstly, wallowing may contribute to positive welfare when it provides relief from negative experiences such as heat stress and ectoparasites. This would be a most basic and perhaps not very ‘positive’ (but nevertheless potentially important) contribution to the welfare debate.

Secondly, a comparison with related behaviours in humans such as swimming and bathing suggests that wallowing in pigs could involve ‘pleasure’. When these human behaviours are described in biological terms, it may be noted, for example, that people seem to show rebound behaviour, that they help to clean the body and that there is a relation with ambient temperature (e.g. more swimming occurs during warm days) (e.g. Cohen-Mansfield and Parpura-Gill, 2007, Tucker and Gilliland, 2007). This suggests that these behaviours appear to be regulated in humans by (at least partially) similar control systems as is wallowing in pigs, namely thermoregulation and body care. However, most people simply experience these activities as pleasurable. Further observations support this. For example, since people also visit heated swimming pools in winter, thermoregulation cannot be the only reason for swimming. Similar behaviour in pigs (i.e. visiting a heated mud pool during cold weather), could, therefore, support the hypothesis that pigs may wallow for pleasure. In any case, several reports already indicate that pigs may wallow at temperatures well below the upper end of their thermal comfort zone (Graves, 1984, Fernández-Llario, 2005; McGlone, personal communication).

Thirdly, the behaviour may be regulated through an internal control mechanism, which involves a pleasurable sensation arising from the performance of the behaviour itself (at certain intervals and provided certain requirements are met, e.g. with respect to temperature). This is especially the case in body care behaviour, as has been described e.g. for dustbathing in poultry (Hogan and Van Boxel, 1993, Widowski and Duncan, 2000) and for grooming and wallowing behaviour in American Bison (Mooring and Samuel, 1998). The biological reason for this may be that it is difficult or impossible for an animal to monitor skin condition (e.g. stale feather lipids; tick larvae). Similarly, wallowing in pigs may be fairly difficult to get under full external control (i.e. dependent on external cues). If so, it would make sense to reward the behaviour per se, at least to some extent, such that over time the animal is able to build up experiences generating the required (long-term) feedback that may be used to fine-tune the behaviour. This would especially apply in as far as wallowing plays a role in skin care, e.g. to reduce parasites and maintain skin and hair condition.

In addition, proper wallowing involves a range of functional behavioural elements that may contribute to a positive welfare experience, such as rooting, resting and comfort (scratching) behaviours. In proper wallowing these elements are part of a functional sequence that may also contribute positively to welfare (Bracke, 2008), over and above the fact that it provides things to do (contra boredom, McFarland, 1989, Wemelsfelder, 1993). This suggestion seems to be supported by Spoolder et al. who reported that farmers may reduce pasture damage by providing wallows to pigs (Spoolder and Vermeer, 2002, Van der Mheen and Spoolder, 2005). Since pigs may enjoy rooting, wallowing that is effective in attracting pigs away from such a preferred activity, may be (even more) pleasurable.

A final suggestion that wallowing may be pleasurable relates to its evolutionary history. Wallowing is observed in many related species and for many different reasons, suggesting that the animals may have ‘wanted’ to perform the behaviour and subsequently have ‘found’ biological control systems to accommodate their more basic ‘ethological need’, i.e. the pleasure/joy/reward from performing the behaviour and its experience itself. This is a tentative suggestion that seems to oppose the prevalent view that animal feelings are the products of biological functioning, not vice versa (e.g. Broom, 1998, Dawkins, 1998). Tentative as it may be and in addition to the points presented earlier (e.g. that pigs descend from a large ancestor and are related to water-‘loving’ mammals), pigs and the relatively hairless megaherbivores do not only share a propensity for (additional) cooling through wallowing in mud, they also share a relative high position in the food chain: they have relatively few predators. Pigs are intelligent (e.g. in avoiding hunters) and groups of adult pigs and males with their large canine teeth are very well capable of defending themselves (Clutton-Brock, 1999). Water sources can be dangerous places for ungulates, especially during periods of drought when the need for drinking and cooling is often increased. This may explain why most ungulates have evolved functional sweat glands. Pigs, however, did not, that is they do have sweat glands and they are capable of discharging (as indicated by a variable response when injected with adrenaline, Ingram, 1967), nevertheless pigs do not sweat in response to elevated ambient temperatures. Wallowing may, thus, have been a ‘luxury’ behaviour that they could afford enjoying. This would suggest that the prevalent view that pigs must rely on wetting the skin because they do not have functional sweat glands (SVC, 1997) may also be reversed: perhaps pigs do not have functional sweat glands because they (preferred to) wallow.

In addition, wallowing has biological costs. These costs include effort to make wallows (which requires an investment of available metabolic energy) and costs associated with predation and reduced hygiene (see also Bracke, in press). When pigs are willing to incur these costs, they should be offset by benefits, otherwise the behaviour would not have been preserved in the course of evolution. If so, psychological mechanisms involving pleasure/liking can be expected to outweigh the pigs’ tendencies to avoid these costs (which again, would be very difficult to regulate using only external cues). If so and when pigs are kept under domestic conditions in which the original biological costs have been mitigated (by providing protection from predation, providing hygienic conditions, health care, sufficient food, a thermocontrolled environment, etc.), the overall outcome of providing pigs with proper wallows may well indicate pig happiness.

Controlled observations are needed to verify the suggestion that wallowing may be pleasurable, e.g. by showing ‘contrafreeload-wallowing’, i.e. a motivation to wallow under conditions where other explanations (in terms of more basic needs and utilities) such as thermoregulation, body care and illness have been ruled out. In addition to the need for further research, this paper also clearly indicates the relevance of reviewing an almost ‘forgotten’ natural behaviour like wallowing in pigs in its evolutionary and motivational context. A more formalised assessment of the welfare importance of wallowing and its ethical implications are discussed in another paper (Bracke, in press).
