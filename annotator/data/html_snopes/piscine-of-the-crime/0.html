Chlorine Dosage For Kiddie Pool (6/10/99)

Q:
I have read the archives and understand that maintaining a small children's pool will require water changes. What I couldn't find an answer to is: What is the appropriate quantity of chemical to put in the pool? I have a 300 gallon children's pool in which I would like to keep the water for at least 3 weeks. I have a cleaning system and cover. The manufacturer recommends household bleach, but I can't seem to get the quantity correct. Local pool supply merchants have been most unhelpful, and local stores sell supplies for pools in 10,000 gallons per tablet dosages.... Do I just need to guess?

K.H. - Houston, TX

A:
If you want a simple quantity to add, I can't help you. You have to add enough chlorine to maintain a level of 1-3 PPM. Unfortunately, in a small kiddy pool that is not as easy as it seems. Bathers deplete chlorine, urine really depletes chlorine big time, waste products and debris deplete chlorine, sunlight depletes chlorine, etc.

You have to do a little trial and error. A small floater with a chlorine tablet might be a good idea. The product is slow dissolving and you could remove the floater, if the level rises too high.

There is not substitute for testing, if you want to optimize the water -- even in a kiddy pool.

Good luck.

Sincerely,
Alan

Solar Cover's Affect On Water Chemistry (11/4/98)

Q:
How does a pool cover (meant to trap infrared heat) affect the chemistry of the pool water? I've asked this question to a number of different "experts" and received rather conflicting answers. Here are some examples:

- The pool cover heats the water, which causes the chlorine to bubble out of the water faster.

- The pool cover prevents atmospheric oxygen from "reacting" with the pool water, thus making the pool acidic regardless of whether or not the pool has chemicals in it.

- The pool cover prevents the pool water from evaporating, which keeps the chemicals in the water longer. The pool should remain covered whenever you are not using it.

What is the CORRECT answer to the question?

S.N. - San Jose, CA

A:
I'm not sure that I agree with all of the answers.

1. The cover does cause the water temperature to rise. The reason that the chlorine levels can drop more quickly is that the higher temperature makes the chlorine more reactive with algae and organic wastes.

2. The cover can reduce the absorption of atmospheric gases. Carbon dioxide absorption will cause a slight lowering of the pH. I don't believe that oxygen will affect the pH to any significant extent. Because the water temperature is higher, pools on 3" trichlor tablets will be dissolving them at a greater rate and this in turn will help lower the pH.

3. Evaporating water does not remove the chemicals from the water -- they remain behind.

I hope that I have been helpful.

Sincerely,
Alan

Irritated Eyes (7/15/98)

Q:
I hear people when they come out of a pool say their eyes are bothering them because there is too much chlorine in the pool. I think their eyes are bothering them because the PH level is off, or not enough chlorine, or maybe their are other chemicals in the water. What is the truth? Can over-chlorinating be a problem where you shouldn't swim?

T.D. - Raynham, MA

A:
High or low pH reading could lead to irritation. The pH of the eye is approx. 7.6.

Too high a chlorine level can preclude swimming. That's why there are recommended ranges.

The irritation you are referring to is most likely caused by the presence of high levels of combined chlorine (chloramines). Chloramines are irritating, odorous, ineffective forms of combined chlorine. The chlorine odor associated with pool water is most likely chloramine and is the reason that pool water is periodically shocked.

I hope that I have been helpful.

Sincerely,
Alan

The Color Of A Swimming Pool (7/9/98)

Q:
I have a somewhat unusual question for you, a question about the color of pool water. Are pools the color they usually are (i.e. blue or aqua) because the chlorine is that color, or are they blue/aqua because their walls and bottoms are painted this color? For example, would it be possible to have, say, a pink or purple pool, or would the chlorine give the water a blueish tint, affecting the look of the pool?

A:
The color of a swimming pool is largely the result of the color of the walls and bottom.

Most pools are in the aqua-blue range, because that reinforces the natural faint blue color of pure water. The addition of chemicals can slightly alter the color. Pools are built with all types of colored surfaces, from white to black.

While it is possible to add colorants to the water, it is not necessarily effective or permanent.

A pink or purple wall would certainly impart a pink-purple caste to the water.

I hope that I have been of assistance.

Sincerely,
Alan

Frogs In The Pool (6/23/98)

Q:
I have been out of my house since Nov. Now I have frogs living in my pool (hundreds of em) and it is dark green. I dropped the water level down about 1 foot where the jets are showing above the water line. What do you recommend next for me to do? My pool is 15000 gal. inground deep end is 8 ft. low end is 3 ft. It has a 4 year old surface on it.

J.B. - Cocoa, FL

A:
Sounds like you are allowed your pool to become a pond and a green one at that.

In Florida it is not uncommon to have frogs, snakes and other living creatures in there from time to time. It's one of the penalties of not having a screened in pool. I scoop them out with the net and give them the heave ho.

My wife happens to collects frogs - not the living types - so let's not hurt them!!!!

Get the water level to the proper height and start filtering continuously. Add shock at the rate of 1 pound/gallon per 5,000 gallons of water. Adjust pH, T/A, etc. Retest FREE CHLORINE level every few hours and keep adding shock until you get FREE CHLORINE level of at least 3 PPM that overnight. Thereafter maintain normally. Your filter may be prone to short cycles because of all the debris. If pressure rises too high, perform appropriate maintenance. To help clear up the water quicker, try adding Swim-Free Algalator or SUN Tru-Blue.

You are less likely to have a frog problem if the pool does not resemble a pond.

Good luck.

Sincerely,
Alan

Draining A Pool (6/8/98)

Q:
We bought our new home Aug. 97 and have had the water tested in by a pool store and by our pool man. Both state that the pool water must be completely drained about every five years. Since we know nothing about pools and we aren't sure whether or not this is correct and I have been unable to find anything on the net regarding this question, can you please advise.

K.R. - Agoura, CA

A:
There is no set answer to the question as to how long a pool can go without being emptied. It depends on the nature of the source water, types and quantities of chemicals used, total dissolved solids, pool construction, filter type, part of the country, etc. If the pool is winterized that effects the water quality over the long term.

I'm guessing that you have a gunite pool. Masonry pools are periodically emptied for acid washing. How often depends on many factors.

If the pool looks good and there are no water chemistry problems, I'm inclined to think that you should take it one year at a time. Ask some neighbors about their experiences.

Enjoy the season.

Sincerely,
Alan

Mosquitoes Around The Pool (5/6/98)

Q:
We had an inground pool installed in our backyard at the end of last summer. We live deep in the woods and never had a problem with mosquitoes until this spring. Since we opened the pool we have been plagued with mosquitoes using the solar cover as a breeding ground. Is there a product that we can put in the pool water, that will eliminate the mosquito problem, both now and throughout the summer months? Even when we are using the solar cover?

K.S. - Georgetown, DE

A:
Mosquitoes will do what mosquitoes do.

Standing water is the problem. I wouldn't recommend using an insecticide in the pool.

There is something you can try in dealing with the mosquitoes on the pool surface. They are able to walk on the surface because of the surface tension of the water. If you add SUN Algae Preventor as an algaecide, you will lower the surface tension and, hopefully, they will slip under and drown.

I hope the advice works. Good luck.

Sincerely, Alan

Urine In The Pool (4/28/98)

Q:
Is there any chemical that will turn a color when someone urinates in the pool??

Mitch

A:
There is no chemical that can function as an indicator for urine in a pool.

If a child has done it in the pool, there will be an immediate rise in the combined chlorine level and the pungent odor of chloramines. This will require a large amount of shock to destroy the combined chlorine and re-establish a free chlorine level.

Deposits In Automatic Chlorinator (4/3/98)

Q:
What is the best and easiest way to clean out the deposits which accumulate in the chlorinator. I have tried dilute muriatic acid, but it doesn't seem to work very well. Any suggestions would be greatly appreciated. Thank you.

C.L - Effingham, IL

A:
It doesn't surprise me that dilute muriatic acid didn't work. Chlorine tablets form a weakly acidic solution in the chlorinator and the acid solution that you tried was too similar.

It is possible that the deposits are caused by trace minerals present in the local water or by action of the chlorine over the long term.

Not knowing the specific details and rather than speculate, why not ask the pool dealer? If it is widespread in your area your local dealer will have knowledge of it.

As a precaution you should never clean the chlorinator with anything that will contact the chlorine. Remove all chlorine!!! Rinse all traces of chlorine and cleaner.

I hope that I have been of assistance.
