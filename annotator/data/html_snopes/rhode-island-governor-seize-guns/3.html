Hace aproximadamente dos meses, anuncié que pediríamos una revisión ...exhaustiva de las escuelas públicas de Providence que informaría un curso de acción a largo plazo para mejorar el distrito. Un equipo de la Universidad de Johns Hopkins pasó semanas liderando conversaciones con estudiantes, maestros, personal, padres y miembros de la comunidad. Publicaron sus hallazgos esta semana y su informe lleva a una conclusión clara: estas escuelas están en crisis. Este informe es impactante, molesto y difícil de leer. Detalla la disfunción generalizada que ha afectado a estudiantes, maestros y padres que se sienten frustrados, abrumados e ignorados. Sabíamos que había problemas, pero este informe es peor de lo que muchos de nosotros pensábamos. Estoy molesta por las generaciones de niños que han sufrido un sistema totalmente defectuoso, por los maestros que no se sienten apoyados y por los padres que se sienten indefensos. Los niños no pueden aprender y los maestros no pueden enseñar cuando no se sienten valorados o seguros. Pero a partir de ahora, mejorará. Tenemos mucho trabajo por delante, pero no podemos sentarnos y desanimarnos. Necesitamos canalizar nuestra ira en acción. Este informe nos dice lo que ha sido, y ahora depende de nosotros decidir qué será. El cambio no se producirá de la noche a la mañana, pero el proceso comenzó esta semana con una serie de conversaciones comunitarias dirigidas por nuestra nueva Comisionada de Educación, Angélica Infante-Green. Esas conversaciones son esenciales, y necesitamos a todos en la mesa. Me comprometo a hacer lo que sea necesario para garantizar que las escuelas públicas de Providence ofrezcan una educación de alta calidad para todos los estudiantes. Mehr anzeigen

Gina Raimondo
26. Februar 2018 ·

Over the last couple of weeks, I’ve been talking a lot about what happened in Florida with my kids, and particularly my daughter. She’s going to be a freshman in high school next year – not much younger than the kids we’ve been watching on TV.

Every time we talk about it, Ceci cuts me off and says, “Mom, what are you going to do? Why hasn’t anyone done anything?”

Ceci was in third grade when the Sandy Hook shooting took place – not much older than the kids who were killed that day. In the five years since, there have been 63 school shootings and more than 1,600 mass shootings across our country, including the Pulse Nightclub shooting in Orlando that killed 49 people, the First Baptist Church shooting in Sutherland Springs that killed 26 people and the shooting in Las Vegas last fall that killed 58 people.

Since I’ve been Governor, I’ve ordered the flags to half-staff nine times because of a mass shooting. Nine times.

So, I can understand where the question comes from: “Mom, what are you going to do?”

Ceci, Tommy and their friends have grown up hearing news stories and watching reports about mass shootings in schools, malls, movie theaters, churches and concerts. We had fire drills when we were kids. They have active shooter drills. And after each and every one of these shootings, they watch Congress do nothing.

So, when she says, “Mom, what are you going to do?”, it’s not really a question. It’s a directive. She’s saying to me: “If they won’t act, you need to.”

I’m proud that Rhode Island has some of the nation’s strongest gun laws. But our nation has some of the weakest. Last year, we took a step to make them a little stronger when we came together and passed a new law that takes guns out of the hands of abusers.

But we need to do more. We need to keep going. We can’t sit around another minute waiting for Congress to act.

So today, I’m taking decisive executive action to immediately establish a Red Flag policy. The Rhode Island Red Flag Executive Order does three things:

First, it directs Rhode Island State Police to assess all red flag reports and take whatever steps are legally available to remove guns from dangerous people. I appreciate all of our law enforcement partners who are here today, including Colonel Ann Assumpico and Chief Mello from Jamestown who have both been longtime supporters of common sense gun safety policies.

Second, this order directs the Rhode Island Department of Health, the Rhode Island Department of Education - RIDE and the Department of Public Safety and others to work together to develop and execute a comprehensive public education campaign to raise awareness of red flags.

And third, this order establishes a Gun Safety Working Group – which will be appointed in the coming weeks – to develop recommendations to implement a permanent Red Flag Law and support Rhode Island’s participation in the States for Gun Safety coalition we helped launch last week with New York, New Jersey, Connecticut and now Massachusetts.

I don’t take executive action lightly. I’m doing this today because we can’t rely on the federal government to take any action of substance.

I’m signing this executive order today so that we can put a red flag policy into place immediately. The action that we're signing today sets the stage for a complementary legislative effort.

I applaud our leaders in the legislature for taking action and urge the General Assembly to pass those bills, as introduced, quickly. Rhode Islanders need this to be law.

Let me also be clear that this cannot be the only reform we put in place this year. Rhode Island has some of the strongest gun laws in America, but it’s still legal to purchase a military style weapon; it’s still legal to purchase high capacity magazines;
it’s still legal for civilians to carry a concealed weapon into a public school or state government building.

Let’s work together to fix that this year.

For two weeks, I’ve said a prayer for the kids and parents in Parkland before going to bed. And I’ve said a prayer for Ceci and Tommy and their friends. At moments like this we need prayers. … But we also need action.
